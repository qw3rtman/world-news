US and Venezuela agree to reestablish diplomatic relations in major shift after Maduro’s ouster, dated 2026-03-05, reports that the United States and Venezuela agreed on Thursday to restore diplomatic relations after they were cut off in 2019 during Donald Trump’s first administration, when Nicolás Maduro shut embassies after Trump backed opposition lawmaker Juan Guaidó, then self-declared interim president; the State Department said talks were “focused on helping the Venezuelan people move forward through a phased process that creates the conditions for a peaceful transition to a democratically elected government.” The shift followed a U.S. military operation that deposed former President Nicolás Maduro in January, after which Trump officials visited the oil-rich South American nation and pressed Maduro loyalists in power to accept Washington’s phased plan. The announcement came at the end of a two-day visit by U.S. Secretary of the Interior Doug Burgum, whose trip centered on Venezuela’s mining sector, and followed a February visit by Energy Secretary Chris Wright focused on oil potential and foreign investment. Acting President Delcy Rodríguez, Maduro’s former vice president, said on state television that the steps “will strengthen relations between our two countries,” and later said reestablishing ties “will contribute to strengthening understanding and opening opportunities for a positive and mutually beneficial relationship,” adding, “These relations ought to result in the social and economic happiness of the Venezuelan people.” The article also notes the Trump administration’s pressure for an open oil sector, Rodríguez’s amnesty law that has freed politicians, activists, lawyers and others after acknowledging hundreds imprisoned for political motives, Trump’s surprise decision to work with Rodríguez rather than the opposition, and opposition leader María Corina Machado, winner of the 2025 Nobel Peace Prize, saying Sunday she will return to Venezuela in coming weeks and elections will be held there. ### Iranian clerics convene to choose Ayatollah successor : NPR, dated 2026-03-05, says Iran’s clerics are meeting after Ayatollah Ali Khamenei was killed in Israeli airstrikes to choose a successor, with Khamenei’s 56-year-old son Mojtaba considered the front-runner; the panel is the council of experts, 88 clerics charged with selecting the next supreme leader, and it is meeting virtually because of the war.
