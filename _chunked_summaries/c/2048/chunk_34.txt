Nepal awaited the first results of a pivotal parliamentary election on Friday, with vote counting beginning after a closely watched poll expected to shape the country’s political direction following last year’s deadly protests. Election Commission spokesman Prakash Nyupane said counting had started peacefully in most parts of the country, with some early results expected later on Friday and final nationwide tallies possibly taking several days; if no single party won a clear majority, negotiations to form a government could also be lengthy. Interim Prime Minister Sushila Karki praised the orderly conduct of the vote, calling it crucial for “determining our future”. The election would decide who replaced the interim administration that had governed since the September 2025 uprising, during which at least 77 people were killed and parliament along with dozens of government buildings were set on fire. The unrest was driven largely by youth-led protests, initially sparked by a brief ban on social media but quickly fuelled by wider frustration over corruption and economic hardship. Among the leading contenders were KP Sharma Oli, the 74-year-old Marxist former prime minister seeking a return to power, Balendra Shah, a 35-year-old rapper-turned-politician popular with younger voters, and Gagan Thapa, the new leader of the long-established Nepali Congress party. Later live updates said Balen Shah, described as a former rapper and engineer, was set to lead the Himalayan nation after a decisive electoral sweep, with the “Gen-Z unrest” of last September having toppled the KP Sharma Oli government after at least 77 people were killed when police opened fire on demonstrators. The Nepal election commission estimated around 60% of the country’s 19 million registered voters turned out in Thursday’s election, the first parliamentary elections since the Gen Z-led protests collapsed the KP Sharma Oli-led coalition regime last year; another update said preliminary turnout had crossed 60% by 5pm, and later reports put turnout at 59 per cent.
