US and Venezuela agree to reestablish diplomatic relations in major shift after Maduro’s ouster | 2026-03-05: The State Department said on Thursday that the United States and Venezuela agreed to reestablish diplomatic relations, a major shift after a historically adversarial breakup, following the Trump administration’s U.S. military operation that deposed former President Nicolás Maduro in January. The deal came after rounds of visits by Trump administration officials to Caracas, including a two-day visit by U.S. Secretary of the Interior Doug Burgum focused largely on Venezuela’s mining sector, following a February visit by Energy Secretary Chris Wright on Venezuela’s oil potential. Relations had been cut off in 2019, when Maduro closed embassies after Donald Trump publicly backed opposition lawmaker Juan Guaidó, who claimed the interim presidency in January that year, prompting U.S. diplomatic staff to move to Colombia. The State Department said talks were “focused on helping the Venezuelan people move forward through a phased process that creates the conditions for a peaceful transition to a democratically elected government.” Acting President Delcy Rodríguez, formerly Maduro’s vice president, said the steps “will strengthen relations between our two countries,” and the government later said reestablishing ties “will contribute to strengthening understanding and opening opportunities for a positive and mutually beneficial relationship,” adding that such relations should lead to the “social and economic happiness of the Venezuelan people.” The Trump administration has pressed Maduro loyalists in power to accept its vision for the oil-rich country, including opening the oil sector to foreign companies; Rodríguez’s government also approved an amnesty law that has enabled releases of politicians, activists, lawyers and many others, implicitly acknowledging hundreds had been jailed for political motivations.
