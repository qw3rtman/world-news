Unexpected job losses, rise in unemployment rate fan US labor market doubts | Reuters, 2026-03-06: Washington, March 6, 2026, Reuters reported the U.S. economy unexpectedly lost jobs in February and the unemployment rate rose to 4.4%, deepening doubts about labor-market stability and complicating the Federal Reserve’s stance amid rising oil prices. The Labor Department’s Bureau of Labor Statistics said nonfarm payrolls fell by 92,000 after a downwardly revised 126,000 increase in January; Reuters economists had expected a gain of 59,000 after a previously reported 130,000 increase. The February decline was the sixth since January 2025 and the second largest, with some of the drop attributed to a strike by healthcare workers and a winter storm that hit construction and leisure and hospitality. The report said payroll losses were widespread, led by healthcare, which shed 28,000 jobs after a January gain of 77,000; physicians’ offices dropped 37,000, mostly because of a strike by 31,000 Kaiser Permanente healthcare workers and bad weather. Information lost 11,000, federal government 10,000, transportation and warehousing 11,000, construction 11,000, leisure and hospitality 27,000, manufacturing 12,000, and professional and business services also fell; social assistance and financial activities eked out gains. Average hourly earnings rose 0.4% in February after a similar January gain, and were up 3.8% year over year. The workweek averaged 34.3 hours, unchanged from January, while 228,000 people reported they could not report for work because of bad weather, only slightly above 218,000 in January. The unemployment rate increase to 4.4% reflected household employment dropping 185,000 and the participation rate falling to 62.0%, the lowest since December 2021; January’s participation rate was revised to 62.1% from 62.5% after updated Census-based population controls, which cut the civilian noninstitutional population by 231,000 and revised labor force and employment levels down by about 1.4 million each in January only. Economists said the three-month average payroll gain slowed to 6,000 from 50,000 in the prior three months, and comments quoted Fitch Ratings’ Olu Sonola as saying, “It’s bad news whichever way you look at it,” while J.P. Morgan Wealth Management’s Elyse Ausenbaugh said the report made it harder for the Fed to sell a labor-market stabilization narrative. The article said traders saw stocks lower, the dollar softer, and Treasury yields falling, while the Fed was still expected to hold its benchmark rate at 3.50%-3.75% on March 17-18 though June cut odds rose. ### Fed Governor Miran says job losses in February add to the case for more interest rate cuts, 2026-03-06: Federal Reserve Governor Stephen Miran said in a CNBC “Money Movers” interview that the weak February jobs report, including the 92,000 drop in nonfarm payrolls, strengthens the case for lower interest rates because he thinks the Fed should focus more on supporting the labor market than on inflation. Miran said, “I think that we don’t have an inflation problem,” and argued the current policy stance, at 3.5% to 3.75%, is modestly restrictive rather than neutral. He said he sees neutral about a full percentage point lower, while the December FOMC consensus put neutral around 3.1%, implying two more cuts. He also said stubborn inflation readings are partly a measurement issue at Commerce and Labor, citing portfolio management fees that rise when stock markets rise, and said the recent surge in oil prices tied to the Iran war is less of a concern because oil shocks are typically one-off and core inflation matters more. Miran has dissented at each FOMC meeting he attended since September, favoring larger half-point cuts when the committee approved quarter-point reductions, and in January wanted a quarter-point cut when the committee held rates steady. He said of another dissent, “I hope not, but that would be up to my colleagues.
