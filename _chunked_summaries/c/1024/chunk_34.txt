The article framed the contest as a pivotal three-way fight between interim Prime Minister Sushila Karki’s caretaker setup, CPN-UML chairman and former prime minister KP Sharma Oli, and Rastriya Swatantra Party leader Balendra “Balen” Shah, the 35-year-old former Kathmandu mayor and rapper-turned-politician who emerged as the central figure of the “Gen-Z unrest.” It said the unrest began with a brief social media ban and widened into protests over corruption and economic hardship, with parliament and dozens of government buildings burned. As results trickled in on Mar 6 and Mar 7, the RSP surged from early leads in 23 seats to 41, 52, 70, 77, 90, 95, 96, 105 and even “105 constituencies” plus one win, with supporters celebrating at counting centers, dancing and ringing bells, and one live update saying the party was “on the brink of landslide victory” and expected “one hundred and eighty-six seats in total,” an apparent claim from RSP Vice President Dol Prasad Aryal. It reported the Nepali Congress conceding defeat through its vice president, while still winning some seats, and showed the RSP sweeping Kathmandu and elsewhere: three Kathmandu seats, including Kathmandu-1, Kathmandu-6 and Kathmandu-8, and leads in Kathmandu-7 and Kathmandu-10, plus Bhaktapur-1 and Bhaktapur-2 and five of Morang’s six constituencies. Specific candidate numbers included Balendra Shah leading KP Sharma Oli in Jhapa-5 by 13,654 to 3,011 in one update, then by 10,466 to 2,205, then by 5,254 to 1,248; Rabi Lamichhane leading in Chitwan-2 with 9,234; Pushpa Kamal Dahal winning Rukum East-1 with 10,240 and a margin of 6,778; Rajiv Khatri leading in Bhaktapur-2 with 7,002 and later 3,060 versus Mahesh Basnet’s 1,573; Ranju Darshana Neupane winning Kathmandu-1 with 15,455 and a margin of 9,091; Shishir Khanal winning Kathmandu-6 with 27,916 and a margin of 21,249; and Biraj Bhakta Shrestha winning Kathmandu-8 with 24,592 and a margin of 21,375. The article also said the Nepali Congress won seats in Manang-1 and Mustang-1, that the RSP led in 105 constituencies at one point while the Nepali Congress had one win and leads in 10, CPN-UML in 12, the Nepali Communist Party in 10, the Shram Sanskriti Party in four, the Rastriya Prajatantra Party and Janata Samajbadi Party–Nepal in one each, and two independents led in their areas.
