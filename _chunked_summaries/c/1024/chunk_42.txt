Oil markets and the Iran war dominated several March 9-10, 2026 reports. In “Oil prices: Analysts raise the alarm as crude soars over Iran war” (2026-03-09), CNBC said the war was heightening fears of prolonged production shut-ins from crude-rich Middle East countries as the strategically vital Strait of Hormuz was “effectively closed.” Neil Atkinson, former head of oil at the International Energy Agency, told CNBC on Monday, “‘The sky is the limit,’” and warned the world was entering a “potentially game-changing and unprecedented energy crisis.” Brent crude futures with May delivery traded 11.6% higher at $103.47 per barrel and U.S. West Texas Intermediate futures with April delivery were 12.2% higher at $101.97, after Brent had hit $119.5 and WTI $119.48 intraday. Iraq and Kuwait had already begun to shut in production, with the UAE and Saudi Arabia said vulnerable if the strait stayed closed; typically about 20% of the world’s oil and gas passes through Hormuz, but shipping had “all but halted.” The report also noted a G7 emergency meeting Monday, confirmed by the U.K. Treasury and French government, to discuss a possible joint release of petroleum reserves coordinated by the IEA. Tyler Goodspeed of ExxonMobil said everyone but Russia had an interest in normal traffic resuming, but Rystad’s Janiv Shah said Brent could climb to $135 if the situation persisted for four months and stay above $110 over two months; Societe Generale warned the UAE could be next to shut in output within “five to seven days,” Qatar was vulnerable, and Saudi shut-ins could become plausible in “two to three weeks.” A duplicate CNBC version repeated the same figures and quotes. In “Oil above US$100 may persist as Goldman warns rally not over” (2026-03-09), the South China Morning Post said Brent jumped 23 per cent to US$114.14 in London and WTI 24 per cent to US$113.11, with traders pricing in prolonged disruption after Iran closed Hormuz last week; Goldman Sachs said oil could challenge the 2008 record if the chokepoint handling about one-fifth of global crude flows stayed closed through March, while Deutsche Bank floated a worst-case US$200 a barrel. NPR’s “Energy analyst discusses how high Iran war could drive oil prices” (2026-03-09) featured Clay Seigle of CSIS saying there was no modern precedent for a “complete shut-off” of the Mideast Gulf’s 20 million barrels a day of oil and 10 billion cubic feet a day of LNG, and that if the war kept disrupting exports, prices could reach $200 a barrel, curbing travel and spending; he said global commodity markets mean a disruption anywhere raises prices everywhere and noted a temporary 30-day waiver allowing India to receive Russian barrels stranded at sea. On the consumer side, Newsweek’s “Chances of gas prices hitting $5 by end of March” (2026-03-09) said the average regular gas price had risen to $3.46 per gallon per GasBuddy, $3.48 per AAA, and that Polymarket users were giving 43 percent odds of $5 gas by month’s end; Mark Zandi said Americans would “soon be paying $4 a gallon,” and Patrick De Haan said prices could reach $5 to $5.50 if Brent topped $150. USA TODAY’s “Why oil prices fell below $100 per barrel, giving stocks some relief” (updated March 9, 2026, 5:24 p.m. ET) said Brent surged to $119.50 early that day but ended down 5.03% at $88.03, while WTI fell 7.59% to $84, as hopes grew for a short war and a G7 reserve release; De Haan said there was roughly an 80% chance U.S. gasoline reached $4 within a month, with the national average at $3.455. CNBC’s “Recession odds jump on Kalshi after oil tops $100” (2026-03-09) said Kalshi’s 2026 U.S. recession market jumped above 34%, its highest since November, from under 25% late last week; Polymarket showed 31% recession odds by year-end, and Kalshi participants saw about a 60% chance gas exceeded $4 this month while AAA’s national average stood at $3.48. On March 10, AP’s “US says it destroyed 16 Iranian minelayers” said American forces destroyed 16 minelayers near Hormuz, where about 20% of the world’s oil ships, after Trump warned of “a level never seen before” if mines were not removed; AP also reported Iran vowed not to let “even a single liter” be shipped to its enemies, Saudi Aramco said tankers were being rerouted and its east-west pipeline would reach 7 million barrels a day, and U.N. data put Lebanon’s displaced at more than 667,000. Reuters’ “U.S. forces sink 16 Iranian minelayers as reports say Tehran is mining the Strait of Hormuz” (2026-03-10) similarly said Central Command sank 16 minelaying vessels and that CNN reported “a few dozen” mines had been laid in recent days; Reuters said roughly 13 million barrels a day passed through Hormuz in 2025, or about 31% of seaborne crude flows, and that WTI was at $83.8 and Brent at $87.9 after having neared $120 on Monday. Reuters also reported on March 10 that Trump and Putin discussed the Iran war, Ukraine and Venezuela’s impact on oil markets, while Trump said he was “also waiving certain oil-related sanctions to reduce prices” and might have U.S. Navy and partners escort tankers if needed; separate Reuters versions said the U.S. was weighing broader easing of Russia oil sanctions, including for India, and that Putin warned a global energy crisis had arrived. NBC-style political coverage added “Emmer predicts oil prices will drop after Iran war” (2026-03-09), in which House Majority Whip Tom Emmer called the conflict a “short-term experience” and Trump wrote on Truth Social that short-term oil prices would “drop rapidly” when the Iran nuclear threat was over; on Monday WTI had risen above $100 for the first time since 2022. Reuters’ “Fraying loyalist base will challenge Iran's next leader - and Islamic Republic's survival” (2026-03-09) argued Iran’s next leader would face massive external assault and growing internal anger, with Ayatollah Ali Khamenei killed in U.S. and Israeli strikes a week earlier and Mojtaba Khamenei, his hardline son, seen as frontrunner; it said the Islamic Republic once had millions of supporters after the 1979 revolution, but corruption, repression and mismanagement had thinned that base, even as Basij members like Mahdi Rastegari and Ali Mohammad Hosseini still vowed to die for the regime.
