Texas Senate primary runoff ahead for Cornyn, Paxton as Talarico beats Crockett, 2026-03-04: in Texas’s marquee 2026 midterm primary night, NBC News projected that Republican Sen. John Cornyn and state Attorney General Ken Paxton will advance to a May 26 runoff because neither reached the nomination threshold, while state Rep. James Talarico won the Democratic Senate primary by defeating Rep. Jasmine Crockett and, by later calls, avoiding a runoff. The race was closely tied to President Donald Trump’s influence, since he had not endorsed in the GOP race, while Cornyn warned Paxton was a “dead weight to the top of the ticket” and Paxton hit back that “Texans want new leadership” and that “Texas is not for sale.” Cornyn said, “I refuse to allow a flawed, self-centered and shameless candidate like Ken Paxton to risk everything we’ve worked so hard to build over these many years,” and later added, “Judgment Day is coming for Ken Paxton.” The National Republican Senatorial Committee urged support for Cornyn, while the Senate Leadership Fund attacked Rep. Wesley Hunt for his “career-ending vanity tour.” Talarico, a 36-year-old Presbyterian seminarian and former teacher from Round Rock outside Austin, told supporters, “This is proof that there is something happening in Texas,” and “Tonight the people of our state gave this country a little bit of hope. And a little bit of hope is a dangerous thing.” Crockett, a 44-year-old Dallas-area Democrat, conceded later and said, “Texas is primed to turn blue and we must remain united because this is bigger than any one person.” The article also covered election-night polling turmoil in Dallas and Williamson counties: thousands of voters showed up at the wrong locations after Texas primary rules changed to precinct-based voting, a Dallas County judge ordered Democratic polling sites open from 7 p.m. to 9 p.m. local time, and the Texas Supreme Court later blocked the extension and ordered ballots cast by voters not in line before 7 p.m. to be separated. Dallas County Elections Department communications director Nick Solorzano said anyone in line before 7 p.m. cast a regular ballot and anyone after 7 p.m. cast a provisional ballot; Crockett said she did not expect results that night because of the Dallas County problem, and Talarico called it “voter suppression.” Elsewhere in the live coverage, state Rep. Steve Toth unseated Rep. Dan Crenshaw in a Republican House primary, making Crenshaw the first congressional incumbent to lose renomination in the 2026 midterm cycle; Rep. Tony Gonzales advanced to a runoff after allegations about an affair with a staffer who died by suicide; Henry Cuellar won the Democratic primary in Texas’ 28th Congressional District after being pardoned by Trump; and Trump-backed Tano Tijerina advanced in the GOP primary there. The live blog also noted Michael Whatley defeating Michele Morrow and Don Brown for the North Carolina GOP Senate nomination and Roy Cooper winning the North Carolina Democratic Senate primary, with early voting in that state up “about 25%,” or “701,000” ballots versus “559,000” in 2022. In Arkansas, incumbent Gov. Sarah Huckabee Sanders and Sen. Tom Cotton won their GOP primaries. Cornyn and Paxton’s primary was described as one of Texas’ most expensive ever, with ad spending on the race approaching “almost $100 million” and some reports citing “$110 million” in advertising and reserved ad time. Talarico’s side raised “more than $20 million” or “more than $21 million,” depending on the report, while Crockett raised “$3.7 million,” “nearly $8.6 million,” or transferred “$4.8 million” from her House account in different outlets. The Democratic vote total was cited at “more than 2.2 million,” and one report said “1.5 million” early votes were cast over 11 days. Talarico’s campaign benefited from national attention after Stephen Colbert said an interview could not air on CBS broadcast because of FCC concerns, and Crockett had attracted Kamala Harris’ endorsement.
