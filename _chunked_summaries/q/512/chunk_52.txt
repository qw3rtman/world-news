The US is considering Strait of Hormuz naval escorts. There are risks and it could go disastrously wrong | CNN, published 2026-03-16, said that after the US and Israel attack on Iran on February 28, 2026 and Iran’s vow to strike any vessels associated with the United States, Israel, or their partners, President Donald Trump was pressuring Japan, South Korea, and NATO members to contribute military vessels for escort duties through the Strait of Hormuz, which is about 10 miles across at its narrowest point. Naval experts said even a successful escort mission would restore only about 10% of pre-war commercial traffic, and Lloyds List Intelligence editor-in-chief Richard Meade estimated a basic convoy of five to 10 commercial vessels would need eight to 10 destroyers. As of March 2026 the Congressional Research Service said the US Navy had 73 Arleigh Burke-class destroyers and Navy officials said about 68% of US surface ships are combat-ready at any one time; in January 2026 the Navy moved its four dedicated minesweepers in the Persian Gulf back to the United States on a heavy-lift vessel for eventual scrapping and planned to rely on littoral combat ships (LCS) with the mine-countermeasure mission module, with three LCS in the region before the war. Jennifer Parker of UNSW Canberra warned massive oil tankers create blind spots for escorting destroyers, Carl Schuster said AWACS planes and reconnaissance drones would be needed to detect launches from inland Iran, and he listed spiked contact mines, moored mines triggered by acoustic or magnetic signals, and mines with counting devices; Collin Koh of the S. Rajaratnam School of International Studies said threats are too dispersed and mobile, from small fishing boats, dhows, or pleasure craft, to eradicate entirely, and he questioned whether the US Navy is prepared for an intense campaign that stresses hardware, logistics, and sailors, while Yu Ji-hoon said South Korean navy minesweepers lack tolerance, self-defense capability, and long-range logistics. Alessio Patalano said Western democracies assumed they could meet challenges to contested shipping and failed to recognize that shipping is the lifeblood artery of modern economies and can be contested; he noted capability depth among countries is a fraction of what it was in the 1980s, Collin Koh cited Houthi rebels and said a Houthi missile came within seconds of hitting a US destroyer, and Schuster said Iran has used drones, fast attack boats, and suicide boats since the late 1980s, so the tactics are no surprise.
