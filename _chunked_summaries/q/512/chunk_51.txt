Oil-poised gains and war-linked supply fears dominated a March 15-16, 2026 batch centered on the U.S.-Israeli war against Iran and its effects on the Strait of Hormuz, oil benchmarks, and diplomacy. In “Oil poised for gains as Iran war threatens export facilities” dated 2026-03-15, Reuters said Brent crude closed Friday at $103.14 a barrel, its second straight session above $100, while U.S. West Texas Intermediate settled at $98.71 after rising 3.11%, or $2.98; another Reuters version on the same date said Brent futures rose 2.67%, or $2.68, to $103.14. The article said the International Energy Agency would release more than 400 million barrels of reserves, with Asia Oceania supplies immediately and Europe and the Americas at the end of March, to offset price spikes from the U.S.-Israeli war against Iran. It said Brent and WTI had surged more than 40% since the start of the month to their highest levels since 2022, the Strait of Hormuz had been shut by Iran, and about a fifth of global oil supply moved through it. Donald Trump urged allies to deploy warships to secure the strait and, according to The Wall Street Journal, planned a coalition to escort ships through it; he also threatened more strikes on Iran’s Kharg Island oil export hub after U.S. forces hit military targets there on Saturday, drawing a defiant Iranian response. Iranian drones then hit the Fujairah oil terminal in the UAE, which serves as an outlet for about 1 million barrels per day of Murban crude, roughly 1% of world demand, and a local industry source said loading had resumed by Sunday. JPMorgan’s Natasha Kaneva said the Fujairah attack marked an escalation and named Saudi Arabia’s Ras Tanura export terminal and Abqaiq processing facilities as critical and highly vulnerable. The IEA said global oil supply could fall by 8 million barrels per day from shipping disruptions and at least 10 million barrels per day from Middle Eastern output cuts.
