Oil markets were hit by a cascading set of war and supply-shock developments across March 15-16, 2026. In “Oil poised for gains as Iran war threatens export facilities” (2026-03-15), Reuters said the U.S.-Israeli war against Iran had entered its third week, with U.S. President Donald Trump urging allies to deploy warships to help secure the Strait of Hormuz and threatening more strikes on Iran’s Kharg Island oil export hub after U.S. forces hit military targets there on Saturday. Tehran answered with a defiant vow of further retaliation. Iranian drones then hit the Fujairah oil terminal in the United Arab Emirates, a key outlet for about 1 million barrels per day of the UAE’s flagship Murban crude, roughly 1% of world demand; a local industry source said by Sunday oil loading operations there had resumed. JPMorgan analyst Natasha Kaneva called the Fujairah attack “an escalation in the conflict” and, with colleagues, flagged the Ras Tanura export terminal and Abqaiq oil processing facilities in Saudi Arabia as critical and highly vulnerable. The International Energy Agency said more than 400 million barrels of reserves would begin flowing to the market, with stock releases from Asia Oceania immediately and from Europe and the Americas at the end of March, as the agency expected global oil supply to fall by 8 million barrels per day from shipping disruptions and at least 10 million barrels per day of Middle Eastern producer output cuts. U.S. Energy Secretary Chris Wright said he expected the war to end “within the next few weeks” and that energy costs would decline once supplies rebounded; sources said the Trump administration rebuffed Middle Eastern allies’ diplomatic feelers, while Iran rejected any ceasefire until U.S. and Israeli strikes ended. A Reuters version of the same story credited Florence Tan and Shariq Khan and noted Brent and WTI were at their highest since 2022. Another Reuters piece, “Oil poised for further gains as Middle East conflict threatens export facilities” (2026-03-15), repeated that Brent and U.S. West Texas Intermediate crude had surged more than 40% since the start of the month and that the Strait of Hormuz — through which a fifth of global oil supply flows — had been shut by Iran after U.S.-Israeli attacks. The Wall Street Journal reported Trump planned to announce a coalition to escort ships through the chokepoint; Reuters also said he had asked “numerous countries” affected by the “thuggery of Iran” to help secure it, while Iran’s Foreign Minister Abbas Araqchi told CBS Iran had never asked for a ceasefire or negotiations and would defend itself “for as long as it takes.” In “Oil falls as Trump pressures allies to protect Strait of Hormuz” (2026-03-15), Brent crude futures closed at $100.21 per barrel, down 2.84%, while U.S. West Texas Intermediate fell 5.28% to $93.50 a barrel after briefly surpassing $100 intraday, as Trump increased pressure on allies to safeguard tanker traffic in the Strait of Hormuz. In “U.S. oil tops $100 as Iran war shows no sign of ending soon” (2026-03-15), U.S. crude oil hit $100 per barrel and Brent rose to $106 per barrel when futures trading resumed at 6 p.m. ET, after 32 International Energy Agency countries agreed on Wednesday, March 11, 2026, to release 400 million barrels — the largest emergency oil release ever — briefly sending prices below $80 before they rebounded. The same article said U.S. oil prices were up nearly 50% since the war started and nearly 75% year-to-date, unleaded gasoline averaged around $3.70 per gallon on Sunday, March 15, 2026, and was up about 70 cents since the U.S. and Israel launched strikes on Iran. Trump told NBC News on Saturday, March 14, that “We may hit [Kharg Island] a few more times, just for fun,” while insisting he was not yet ready to negotiate a cease-fire and saying the strikes did not affect the island’s oil infrastructure; Helima Croft of RBC Capital Markets said they had “never had the most important waterway for energy effectively closed,” Chris Wright said the Strait was not safe for ships and that military escorts could still be “a number of weeks” away, and industry veteran Andy Lipow said $100 may be only a short-term target because production is being throttled and storage is filling up as tankers cannot load. Reuters again on 2026-03-15 reported the IEA’s more than 400 million barrels release, immediate Asia Oceania draw, later Europe and Americas supply, and the Trump administration’s refusal of negotiations, with editors Shri Navaratnam, Bill Berkrot and Diane Craft. On 2026-03-15, Reuters also reported Trump demanding other countries help secure the vital Strait of Hormuz and naming China, France, Japan, South Korea and Britain in a social media post, warning NATO of a “very bad” future if members failed to assist; he said countries that receive oil through the strait “must take care of that passage,” and should send “war ships” and even minesweepers, while noting the U.S. Navy would “soon” start escorting tankers, and Abbas Araqchi countered that Iran was “stable and strong enough.” On 2026-03-15, The Times of India said Trump planned a “global coalition” to escort commercial vessels through the Strait of Hormuz, that 13 U.S. service members had died in the conflict, that Iran was using sea mines and small vessels to restrict passage, and that after two weeks of U.S. operations oil had surpassed $100 per barrel and U.S. gas prices had risen more than 70 cents per gallon. On 2026-03-15, France 24’s “Socialist leads Paris mayoral race but leftist rival threatens to split vote” and related copies said Emmanuel Grégoire won 37.98% in the first round of the Paris mayoral race, Rachida Dati took 25.46%, and Pierre-Yves Bournazel, Sophia Chikirou and Sarah Knafo cleared the 10% threshold for the March 22 runoff; Chikirou of France Unbowed (LFI) got 11.72% and threatened to split the left despite Grégoire’s left-wing alliance with the Greens and Communists. The campaign was marked by public anger over child sexual abuse allegations involving nursery and primary school monitors, roadwork disruptions and mounting debt; Grégoire promised a “change of method” and to continue the “green revolution” of Bertrand Delanoë and Anne Hidalgo, while Dati urged Bournazel to rally behind her and faced a September 2026 trial for corruption and abuse of power connected to her former European Parliament mandate. In the French municipal sweep, Barron’s on 2026-03-15 said the first-round local elections were a political barometer ahead of France’s presidential polls, with Socialist Emmanuel Gregoire leading in Paris and the far right looking strong in several southern cities; NPR’s “Mayoral elections across France reflect the rise of the far-right” said 36,000 municipalities were choosing mayors, 32-year-old Sarah Knafo of Reconquete campaigned on “a happy city” and represented an affluent, harder-line anti-immigration right, while Marine Le Pen’s Rassemblement National was poised to take Nice, Marseille and Toulon. NPR also noted support for Knafo among Paris retiree Edith Daubert in the 7th arrondissement, complaints about cars, bike lanes, crime, insecurity and public housing, and analyst Jean-Yves Camus’ contrast between Reconquete’s free-market line and RN’s more socialist economics. ABC Listen’s “Invasion of Lebanon looms” said Israel was reportedly preparing a ground invasion of southern Lebanon against Hezbollah, the Iranian-backed militia. AL-Monitor’s “Israel and Lebanon expected to hold talks soon” said Israeli officials expected talks with Lebanon led by Ron Dermer, Benjamin Netanyahu’s confidant and former minister of strategic affairs, with France involved; Lebanese President Joseph Aoun wanted a full ceasefire as the first condition, Lebanon had not yet received official notification from Israel, and the talks were intended to secure a durable ceasefire that would see Hezbollah disarmed. The report said Hezbollah opened fire at Israel on March 2, 2026, to avenge the killing of Iran’s supreme leader, that Israel’s offensive in Lebanon had killed more than 800 people and displaced more than 800,000, and that Beirut had banned Hezbollah’s military activities even as the group kept firing hundreds of rockets. Semafor’s “Israel says it is prepared for at least three more weeks of war” said Israel told the U.S. it had been running critically low on air-defense interceptors but now said it had enough to counter Iranian attacks; the Israeli military said it was preparing for at least three more weeks of war and the government approved urgent defense procurement funding, while The Washington Post said Donald Trump was nearing some Pentagon goals but facing a hardened Iranian regime that could damage the global economy. Iran International’s “Israeli military says expanding strikes to western, central Iran” said the Israeli military announced expanded strikes on Iranian infrastructure in western and central Iran. On 2026-03-16 Reuters’ “Middle East oil becomes most expensive in the world as war cuts supply” from Singapore and New Delhi said cash Dubai crude for May-loading cargoes hit a record $157.66 per barrel, surpassing Brent’s 2008 all-time high of $147.50, while Oman crude futures touched $152.58 and Murban futures settled at $114.03, leaving Dubai swaps at a record $60.82 premium versus 90 cents in February and Oman at a $55.74 premium versus 75 cents. Kpler data showed Middle East crude exports falling to 11.665 million barrels per day in March from nearly 19 million in February, about 32% below March 2025, after shipping through the Strait of Hormuz was halted; TotalEnergies was cited as the main buyer in the Platts window, snapping up 21 million barrels of Oman and Murban crude across 42 cargoes, while Brazilian spot crude premiums hit $12-$15 a barrel to dated Brent and West African cargoes rose about $1 a barrel, as S&P Global Platts said it was seeking feedback on Middle East crude deliverability and the Platts Dubai benchmark methodology. The Reuters story credited Florence Tan and Siyi Liu, with editing by Harikrishnan Nair, Stephen Coates and Jason Neely. Also on 2026-03-16, CBS News said U.S. gas averaged $3.72 per gallon according to AAA, Brent slipped about 2.4% to $101.93 and WTI fell 4.1% to $94.62 after topping $102 earlier, while oil had risen from roughly $70 per barrel since the U.S.-Israeli attacks on Iran, and Iran had responded with drones and missiles against Gulf energy infrastructure. The S&P 500 was up 89 points, or 1.3%, to 6,720.75, with the Dow up 1.2% and Nasdaq 1.5%, even as Paul Christopher of Wells Fargo said the rapid escalation suggested both sides faced growing constraints that might preclude a long conflict. White House press secretary Karoline Leavitt said countries benefiting from U.S. military action should help “take care of that passage,” defended the goal of ensuring Iran never gets a nuclear weapon, and declined to say what contingency plans existed if others did not help reopen the Strait. The Times of India’s UAE fuel-price piece said Brent was close to $105 per barrel, about one-fifth of global oil still moved through the Strait of Hormuz, Brent prices influenced around two-thirds of internationally traded crude, and the UAE Fuel Price Committee — in place since 2015 — reviews Brent, refining and distribution costs plus currency fluctuations to set monthly pump prices, with analysts saying UAE petrol could rise toward Dh3.60-Dh4 per litre if crude reaches $110-$120 and possibly Dh3.80-Dh4 in April; Kuwait is more heavily subsidised, while Qatar, Oman and Bahrain also link local prices to international benchmarks, and higher oil revenue could strengthen Gulf government finances. On 2026-03-16, Reuters again emphasized that cash Dubai crude had become the world’s most expensive oil, that the Strait closure had reduced global supply, and that S&P Global Platts’ MOC activity remained “robust this month, with multiple cargoes delivered,” while ABC/BBC/Reuters-type reporting also captured Trump’s Beijing summit pressure, saying he expected China to help unblock Hormuz before his March 31-April 2 Beijing trip, that the last U.S. presidential visit to China was Trump’s in 2017, and that he told Air Force One he may delay because the two weeks until then were “a long time.” He linked the issue to China’s dependence on Hormuz — saying China got about 90% of its oil through the strait, though cited data also said Beijing held 1.2 billion barrels of onshore crude stockpiles, enough for three to four months, and that Hormuz flows represented just 6.6% of China’s total energy consumption — while CFR’s Rush Doshi said seaborne oil imports through the strait are less than half of China’s total oil shipments and that China has spent two decades diversifying and building strategic reserves. Edward Fishman called Trump’s summit threat a “bluff,” noted China’s leverage from being the world’s biggest producer of solar panels, batteries and EVs, and said the U.S. had launched trade investigations under Section 301 that Beijing called unilateral and abusive, prompting formal representations and a warning to monitor progress and defend China’s interests. Finally, CNBC on 2026-03-16 said traders were nervously entertaining a $200 oil scenario after Iran’s stark warning that it would continue blocking shipping traffic through the Strait of Hormuz; Greg Newman, group CEO of Onyx Capital Group, said $200 was “not ridiculous” given the Iran war.
