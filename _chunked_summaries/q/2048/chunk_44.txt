Oil prices: Analysts raise the alarm as crude soars over Iran war, 2026-03-09: CNBC’s Holly Ellyatt reported that after fresh U.S. and Israeli strikes across Iran, including oil depots, international benchmark Brent crude futures with May delivery rose 11.6% to trade at $103.47 per barrel and U.S. West Texas Intermediate (WTI) futures with April delivery were 12.2% higher at $101.97 per barrel; both markets had earlier hit extreme session peaks, with Brent at $119.5 per barrel and WTI at $119.48 per barrel, before easing after news that G7 finance ministers were holding an emergency meeting to discuss a possible joint release of petroleum reserves coordinated by the International Energy Agency (confirmed to CNBC by the U.K.’s Treasury and the French government). The article said Iraq and Kuwait had already begun to shut in crude production, while Societe Generale analysts warned the United Arab Emirates could be next within five to seven days and Saudi Arabia could face plausible shut-ins if the Strait of Hormuz stayed closed for a further two to three weeks; they also said prolonged shut-ins “materially increase” restart-complication risk and noted Qatar is vulnerable despite modest oil volumes relative to LNG exposure. The Strait of Hormuz normally carries about 20% of the world’s oil and gas, but shipping traffic had “all but halted,” and Tyler Goodspeed, chief economist at ExxonMobil, said the prior consensus was that there was “abundant oil on the water and some strategic reserves to cover any short-term gap,” yet he argued there are more probable scenarios in which the strait remains effectively closed “harder for longer” than ones where normal traffic resumes, adding “everyone but Russia” has an interest in normal traffic resuming. Neil Atkinson, former head of oil at the International Energy Agency, speaking on CNBC’s “Squawk Box Europe,” said the effective closure of the strategically vital strait had no precedent and that “the sky is the limit” for oil prices because there is no precedent for a real closure of Hormuz. Janiv Shah, vice president of oil markets at Rystad Energy, said if the disruption persisted four months Brent could climb to $135 per barrel, while on a two-month view prices would stay above $110, because “there is nowhere left to put the oil” as physical supply is being choked off by drone strikes.
