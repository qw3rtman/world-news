Iran’s march 2026 leadership and war cascade centered on the appointment of Mojtaba Khamenei, the second son of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, as supreme leader after his father was killed on February 28, 2026, in a U.S.–Israeli strike on Tehran; the clerical Assembly of Experts selected him in early March 2026, and by March 8 Iranian state media and AP/Reuters/CNN live updates were describing him as the third supreme leader of the Islamic Republic. The AP and Reuters accounts stressed that Mojtaba Khamenei is a Hojjatoleslam with no prior elected or appointed government post, that he had studied in Qom, and that he had long served his father as a gatekeeper and, in practice, a “mini-supreme leader”; he had also been sanctioned by the U.S. Treasury in 2019 for representing the supreme leader in an official capacity, and many clerics view the slain Ali Khamenei, who ruled for 37 years and died aged 86, as a martyr likened to Imam Hussein. Reuters and AP noted Donald Trump called Mojtaba’s ascension “unacceptable,” and warned in an ABC News interview that a new Iranian leader “is not going to last long without my approval,” while analysts cited the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps as the main power center expected to gain even more authority; Paul Salem compared Iran’s trajectory to Iraq under Saddam Hussein after 1991 and Syria under Bashar al-Assad after 2012. The Reuters piece said the country was already battered by inflation, currency collapse, widening poverty, and deep domestic unrest, with at least 1,230 people reported dead in Iran, more than 300 in Lebanon, and around a dozen in Israel; it also said “the Great Satan” narrative, martyrdom rhetoric, and support from Hezbollah and other loyalists framed the succession, though some internal political figures criticized it as creating a clerical version of the shah. ### The NPR/AP/Reuters batch on March 8 described the war as a U.S.-Israeli campaign launched February 28, 2026, and running into its second week, with seven U.S. service members dead by Sunday, March 8, including six U.S. Army Reserve members killed in Kuwait while working at a command center and a seventh who died in Saudi Arabia from injuries sustained in a March 1 attack; a dignified transfer for the first six was held at Dover Air Force Base with President Donald Trump and Vice President Vance attending on Saturday, March 7. Central Command and other reporting said Iran and Israel were attacking each other’s critical infrastructure: Israel hit an Iranian civilian oil facility for the first time, also striking fuel depots and other targets in central Iran, including around 50 ammunition bunkers in Tehran and the headquarters of the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps’ aerospace force, while Iran’s attacks damaged a Bahrain desalination plant for the first time and hit a Saudi oil field at Shaybah, where Saudi Arabia said its Defense Ministry intercepted a drone and warned Iran it would be the “biggest loser” if attacks continued. Iran also fired on Gulf targets, including an alleged strike on a desalination plant in Bahrain and attacks said by the IRGC spokesperson to be using 60% of Iran’s firepower against U.S. bases and strategic interests; Iraq was hit too, with a missile striking the Erbil airport and killing a Kurdish security member, while rockets hit the U.S. Embassy in Baghdad and Komala said a base near Sulaymaniyah was struck. In Lebanon, the Israeli military hit a hotel in central Beirut—video showed black smoke billowing from the Ramada Plaza—killing at least four people, while the broader offensive against Hezbollah had by early March killed nearly 400 people, including 83 children, and displaced over half a million; an Israeli strike on a Beirut hotel reportedly killed five commanders from the Quds Force’s Lebanon Corps and Palestine Corps, and Israel said it had killed five commanders of the Iranian Revolutionary Guard based in Lebanon. The reports also corrected that Israel’s oil-facility attacks in Iran began on Saturday, not Sunday, and described Britain’s role: Prime Minister Keir Starmer approved “defensive” U.S. action from bases in Gloucestershire and Diego Garcia in the Indian Ocean, prompting Trump to attack Starmer on Truth Social over carrier deployments and to say, “We will remember. We don’t need people that join Wars after we’ve already won!” China’s Foreign Minister Wang Yi, meanwhile, called for a ceasefire, saying “Might does not make right,” and President Trump similarly posted about Britain and other allies. ### CNN, Reuters, AP, Bloomberg, and the South China Morning Post all reported on March 8 that the conflict had become a test of U.S.-China diplomacy as Wang Yi, speaking during the fourth session of the 14th National People’s Congress in Beijing, said the two sides needed to “make thorough preparations,” create a suitable environment, manage existing risks, and remove unnecessary disruptions before a planned Trump-Xi meeting; he warned against “erecting tariff barriers” and “pushing for economic and technological decoupling,” likening it to using kindling to put out a fire and saying “You will only get burned,” and argued that turning their backs on each other would bring “mutual misperception and miscalculation” and could drag the world into confrontation. The scheduled Trump trip to China was described as March 31 to April 2, 2026, the first visit by a sitting U.S. president since 2017, though Beijing had not yet formally confirmed the dates; Wang Yi said he had spoken by phone with at least seven foreign ministers since the conflict began, including those of Russia, Iran, and Israel, and that high-level exchanges with Washington were already “on the table.” Bloomberg said Wang Yi’s remarks came hours after Trump threatened to expand strikes on Iran and hit Beijing’s strategic partner very hard, while Wang answered by saying 2026 could be a “landmark year” for China-U.S. relations if both sides treated each other with sincerity and good faith and developed ties that were “sound, steady and sustainable.” The Japan Times said a March summit in Busan, South Korea, at Gimhae International Airport during the APEC summit was unlikely to deliver even a limited reset of business and investment ties: American business leaders had not secured a CEO delegation, Beijing was not on track to win investment protections for Chinese companies, and the backdrop was a “bruising period” of Trump tariffs and China’s chokehold on rare earths exports, though October talks had lowered tariffs to below 50% for one year after duties once rose to well over 100%. ### Gold and markets reflected the war shock. On March 9, 2026, CBS News said experts saw gold above $5,000 an ounce as central banks continued buying, de-dollarization accelerated, and geopolitical escalation—including “any escalation involving Iran”—pushed demand for safe-haven assets; Hiren Chandaria of Monetary Metals called it a “multi-year bull cycle,” Thomas Winmill of Midas Funds said gold could “surpass $5,500,” and James Cordier said the dollar’s firming from fewer expected Fed cuts would cap prices somewhat, while advising investors to keep precious metals to about 10% of a portfolio and to talk to an investment professional.
