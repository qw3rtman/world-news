Oil markets were rattled across mid-March 2026 by the U.S.-Israeli war against Iran, with Reuters’ “Oil poised for gains as Iran war threatens export facilities” on 2026-03-15, Reuters’ “Oil poised for further gains as Middle East conflict threatens export facilities” on 2026-03-15, and a Reuters follow-up about market reaction all centering on the same set of flashpoints: the Strait of Hormuz, Kharg Island, and the Fujairah oil terminal. The International Energy Agency announced a record release of “more than 400 million barrels” of oil reserves, with reserves from “Asia Oceania” to begin flowing immediately and stocks from “Europe and the Americas” to become available “at the end of March,” as a bid to counter price spikes as global supply was expected to fall by “8 million barrels per day” from shipping disruptions and by “at least 10 million barrels per day” from Middle Eastern producer cuts. Brent crude, described as the international benchmark, rose on Friday, 2026-03-15, to “$103.14 per barrel” in one Reuters piece, “2.67%, or $2.68,” and U.S. West Texas Intermediate settled at “$98.71 a barrel” after climbing “3.11%, or $2.98”; another March 15 Reuters market story, “Oil falls as Trump pressures allies to protect Strait of Hormuz,” reported Brent closing at “$100.21 per barrel” after a “2.84% loss” and WTI falling “5.28% to settle at $93.50 per barrel,” even though WTI had surpassed “$100” intraday. The war pushed both benchmarks “more than 40%” above where they started the month, to their highest levels since “2022,” while in an evening trading resumption on March 15 U.S. crude jumped “more than 2% to around $102 per barrel” and Brent rose to “$106 per barrel.” By March 16, 2026, oil remained elevated: Reuters and other market stories said Brent had jumped “2.9% to almost $106.12 a barrel” and U.S. oil was up “2.6%” to “$101.53,” while CBS News reported Brent slipping “about 2.4% to $101.93 per barrel” and WTI falling “4.1% to $94.62 per barrel”; Reuters’ “Middle East oil becomes most expensive in the world as war cuts supply” on 2026-03-16 said cash Dubai crude for May-loading cargoes hit “$157.66 per barrel,” above Brent’s 2008 all-time high of “$147.50,” while Oman crude futures reached “$152.58 per barrel,” and Murban futures settled at “$114.03.” That Reuters piece said Middle East crude exports fell to “11.665 million barrels per day” in March 2026 from “nearly 19 million” in February, about “32% versus March 2025,” as shipping through the Strait of Hormuz was halted by the war, and traders saw premiums for Brazilian spot crude at “$12-$15 a barrel to dated Brent” and West African crude premiums up about “$1 a barrel” from a month earlier; TotalEnergies was identified as the main buyer, snapping up “21 million barrels” of Oman and Murban crude in “42 cargoes.” The conflict’s geographic choke point was repeatedly the Strait of Hormuz, through which “a fifth” or “20%” of global oil flows and also, in the Reuters/Reuters follow-ups, “a fifth of global oil supply” and “roughly one-fifth” of daily supply; Tehran shut it down in response to U.S.-Israeli attacks, and Trump urged allies to deploy warships and even planned to announce “a coalition to escort ships through the Strait of Hormuz,” according to The Wall Street Journal report cited in Reuters, while on 2026-03-15 he also asked allies to help secure tanker traffic and was said to be negotiating with “seven countries,” naming “China, France, Japan, South Korea, and Britain,” and warning NATO could have a “very bad” future if it failed to help. U.S. forces had struck military targets on “Kharg Island” on “Friday, March 13, 2026,” prompting Trump to threaten “more strikes” on the island’s oil export hub and to tell NBC News on “Saturday, March 14, 2026,” “We may hit [Kharg Island] a few more times, just for fun,” while also saying the strikes did not affect the island’s oil infrastructure and he was not yet ready to negotiate a cease-fire. Iran responded with a “defiant response of further retaliation,” and Iranian drones hit the UAE’s “Fujairah oil terminal,” a key outlet for about “1 million barrels per day” of the UAE’s flagship Murban crude, roughly “1% of world demand”; local industry sources said oil loading operations at Fujairah had “resumed” by Sunday, though Reuters later noted it was uncertain whether activity had fully returned to normal. JPMorgan analyst “Natasha Kaneva” said the Fujairah attack marked “an escalation in the conflict,” and JPMorgan analysts also flagged Saudi Arabia’s “Ras Tanura export terminal” and “Abqaiq oil processing facilities” as critical and highly vulnerable. U.S. Energy Secretary “Chris Wright” said he expected the war to end “within the next few weeks,” that “energy costs will decline” once oil supplies rebound, and that the Strait of Hormuz was “not safe for ships to pass,” adding military escorts might still be “a number of weeks” away; the White House had considered “naval escorts to protect oil tankers” but had not given details. Other market commentary reinforced the danger: RBC Capital Markets’ “Helima Croft” said they had “never had the most important waterway for energy effectively closed,” and industry expert “Andy Lipow” argued the “$100” oil mark might only be a short-term target because production was being throttled and storage was filling up as tankers could not load. Additional coverage on 2026-03-15 and 2026-03-16 linked the war to gasoline, with U.S. unleaded gas averaging around “$3.70 per gallon” on Sunday, then “$3.72 per gallon” on Monday, while CBS said the price was up “almost 79 cents from a month earlier”; another piece noted prices had risen nearly “50% since the war started” and nearly “75% so far this year,” and that the S&P 500 rose “89 points, or 1.3%, to 6,720.75” in morning trading as investors weighed the conflict, with the Dow up “1.2%” and Nasdaq “1.5%.” A CNBC report dated 2026-03-16, “Oil prices: Why traders are getting nervous about Iran’s $200 warning,” quoted Onyx Capital Group chief executive “Greg Newman” saying “$200” a barrel “was not ridiculous” amid Iran’s threat to keep blocking shipping through the Strait of Hormuz.
