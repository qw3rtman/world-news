### ARTICLE 44c6902e0d80 QA | 2026-03-09 | Oil prices: Analysts raise the alarm as crude soars over Iran war
Published on 2026-03-09, this CNBC report, contributed to by Holly Ellyatt, said Brent crude futures with May delivery rose 11.6% to trade at $103.47 per barrel and at one point hit a session high of $119.5 per barrel, while U.S. West Texas Intermediate (WTI) futures with April delivery were 12.2% higher at $101.97 per barrel and hit a session high of $119.48 per barrel. The surge came after fresh U.S. and Israeli strikes across Iran over the weekend, including oil depots among the targets, and traders said shipping traffic had all but halted through the Strait of Hormuz, through which typically about 20% of the world’s oil and gas passes. Analysts said Iraq and Kuwait had already begun to shut in crude production. Neil Atkinson, former head of oil at the International Energy Agency, speaking on CNBC’s “Squawk Box Europe,” warned the situation was unprecedented because there is no precedent for the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz and said “the sky is the limit” for oil prices. Janiv Shah, vice president of oil markets at Rystad Energy, said prices would stay above $110 per barrel on a two-month basis and could climb to $135 per barrel if the disruption persisted for four months, adding that Middle Eastern producers had reached a point where “there is nowhere left to put the oil” because physical supply was being choked off by drone strikes. Societe Generale analysts said the United Arab Emirates was likely the next producer at risk of shutting in output within five to seven days, with Saudi Arabia vulnerable if the strait remained closed for a further two to three weeks; they also said Qatar was vulnerable, though its oil volumes are modest relative to LNG exposure, and warned prolonged shut-ins “materially increase” the risk of restart complications. Tyler Goodspeed, chief economist at ExxonMobil, said the earlier consensus was that there was abundant oil on the water and some strategic reserves to cover any short-term gap, but he argued that everyone but Russia had an interest in normal traffic resuming through the strait and that there are more probable scenarios in which it remains effectively closed harder for longer than ones where normal traffic resumes.
