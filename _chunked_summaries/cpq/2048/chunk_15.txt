### Fact-checking Trump 2026 State of the Union address on jobs, inflation, crime, immigration and more === 2026-02-25
In “Fact-checking Trump 2026 State of the Union address on jobs, inflation, crime, immigration and more === 2026-02-25,” published Feb. 25, 2026, 12:14 AM EST and updated Feb. 25, 2026, 10:08 AM EST, Jane C. Timm and Adam Edelman examined President Donald Trump’s “longest State of the Union address in history” Tuesday night and tested a series of claims about the economy, food stamps, the military, crime, drugs, inflation, jobs, investment, taxes, migrants, fraud, eggs, beef, wars, mail voting and elections. They said Trump touted his administration’s economic policies and immigration enforcement while condemning Democrats and the previous administration, but made “exaggerated, misleading and false claims throughout the evening.” The fact-check said Trump’s claim that “We have lifted 2.4 million Americans — a record — off of food stamps” needed context because nearly 42 million Americans rely on SNAP and around 2.4 million people are expected to lose eligibility under new work requirements in his “One Big Beautiful Bill”; the Center on Budget Policy and Priorities estimated those losses. The work rules require adults ages 55 to 64 and parents whose youngest children are at least 14 to document “80 hours per month of work, education or volunteering,” with eligibility limited to three months in a three-year period without documentation, and exemptions for veterans and homeless people removed. Trump’s “Warrior Dividend of $1,776” was said to have come from tariffs, but that claim was false: the Defense Department said the money for 1.28 million active-duty service members and 174,000 reserve members came from a supplemental housing fund Congress appropriated in his domestic spending bill, and acting comptroller Jules W. Hurst III said, “We are grateful to President Trump, Chairman [Roger] Wicker, Chairman [Mike] Rogers and the other members of Congress who have made this Warrior Dividend possible through the One Big Beautiful Bill Act.” On Washington, D.C., Trump’s “almost no crime anymore” and “lowest level ever recorded” claim was called exaggerated because MPD data showed crime down in 2026 so far in all but one category, assault with a dangerous weapon, and since Jan. 1 there had still been nine homicides, 126 assaults with a dangerous weapon and 322 motor vehicle thefts. On prescription drugs, Trump’s claim that “Other presidents tried to do it, but they never could” was false because President Joe Biden’s 2022 Inflation Reduction Act capped insulin at $35 a month for Medicare, placed a $2,000 annual out-of-pocket cap on Medicare drug costs and allowed Medicare to negotiate prices, with negotiated prices taking effect Jan. 1 for Eliquis and Imbruvica; Trump’s own deals with at least 16 drugmakers and the self-pay platform TrumpRx, which offers cash prices on 43 medications, did not generally change pharmacy-counter costs for people with private insurance or Medicare. The inflation claim that Biden left “the worst inflation in history” and Trump cut core inflation to 1.7% in the last three months of 2025 was false; annual inflation when he took office was 2.9%, April briefly saw 2.3%, then tariffs pushed it up again, while the peak of recent inflation was 8.9% in 2022 and the highest ever was around 14% in the 1980s. The article said “more Americans are working today than at any time in the history of our country” was true, though job growth slowed sharply, with 584,000 jobs created in 2025, the worst year for job creation since 2020 and, excluding recessions, since 2003. Trump’s “more than $18 trillion” in investment commitments was false because the White House website’s pledges totaled $9.7 trillion, and Bloomberg Economics found more than $2.5 trillion was not investment, about $3.5 trillion was opaque sovereign pledges, another $3.5 trillion corporate investments, with $2.9 trillion of that for data centers and more than $250 billion announced or planned before Trump retook office in January; Fujifilm Biotechnologies was cited as an example of long-term commitments taking years. On taxes, the bill did not eliminate all taxes on tips, overtime and Social Security: some workers can deduct overtime and tips within income caps and maximum deduction limits, some seniors may pay less thanks to a new deduction, but Social Security income is still taxed. Trump’s murder-rate boast was deemed true, citing a Council on Criminal Justice analysis predicting the nationwide homicide rate could be the lowest in law enforcement or public health data going back to 1900, though crime had been falling for years. His claim the One Big Beautiful Bill was the largest tax cut in American history was false; the Tax Foundation said it was the sixth largest. The claim that 70,000 new construction jobs had been added was exaggerated; BLS found 44,000 from January 2025 to January 2026. His remarks that “millions and millions” of migrants came from prisons and mental institutions were marked “needs context”: 10 million entered illegally during the Biden administration, but no evidence supported mass flows from those institutions; and the claim about 11,888 murders was not clearly attributable to Biden because more than 13,000 convicted murderers without legal status are not in ICE custody and could have entered over decades, according to DHS. Trump’s claim that Somali members of Minnesota’s community had pillaged $19 billion “lacks evidence”; Justice Department charges totalled 98 people in Minnesota, 85 Somali, with $1 billion in fraud, while House Oversight estimated fraud “could exceed $9 billion” and prosecutors suggested about $9 billion. Egg prices had fallen about 48% from January 2025 to January 2026, not 60%, and beef prices were still at records, with ground beef at $6.75 per pound, up nearly 22% from a year earlier. On wars, Trump’s “I ended eight wars” was called exaggerated, with debate over whether conflicts between Israel and Iran, Egypt and Ethiopia, Thailand and Cambodia, Serbia and Kosovo, Rwanda and the Democratic Republic of Congo, Armenia and Azerbaijan, and India and Pakistan were ended by him, especially since fighting resumed in some cases and India denied U.S. mediation in its ceasefire with Pakistan. His call for the SAVE America Act was said to be false on mail voting because the House-approved, Senate-pending measure would add proof-of-citizenship and voter-ID requirements but not eliminate mail voting. And the claim that “Cheating is rampant in our elections” was false because there is no evidence of widespread fraud; the Heritage Foundation had only dozens of cases over decades amid tens of millions of ballots. ###
