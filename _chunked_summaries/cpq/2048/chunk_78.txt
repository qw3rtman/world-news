But the article emphasizes what he did not do: the group issued no clear declaration of military intervention, no direct escalations, and no clear threats against Israeli or U.S. interests, and its media messaging was “more disciplined and subdued” than usual. Hezbollah in Lebanon, by contrast, “wasted no time in entering the fray,” and the article says that with “Hezbollah and Iraqi factions now engaged,” Iran has already begun activating some proxies. The Houthis, it says, are in a different geographical and political environment, facing “complex internal and regional considerations” that make any decision to enter war more delicate, especially because a broad confrontation could open “multiple fronts” and “a Pandora’s box with uncontrollable consequences.” The article cites recent precedent showing Houthi pragmatism: in “May 2025,” the “Sultanate of Oman” brokered an agreement between the group and the U.S. that reduced tensions in the Red Sea after months of Houthi attacks on international shipping, and during the “12-day war in June 2025” the Houthis confined themselves to rhetoric of solidarity rather than direct military intervention despite moral pressure from the Iranian axis. If the war continues, the most likely course, the article says, is “a calculated escalation through symbolic operations or carefully calibrated pressure tactics,” possibly via the “Red Sea and the Bab al-Mandeb Strait,” which it calls one of the most important strategic pressure points on global trade and energy routes and a place the Houthis have used to target or threaten shipping and to make the Red Sea attacks part of the regional pressure equation linked to the Gaza war. Direct intervention, including bombing Israel or large-scale operations against American interests, remains “a high-risk option,” especially because Israel is thought to consider the Houthis a “deferred target” and could seize an opportunity for a broad strike on their leadership and military infrastructure. Internal Yemeni dynamics matter too: the article says broad involvement could provoke “unpredictable internal shifts” amid efforts to rearrange the balance of power in the government camp and reorganize military decision-making “with Saudi support,” while “accumulating economic challenges” plus “intermittent security and social tensions” make escalation risky. The text says the Houthis might change calculations only if the war becomes an “existential threat to the Iranian regime” or drags on enough to reshape the regional balance of power. In the end, the article frames the central question as whether the Houthis will remain in Iran’s regional network of influence or emerge as a force making “its own calculations,” rather than simply following the rhythm set by others.
