Officially, Kenya's land borders to Somalia have been closed since 2011, after attacks blamed on Al-Shabab, the al-Qaeda-affiliated insurgency group based in Somalia, led Kenya to shut the frontier; Kenya briefly agreed in May 2023 to a phased reopening of the common border, then reversed course two months later after the killing of five civilians and eight police officers near the frontier. The frontier itself is 680 kilometers long (422 miles) and has also been disputed over a potentially oil- and gas-rich chunk of the Indian Ocean. Border-town voices welcomed the plan: Mohamed Aden Ali, a trader in Beled Hawo opposite Kenya's Mandera, said, "It is a big move. Somali economic analyst Ahmed Khadar said the move made economic sense because Somalia joined the East African Community in 2024, Kenya is the only EAC member bordering Somalia, and Kenya "can be the gateway to connecting Somalia to the other members of the East African Community." The article also noted Somalia's broader push to boost connectivity, trade and tourism through online visas and visas on arrival, plus its joining this month of the East African Community (EAC) passport system after becoming a member of the bloc in 2024; in Mogadishu, western tourists including Sherry Roberts and Australian Nick Benady toured the ruins of formerly grand buildings, beaches and markets, while tour boat operator Aweis Ali at Liido Beach said more tourists were visiting and sharing activities on TikTok. The reopening nonetheless raised security concerns: poor roads, banditry risks and immigration logistics remain challenges, and Ruto said a multi-agency security team would patrol the area and police deployment would be doubled. Khadar said security cooperation had once been "very low, maybe because of weak government in Somalia," though he argued the threat was less acute than 10 or 15 years ago.
