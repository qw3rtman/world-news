### Will Gulf states enter the Iran war? | 2026-03-01
The DW article “Will Gulf states enter the Iran war?” (2026-03-01) says that after a joint US-Israeli attack on Iran started the current war, Iranian missiles hit or threatened a range of Gulf targets over the past two days: a luxury hotel, ports, inner city neighborhoods, industrial areas, airports and oil infrastructure. Iran’s foreign minister told the United Nations Security Council in a letter that Iran would “exercise its right of self-defense,” and the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps, or IRGC, said it would consider all US assets in the region legitimate targets. The escalation included an Iranian missile striking the headquarters of the US Navy in Bahrain, Qatar saying it intercepted missiles heading for Al Udeid Air Base, the largest American base in the region, and Kuwait saying Iranian strikes hit Ali Al-Salem air base, where members of the US air force are hosted. Hasan Alhasan of the International Institute for Strategic Studies, or IISS, said Iran had “crossed a new threshold by targeting Gulf cities, airports and energy infrastructure,” arguing the Gulf states are “much closer to Iran and not as heavily defended as Israel,” improving Iran’s chances of landing hits. After the strikes, Gulf leaders commiserated, expressed unity and condemned Iran; Anwar Gargash, former UAE foreign minister and now a foreign policy adviser to the UAE president, wrote on X, “Your war is not with your neighbors,” and later told The National that the UAE won’t just sit by while being attacked. The piece explains that Gulf states had long seen hosting US bases or troops and buying US weapons as the best deterrent, while Saudi Arabia and the United Arab Emirates had spent diplomatic capital improving ties with Iran and urging a diplomatic solution to Iran’s nuclear capabilities. Before the US and Israel launched the weekend attacks, Saudi Arabia, the UAE and Jordan said they would not allow Americans to use their territory to launch airstrikes against Iran; yet Alhasan said the “exchange of mutual assurances with Iran proved ineffective.” The article says Gulf states are now on the verge of being pulled into a conflict they want no part of, potentially even into an alliance with Israel despite the majority having no official diplomatic relations with it, though the attacks also united them despite recent political disagreements between Saudi Arabia and the UAE. In the “Fighting a longer war” section, experts say the Gulf states probably will not enter with their own militaries; Saudi Arabia’s military is described as the most powerful and well-funded, but likely responses are more limited: greater US access to territory or airspace, or possibly limited strikes in narrow self-defense. Alhasan says that barring “mass casualty events or major outages due to Iranian attacks on Gulf cities or critical national infrastructure,” direct Gulf retaliation remains unlikely, while Andreas Krieg of King’s College London says Gulf militaries entering the war directly is possible but “more likely in narrow self-defense terms rather than through offensive strikes.” Krieg says Gulf states will try to contain spillover rather than actively join a widening war, and once missiles started landing they faced an “ugly choice: Respond hard and risk being locked into escalation, or respond softly and look exposed at home.” The piece gives Gulf-state figures that on the first day of fighting Iran fired 137 missiles at the UAE, 66 at Qatar and 45 at Bahrain, and says the first steps will be “maximizing air and missile defense, protecting bases and critical infrastructure, tightening internal security, and putting continuity plans into motion.” Krieg says Oman and Qatar, which have been working on US-Iran negotiations, will keep lines open and search for an off-ramp. Both he and other analysts say Gulf states will use their close ties to Washington to push for an end to the campaign because they pay much of the retaliation bill through shipping disruption, investor confidence, energy infrastructure or direct strikes. The article warns this threatens the economic model of tourism and being a safe regional hub for investment on which the UAE and Saudi Arabia base their non-oil future. It concludes that the security equation based on a US alliance and improving relations with Iran has been badly shaken; Alhasan says the long-term damage to Iran’s relations with its Gulf neighbors is probably already done and Gulf leaders will focus on deterrence, while Krieg says some countries may recalibrate the US presence and diversify partnerships so geography does not force them into the frontline of someone else’s war. ###
