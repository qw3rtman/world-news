### Who is boycotting the 2026 State of the Union? See a list | 2026-02-25
On Tuesday, Feb. 24, President Donald Trump delivered the State of the Union and, according to USA TODAY’s count, more than 80 Democratic members of the House of Representatives and Senate announced they would boycott the speech. At least 30 representatives and senators skipped the address to attend the “People’s State of the Union” hosted by MeidasTouch, MoveOn Civic Action and other community organizing groups. High-profile Democrats noted as attending the actual address included Massachusetts Sen. Ed Markey and Arizona Sen. Ruben Gallego, even as House Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries (D-New York) framed the choice as either “silent defiance” at the chamber or not attending and sending a message through “alternate programming” around the Capitol complex. Published Feb. 25, 2026, 12:14 AM EST and updated Feb. 25, 2026, 10:08 AM EST, the article by Jane C. Timm and Adam Edelman fact-checked President Donald Trump’s State of the Union address, which it described as the longest State of the Union address in history Tuesday night. It said Trump touted economic policy and immigration enforcement while condemning Democrats and the previous administration, but also made exaggerated, misleading and false claims about the economy, crime, elections and more. On SNAP, Trump said, “We have lifted 2.4 million Americans — a record — off of food stamps,” but the fact-check said this “needs context”: nearly 42 million Americans rely on SNAP, and around 2.4 million people are expected to lose eligibility because of new work requirements in Trump’s One Big Beautiful Bill, according to the Center on Budget Policy and Priorities; adults ages 55 to 64 and parents whose youngest children are at least 14 must document 80 hours per month of work, education or volunteering or else can receive food stamps only three months in a three-year period, and exemptions for veterans and people experiencing homelessness were eliminated. Trump’s “Warrior Dividend” claim — “Every service member recently received a Warrior Dividend of $1,776” — was labeled false as to the claim that the money came from tariffs; the Defense Department said 1.28 million active-duty service members and 174,000 reserve members got $1,776 each from a supplemental housing fund appropriated in Trump’s domestic spending bill, and acting comptroller Jules W. Hurst III said the dividend was possible through the One Big Beautiful Bill Act. Trump’s claim that Washington, D.C., has “almost no crime anymore” and that crime is at the lowest level ever recorded was called exaggerated; Metropolitan Police Department data showed assaults with a dangerous weapon increased in 2026, though other categories fell, and since Jan. 1 there were nine homicides, 126 assaults with a dangerous weapon and 322 motor vehicle thefts. His claim that “Other presidents tried” but never lowered prescription drug costs was false because President Joe Biden’s 2022 Inflation Reduction Act capped insulin at $35 a month for Medicare, set a $2,000 annual out-of-pocket cap and enabled Medicare negotiation of some drug prices; negotiated prices that took effect Jan. 1 included Eliquis and Imbruvica, while Trump’s voluntary deals with at least 16 drugmakers and TrumpRx (offering cash prices on 43 medications) do not generally change what insured patients pay. Trump’s inflation claim was false: annual inflation when he took office was 2.9%, not a record, inflation fell to 2.3% in April before rising after worldwide tariffs, 2022 hit 8.9%, and the 1980s peak was around 14%. His claim that “More Americans are working today than at any time in the history of our country” was true, though 2025 saw only 584,000 jobs created, the worst year for job creation since 2020 and, excluding recessions, since 2003. Trump’s claimed “more than $18 trillion” in investment commitments was false; the White House list totaled $9.7 trillion and Bloomberg Economics said more than $2.5 trillion was not actually investments, about $3.5 trillion was opaque sovereign pledges, and $3.5 trillion was corporate investments, with $2.9 trillion in data centers and more than $250 billion announced before Trump returned to office. His claim of “no tax on tips, no tax on overtime and no tax on Social Security” got a “needs context” verdict: the law created deductions for tips and overtime with income caps and deduction limits, and Social Security income remains taxed.
