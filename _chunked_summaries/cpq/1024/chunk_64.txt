Israel says creating buffer zone in Lebanon, troops seize new positions - AL-Monitor: The Middle Eastʼs leading independent news source since 2012, 2026-03-03: Israel said on Tuesday it was establishing a buffer zone inside Lebanon and ordered its troops to seize additional positions across the border after several projectiles were fired from Lebanese territory into Israel. Defence Minister Israel Katz said “Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu and I have authorised the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) to advance and take control of additional strategic positions in Lebanon in order to prevent attacks on Israeli border communities,” while military spokesman Brigadier General Effie Defrin said Northern Command had “moved forward, taken control of the dominating terrain, and is creating a buffer” between Israeli residents and threats. The Lebanese army responded by redeploying soldiers from several recently established border positions on Tuesday after the Israeli army’s “escalation”; a Lebanese military source said the troops, “numbering in total eight to nine soldiers at each point,” were pulled back “because of the danger to their safety.” Lebanon had been drawn into the regional war a day earlier after an initial rocket attack on Israel by Hezbollah, which said it wanted to “avenge” the killing of Iranian supreme leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei during the US-Israeli strikes. Israel promptly replied with large-scale strikes on Lebanon, where the government on Monday declared an immediate ban on Hezbollah’s military activities. By Tuesday, the Israeli military said it had struck more than 160 Hezbollah targets “throughout south Lebanon,” including members of the Radwan Force, and said it had killed Hezbollah’s intelligence chief, Hussein Muakalled. Later on Tuesday it said several projectiles were fired from Lebanon toward Israel but a majority were intercepted. Israeli military chief Lieutenant General Eyal Zamir said Israel was “determined to eliminate” Hezbollah until it was disarmed. The army said it would strike Hezbollah assets in Tyre, home to a UNESCO World Heritage site, warning residents of some buildings to leave; AFPTV reported a series of air strikes hitting Beirut’s southern suburbs without prior warning, and the military said it had hit Hezbollah command centres, weapons storage facilities, and satellite communication components in Beirut. Hezbollah said it targeted three Israeli military bases in response. After sirens in northern Israel, an AFP journalist reported a rocket struck a house in the village of Yuval on the Lebanese border, lightly injuring one person by glass shrapnel, according to Magen David Adom. Israel said it was not planning a full-scale ground invasion, but Katz said “all options on table” to stop incoming rocket fire, and the military called the order to seize additional locations “tactical measures” rather than a ground invasion. Israeli forces had previously entered Lebanon in September 2024 after nearly a year of cross-border exchanges launched by Hezbollah in support of Hamas after the outbreak of the war in Gaza; tens of thousands were evacuated on both sides then. Israel says it will not evacuate northern communities again, citing reinforced troop deployments and strengthened air defences along the border, while Lebanese authorities said ongoing strikes had displaced more than 58,000 people. Initial tests on a high-grade Linka Pit sample grading 1.4 per cent tungsten trioxide produced a rougher concentrate assaying 22.9 per cent tungsten trioxide with 63.7 per cent recovery at the coarsest grind size tested, 200 microns. The company said tungsten from deposits such as Linka is used in tungsten carbide cutting tools because of its high melting point, and in defence, aerospace, electronics and manufacturing. Linka hosts high-grade scheelite mineralisation within a classic skarn system in Nevada’s Spencer Hot Springs Mining District, about 7 kilometres south of Highway 50; the skarn formed at the contact between Ordovician limestone and granitic intrusives. Scheelite was described as one of the most common tungsten minerals, together with wolframite, its “heavier cousin.” Viking Metals managing director and chief executive officer Julian Woodcock said: “Achieving a 16-times upgrade through the simplest and cheapest processing method available is a fantastic result,” and said the company was fast-tracking flowsheet development to evaluate low-cost modular solutions and had commenced discussions with processing specialist Mineral Technologies. The 63.7 per cent recovery at 200 microns suggested room for improvement through staged grinding optimisation and cleaner gravity work; much unrecovered tungsten was fine scheelite in the tails, which Viking planned to chase with new flotation test work. QEMSCAN automated mineralogy showed the primary waste mineral was garnet with minimal calcite, which can complicate flowsheets and increase reagent consumption. Technical consultants IMO and Base Met Labs were overseeing the program. Beyond metallurgy, Viking was advancing a 3D geological model, mapping drill targets for the June quarter, and lining up required federal permitting to get rigs turning. Linka is part of Viking’s wider Nevada tungsten grounds acquired late last year; across four historic sites, the broader portfolio had previously produced 123,000 tonnes of ore grading 0.54 per cent tungsten trioxide. The historic Linka mine produced tungsten during WWII and the Korean War era, with reported grades between 0.69 and 0.98 per cent tungsten before government buying programs ended. Tungsten is classified as a critical mineral by the US, UK and Australia because of its role in defence, aerospace, electronics, and manufacturing; roughly 60 per cent of global tungsten consumption goes into tungsten carbide, used in drill bits for mining, oil and gas, and metal cutting tools.
