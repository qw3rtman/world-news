### 1970s-style oil shock loading? Crude may hit $100 if Strait of Hormuz shuts amid Middle East tensions - what it means - The Times of India | 2026-03-02
The article says global oil markets were bracing for supply disruptions after US airstrikes on Iran over the weekend revived fears that shipments through the Strait of Hormuz could be affected, amid what it framed as a “1970s style energy shock” risk from “the war between US-Israel and Iran.” It notes Iran produces “over 3 million barrels of crude per day,” about “3% of worldwide output,” and is “the fourth-largest producer within OPEC,” but its strategic position matters more than its output share. The piece recounts the two 1970s shocks: the 1973–74 Arab OPEC embargo against the United States and other countries that backed Israel during the Yom Kippur War, and the 1979 disruption after the Iranian Revolution. It says a shutdown of the Strait of Hormuz, “located between Oman and Iran,” would be far more severe; Saul Kavonic, head of energy research at MST Marquee, told CNBC it could be “three times the severity of the Arab oil embargo and Iranian revolution in the 1970s,” driving oil “into the triple digits” and LNG back toward the record highs of “2022.” Kpler data cited in the article says “around 13 million barrels per day” passed through the strait in 2025, equal to “roughly 31% of total seaborne oil flows,” and that LNG shipments through it had “nearly come to a standstill.” Reuters reported that an EU naval mission Aspides official said commercial ships received VHF radio warnings from Iran’s Revolutionary Guards saying “no ship is allowed to pass the Strait of Hormuz,” though Tehran had not officially confirmed a shutdown. The article details alternative export routes: Saudi Arabia’s “746-mile pipeline” to a Red Sea terminal with capacity of “up to 5 million barrels per day,” the UAE’s Habshan-Fujairah pipeline carrying “around 1.5 million barrels of crude daily,” and Iraq’s Turkey-to-Mediterranean line, while Kuwait, Qatar and Bahrain have no alternatives. It says Iran’s own crude output has risen to “roughly 3.3 million barrels per day,” up from “under 2 million barrels per day in 2020,” and that “nearly 90%” of its exports go to China, with Kharg Island the main shipping and storage hub, capable of holding “tens of millions of barrels” and handling “more than 2 million barrels per day.” The worst case discussed was a loss of as much as “2 million barrels per day” of Iranian exports, regional infrastructure strikes, or a full Hormuz closure; Andy Lipow called “an attack on Saudi oil infrastructure followed by a complete closure of the Strait of Hormuz” the most severe scenario and put its probability at “around 33 per cent.” Vandana Hari of Vanda Insights warned of “a full-scale military conflict between the U.S. and Iran,” Bob McNally of Rapidan Energy Group called it “a very serious development,” and the piece says if Iran “feels they face an existential threat,” it cannot be ruled out that it tries to block the strait. ### Crude Will Top $90 a Barrel Due to Straits of Hormuz Disruption, Say Analysts | 2026-03-02
