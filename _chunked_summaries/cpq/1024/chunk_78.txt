### Iran spent years fostering proxies in Iraq. Now, many aren’t eager to join the war | Reuters | 2026-03-06 — Reuters reported from BAGHDAD that Iran’s long-built Iraqi proxy network was largely staying out of the U.S.-Israel war on the Islamic Republic that began a week earlier, even though Tehran had spent decades and billions of dollars cultivating Shi’ite paramilitaries under the Islamic Revolutionary Guards Corps and its Quds Force. The article centered on “A.J.,” a commander in a pro-Iranian paramilitary group from Shi’ite-majority southern Iraq, who said he had been awaiting orders from Tehran after the war began but none had come; Reuters used his initials because he feared Israeli or U.S. strikes. A.J. said most of the dozens of Iraqi Shi’ite paramilitaries were no longer reliable, and that if orders came they might only go to “two or three” of them. The piece said there had been no mass mobilization inside Iraq and that many claimed attacks under the banner “Islamic Resistance of Iraq” caused no significant damage and in some cases could not be verified. Kurdish officials said most missile and drone attacks came directly from Iran, though explosions did rock Erbil, the Kurdish stronghold hosting a U.S. base. The article detailed the erosion of Iran’s “Axis of Resistance” through targeted killings, loss of secure bases, and commanders’ shift into Iraqi politics and business; Gareth Stansfield of Exeter University said “The idea that the factions are under the thumb of Iran is not the case anymore,” adding militia leaders do not want sanctions and want Western healthcare and children educated abroad, a trend he said accelerated after the “12-day war” between Israel and Iran last June. Iraqi security officials said the proxies lacked modern weapons and that Tehran had sent no new weapons to A.J.’s group since last year’s fighting with Israel, leaving them with outmoded arms because moving weapons would be “too risky.” The story traced major milestones: the 2003 U.S. invasion of Iraq and subsequent embedding of Shi’ite militants in state institutions; the rise of ISIS in 2014; the 2017 defeat of ISIS and the militias’ entrance into parliament and domination of the Popular Mobilization Forces, a “150,000-strong” umbrella receiving “an annual budget of over $3 billion”; the 2019 attack on the U.S. embassy in Baghdad, after which President Donald Trump ordered the early 2020 killing of Qassem Soleimani, head of the Quds Force. A.J. said Soleimani’s replacement, Esmail Ghaani, lacked his predecessor’s stature and that there was “no comparison.” After Soleimani, Hezbollah in Lebanon coordinated the axis until Hezbollah’s leader Hassan Nasrallah was assassinated in September 2024, which A.J. called a moment when “Leaders like this come along only once”; Mustafa Fahs said “Everything changed after Nasrallah was killed,” partly because Hezbollah’s grip on Beirut airport and Lebanese state institutions weakened. The article described Syria as the crucial land route for Iran’s axis, noting Iranian proxies deployed there in 2011 to save Bashar al-Assad, who was ousted in December 2024 and replaced in 2025 by Ahmed al-Sharaa, a former Al Qaeda fighter. A.J. said Damascus was “the key for coordinating the axis of resistance.” The article also said senior Iraqi militia figures such as Qais al-Khazali and Shibl al-Zaidi had softened anti-American rhetoric, privately aligned with the U.S. on Iraq’s new prime minister, and reached out to Western officials; Khazali, who had once threatened U.S. interests and whose men killed U.S. troops in 2007, issued only a statement on X condemning the U.S. and telling supporters to “wear black,” while protesters in Baghdad on March 1 tried and failed to reach the Green Zone and U.S. embassy. One protester shouted that absent leaders were “cowards.” The article identified at least six Iran-backed militants killed in airstrikes since the war began, including a Kataib Hezbollah fighter and drone specialist mourned by A.J.
