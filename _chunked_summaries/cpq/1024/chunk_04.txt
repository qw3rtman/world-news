Trump to travel to China next month, with US tariffs in focus | Reuters | 2026-02-20: Washington said U.S. President Donald Trump will travel to China from March 31 to April 2, 2026, for a highly anticipated meeting with Chinese President Xi Jinping, his first trip to China since 2017 and the leaders’ first in-person talks since their October meeting in South Korea, where they agreed on a trade truce. The trip was confirmed by a White House official on Friday, just as the U.S. Supreme Court struck down many of Trump’s tariffs, dealing him a “stinging defeat” and raising questions for U.S.-China relations. The ruling said Trump had overstepped under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act, or IEEPA, on 20-percent tariffs on China’s U.S.-bound exports tied to national emergencies involving fentanyl distribution and trade imbalances; Section 301 and Section 232 duties remain in place. Trump said he would impose a new 10% global tariff for 150 days, and in the Japan Times version he later said he would raise the global 10% tariff to 15%. The administration had trimmed tariffs on Chinese goods in exchange for Beijing cracking down on the illicit fentanyl trade and pausing export restrictions on critical minerals. Taiwan is expected to figure into the talks: Xi raised U.S. arms sales to Taiwan during a February call, after the October meeting largely sidestepped the issue, and Washington’s largest-ever arms sale approval to Taiwan in December 2025 included $11.1 billion in weapons. China views Taiwan as its own territory, Taipei rejects that, and the U.S. is bound by law to provide Taiwan with the means to defend itself; Beijing has not confirmed the China trip and the Chinese embassy in Washington declined comment. Trump told foreign leaders visiting Washington, “That’s going to be a wild one,” and, “We have to put on the biggest display you’ve ever had in the history of China.” Xi also said he would consider further increasing soybean purchases, important because struggling U.S. farmers are a major political constituency for Trump and China is the top soybean consumer. Scott Kennedy of the Center for Strategic and International Studies said the tariff defeat likely “cements his weakness in their eyes,” while Martin Chorzempa of the Peterson Institute of International Economics said the ruling could indirectly increase pressure on Beijing if effective tariff rates on Southeast Asia fall more than those on China. Trump’s trade war, begun after his second term started in January 2025, has alienated other trading partners, including allies, while critics said broad tariffs insulated Beijing and reduced incentives to move supply chains out of China.
