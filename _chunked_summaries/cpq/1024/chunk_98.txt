Oil prices surged on Thursday, with the Times of India’s “Oil price today (March 12, 2026): Crude jumps after tanker strikes in Iraq; supply fears persist” dated “2026-03-12” reporting Brent Crude up 9.3% to “$100.50 per barrel” and West Texas Intermediate up 8.8% to “$94.92 at around 0305 GMT,” after earlier trading at about “0100 GMT” showed Brent at “$98.60 per barrel” and WTI at “$92.96.” The article said fresh strikes on energy supplies and infrastructure in the Middle East, plus attacks on oil tankers, intensified fears of major disruptions and helped push Brent briefly above “$120 per barrel” earlier in the week before easing to around “$102 on Monday.” It said the conflict had entered its “13th consecutive day on Thursday,” with videos showing vessels engulfed in flames and Iraqi media reports attributing the strikes to Iran. The piece emphasized that strategic reserves were being tapped to calm markets: the United States Department of Energy said on Wednesday it would release “172 million barrels of crude oil” from the Strategic Petroleum Reserve starting “next week,” taking “approximately 120 days to deliver based on planned discharge rates,” while President Donald Trump, in an interview with Local 12 television in Cincinnati, Ohio, said, “Right now we'll reduce it a little bit and that brings the prices down.” The International Energy Agency also said its member countries had agreed to release “400 million barrels of oil” from emergency reserves, described as the largest coordinated release ever and larger than the “182.7 million barrels” released in 2022 after Russia’s invasion of Ukraine; Japan said it could begin releasing oil “as early as Monday,” and Germany announced plans without a timeline. The article linked the price spike to the Iran-Israel-US war and said the Strait of Hormuz, which normally carries “Nearly 20% of the world’s oil trade,” had been effectively shut down, severely disrupting tanker traffic through the key route connecting the Persian Gulf with the Gulf of Oman. ### CNN Business’s “Oil soars above $100 after Iran says Strait of Hormuz will remain shut,” by John Liu and Hanna Ziady, dated “PUBLISHED Mar 12, 2026, 12:40 AM ET” and “Updated Mar 12, 2026, 10:50 AM ET,” said Brent crude hovered around “$100 a barrel” after Iran’s new leader purportedly said the Strait of Hormuz would remain shut “as a tool of pressure,” with the comment attributed on state TV to Mojtaba Khamenei, son of former leader Ali Khamenei, killed by an Israeli strike at the start of the war. CNN said Brent climbed “9% to trade just above $100 a barrel,” while WTI rose by a similar margin to “above $95 a barrel.” It quoted the IEA warning that if ships did not resume transit, supply would shrink further through a choke point ordinarily carrying “around a fifth of daily global oil production,” and said “The war in the Middle East is creating the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market.” The same report said 32 of the world’s biggest economies, including the United States, had agreed Wednesday to add “400 million barrels of oil” to the global market, but that a near-blockade of Hormuz was choking off “around 15 million barrels of crude oil and 5 million barrels of other oil products” every day, meaning the 400 million barrels would be absorbed in “just 26 days.” It also said Iran had ramped up attacks on oil tankers, striking “two foreign oil tankers in Iraqi waters Thursday,” while the United Kingdom’s maritime agency said another vessel in the Persian Gulf had been struck, “the sixth ship to come under attack in the last two days.” The IEA estimated by “March 10” that Gulf-country output cuts amounted to “at least 10 million barrels a day” of crude and other products, naming Saudi Arabia, Iraq, the United Arab Emirates, Kuwait, Qatar, Bahrain and Iran as the seven Gulf countries that had substantially reduced production. ### Reuters’ “Oil prices surge as Iran escalates tanker attacks, shares fall,” dated “March 12” and datelined BOSTON/LONDON, said global shares fell as attacks on oil tankers in the Gulf and Iranian warnings shattered hopes of quick de-escalation, pushing oil prices to around $100 a barrel and stoking inflation concerns. It said the Dow Jones Industrial Average and S&P 500 each dropped about “1.5%,” the Nasdaq Composite lost “1.8%,” the STOXX 600 slipped “0.6%,” and the MSCI All-World index fell “1.5%.” Brent crude futures settled at “$100.46 a barrel, up $8.48, or 9.2%,” after touching “$101.60,” while U.S. West Texas Intermediate settled at “$95.70, up $8.48, or 9.7%,” both highest since August 2022. The article said new Supreme Leader Ayatollah Mojtaba Khamenei, in his first remarks since succeeding his slain father, vowed to avenge the blood of martyrs, “keep the Strait of Hormuz closed” and attack U.S. bases, while earlier two fuel tankers in Iraqi waters were struck by explosive-laden Iranian boats and an Iraqi official said its oil ports “have completely stopped operations.” Iran had stepped up attacks on merchant ships in Hormuz, increasing the number of ships struck since fighting began to “at least 16,” and Tehran warned the world to get ready for “oil at $200 a barrel,” though U.S. Energy Secretary Chris Wright said global oil prices were unlikely to hit that level. Reuters also tied market moves to U.S. CPI data showing a “0.3%” February rise after “0.2%” in January, and to bond markets, where 10-year Treasury yields rose “5.5 basis points to 4.261%” and two-year yields hit a six-month high; Morgan Stanley fell “4%,” Blackstone “4.7%,” and BlackRock “2.9%,” while nearly “40%” of polled economists expected either one or no Fed rate cuts in 2026. The euro slipped “0.45% to $1.1515,” the dollar strengthened “0.28%” versus the yen and more than “1.5%” against a basket, and gold fell about “1.7% to $5,088 an ounce.” ### The Reuters piece “Iran’s new supreme leader vows to keep Hormuz shut, Netanyahu issues threat,” dated “March 12 (Reuters)” from DUBAI/BEIRUT, said Mojtaba Khamenei vowed to keep the Strait of Hormuz shut as leverage against the United States and Israel, urged neighbors to shut U.S. bases, and said Iran would continue targeting them, while Benjamin Netanyahu used his first press conference since the war began to issue a veiled threat to kill Khamenei and defend the assault on Iran.
