Thanks to Trump, Xi Has Time on His Side With Taiwan, February 25, 2026: Ali Wyne argues that the short-term likelihood of a Chinese attack on Taiwan has diminished, not because Beijing is safer to invade but because President Donald Trump may help Xi Jinping extend China’s influence without war. The piece recalls Marco Rubio’s January 2025 confirmation hearing to become U.S. secretary of state, when Rubio said that “unless something dramatic changes” in Asia’s military balance, China would attempt to invade Taiwan before the end of the decade, and the House Select Committee on the Chinese Communist Party’s May warning that “the clock is ticking to stop a war in the Indo-Pacific—and this Congress may be America’s last full chance to do it.” The article says China’s invasion would be risky because the PLA is in turmoil even as modernization accelerates, while the U.S. and Taiwan are fielding new capabilities and deepening security cooperation, with Washington moving to deploy missile and unmanned systems to the Philippines and nuclear-powered submarines to Australia; any “victory” could be Pyrrhic, with possible nuclear escalation and a protracted war. But Xi may see political trends in Washington and Taipei as favorable: Trump has shown little concern for Taiwan’s security, despite authorizing several high-profile arms sales, and has repeatedly framed Taiwan around chips, saying in July 2024 that Taiwan had stolen the U.S. semiconductor industry and “doesn’t give us anything,” a line he repeated after the Supreme Court’s tariff ruling on Friday; in a January interview he said Xi considers Taiwan “a part of China,” and in March 2025 TSMC announced a $100 billion increase in U.S. investment, after which Trump said an invasion would be “a catastrophic event” but that TSMC’s U.S. business would be safe. Xi also has a “stick” in rare-earths leverage, demonstrated in last year’s trade standoff, and a “carrot” in Trump’s desire to be a peacemaker, which Xi appealed to at their South Korea meeting in October 2025, telling Trump, “Mr. President, you care a lot about world peace, and you are very enthusiastic about settling various regional hot spot issues.” The article says future U.S. arms sales to Taiwan may have been discussed, signaling willingness to negotiate what officials had effectively taken off the table with the second of the “six assurances” from 1982. Beijing also sees favorable politics in Taipei: Chinese officials regard President Lai Ching-te as more separatist-minded than Tsai Ing-wen, yet the legislature is divided, with the Kuomintang (KMT) and the Taiwan People’s Party controlling 60 of 113 seats and the DPP 51. The KMT leader Cheng Li-wun, a former DPP member, says Taiwan cannot depend on a U.S. that has elected Trump twice, warned it “must not become a sacrifice or Trump’s bargaining chip” or “become another Ukraine,” said Taiwan would not be “involved in an internecine struggle” and suggested detente could “bring about cooperation between the U.S. and China”; she also cites Trump’s demand that Taiwan spend 10 percent of GDP on defense, versus Lai’s hope for 5 percent by 2030, and Howard Lutnick’s goal of onshoring 40 percent of Taipei’s semiconductor production by the end of Trump’s second term. The article says Cheng’s stance aligns with polls showing growing Taiwanese misgivings about U.S. reliability. It concludes that China’s power is growing militarily, technologically, and diplomatically, that Washington’s “America first” foreign policy is alienating allies in Europe and sowing doubt in Asia, and that Beijing should not get in Trump’s way while he erodes the U.S. diplomatic network; still, no one in Washington should relax, because China could still provoke short of invasion by restricting maritime access, inexperienced PLA leaders might misjudge, crowded seas and skies raise clash risks, and a ham-fisted Beijing response could escalate. Potential invasion triggers include Taiwan moving toward independence or an explicit U.S. defense commitment, and Xi may believe the next three years offer the best reunification window.
