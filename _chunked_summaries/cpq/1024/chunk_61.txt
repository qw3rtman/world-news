### After the strike: The danger of war in Iran | Brookings — 2026-03-03. Brookings experts weighed in after the February 28, 2026 U.S.-Israeli joint military operation against Iran, which killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei and kicked off a war whose central question is whether regime change is possible, sustainable, or even desirable. Suzanne Maloney’s “Khamenei raft” compared Khamenei’s death in an Israeli airstrike on his compound to the iconic February 1979 “Shah raft” (“the Shah is gone”) headline after Mohammad Reza Pahlavi’s departure, arguing the strike was momentous but likely not enough to topple the Islamic Republic because deep networks and institutions remain intact; she noted Tehran’s survival strategy is to escalate strikes on neighbors’ energy and economic infrastructure to raise the cost to Washington and the global economy, after the attrition of proxies and the nuclear program over the previous “18 months” and an internal uprising in January. Itamar Rabinovich’s “Tehran’s radical turn” said the decapitation strike killed Khamenei, several family members, and other officials but did not trigger an immediate popular revolt; he described a radical core taking charge, attacks on Arab neighbors, likely needing more than the initial phase “(five days to several weeks)” for regime change, and warned U.S.-Israeli harmony could break if Trump seeks a swift end while Netanyahu wants broader war aims. Mara Karlin’s “High-end war, low-cost drones” said the U.S. and Israeli militaries had hit “more than a thousand Iranian targets in two days,” while Iranian missiles and drones had attacked “at least nine different countries” by Monday morning, showing low-cost drones can evade air defenses. Vanda Felbab-Brown’s “The limits of leadership targeting” stressed that even killing top IRGC leaders won’t make the regime “melt away,” because the IRGC and armed branches control sprawling religious, military, and economic assets; she warned that a future transitional election may not yield democracy and mentioned Kurdish and Baluch unrest and Islamist militancy. Stephanie T. Williams’s “Flying blind” called the war “volitional and illegal,” said the U.S. has had no diplomatic presence in Iran for nearly “50 years,” noted Iran’s population of “92 million,” and warned of implosion, fragmentation, possible need for ground troops, a Persian Gulf armada, and geopolitical blowback that could embolden Moscow and Beijing. Sharan Grewal’s “The most likely scenario is still a deal, not regime change” argued Trump’s best option is a “Venezuela scenario” of a deal with whoever replaces Khamenei, perhaps a modified nuclear deal plus oil concessions; he said “three U.S. troops already killed over the weekend,” followed by “a friendly fire downing of three jets Monday morning,” would make Trump more likely to seek a bargain, while Iran was trying to create diplomatic pressure. Steven Heydemann’s “The regime change gamble” said the U.S.-Israeli war aimed at reversing the effects of the “2003” invasion of Iraq and the “1979” انقلاب, but regime change could instead produce renewed repression, a worse replacement, or prolonged instability. Dafna H. Rand’s “Civilian tools, not bombs” argued the U.S. should expand civilian statecraft such as the Near East Regional Democracy Program, created in “2009” and having invested nearly “$600 million,” plus internet freedom tools; she cited the Green Movement, Dey protests, and Women, Life, Freedom, noted “roughly one in four Iranians” used a U.S.-funded VPN during the 2021-2022 protests, and urged more multilateral support. In the regional section, Aslı Aydıntaşbaş’s “After Iran, Turkey and Israel face a reckoning” said Turkey and Israel are heading toward long-term collision as Erdoğan blamed Netanyahu and called the strikes “provocations,” while Turkey fears refugees, trade and energy disruption, and Kurdish autonomy in Iran; Kemal Kirişci’s “Turkey is holding its breath” recalled that after 1979 about “1.5 million Iranians” transited Turkey in the 1980s and early 1990s, contrasted that with Turkey’s hosting of “more than 2.3 million” Syrian refugees, and warned inflation, fragile currency, and higher energy prices could worsen Turkey’s economy. Hady Amr’s “After the attack on Iran, questions at home” asked how Iran’s leadership, Israel’s hegemony, Arab alignment, Palestinian issues, U.S. constitutional politics, and oil prices would evolve, while Constanze Stelzenmüller’s “Will Europe enter the fray?” noted European unease, the E-3 statement—France, Germany, United Kingdom—on Sunday night promising “necessary and proportionate defensive action,” French and British bases in the region, and German Chancellor Friedrich Merz’s trip to Washington. Pavel K. Baev’s “For Russia, a major geopolitical setback with a sharp personal twist” said Moscow tried to deter escalation with naval drills but was shocked by the massive strike and Khamenei’s elimination; Putin condemned the “assassination” as a “cynical violation,” amid his own security paranoia. Steven Pifer’s “A limited response from Moscow” said the Kremlin condemned the strikes as an “unprovoked act of armed aggression,” welcomed higher oil prices, but would likely do little despite the “Treaty on Comprehensive Strategic Partnership” that entered into force in October 2025. Ryan Hass’s “The view from China and Taiwan” said Trump had told Xi Jinping in 2017 over dessert that U.S. forces had struck Syria, and argued China sees Iran as a mid-tier partner, will work to restore oil flows, and Taiwan wants the U.S. quickly refocused on deterring China. Tanvi Madan’s “A consequential crisis for India” highlighted India’s West Asia dependence: almost “9 million Indian citizens,” nearly half its oil imports, over two-thirds of LNG imports, more than one-third of its “$135 billion” remittances, and “15%” of merchandise exports; New Delhi has not condemned the killings and has instead condemned strikes on Bahrain, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE. Lynn Kuok’s “Normalizing the use of force: Legal precedents and fallout for Southeast Asia” said the strikes risk normalizing unilateral force under the U.N. Charter, noted ASEAN had not spoken, Malaysia’s strongest rebuke, and warned about Strait of Hormuz disruptions, higher energy prices, the IRGC claim to have hit a U.S. fuel vessel, the South China Sea’s “up to one-third of global maritime commerce” and “over 30%” of crude trade, plus a “Ghost Fleet” of around “60” dark-fleet tankers off Malaysia. In “Domestic and economic implications,” Michael E. O'Hanlon and Caitlin Talmadge reviewed airpower’s mixed record from Kosovo in “1999,” Afghanistan in “2001,” Iraq in “2003,” Libya in “2011,” the “2014 through 2019” anti-ISIS campaign, and Gaza, arguing regime change in Iran is a very high bar.
