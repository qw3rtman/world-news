Exclusive: Hegseth gives Anthropic until Friday to back down on AI safeguards | 2026-02-24: Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth told Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei in a tense Tuesday meeting at the Pentagon that he had until “Friday evening” to give the military unfettered access to Anthropic’s AI model Claude or face harsh penalties; the Pentagon threatened either to cut ties and declare Anthropic a “supply chain risk” or to invoke the Defense Production Act to force the company to tailor its model to the military’s needs. Claude was described as the only AI model currently used for the military’s most sensitive work and, in the classified setting, the only model then used inside those systems; Anthropic said it would adapt usage policies but would not allow Claude to be used for the mass surveillance of Americans or for weapons that fire without human involvement. Hegseth said he would not let any company dictate the terms under which the Pentagon makes operational decisions or object to individual use cases. The meeting was described by one senior Defense official as “not warm and fuzzy at all,” by another source as “cordial” with no raised voices, and Hegseth praised Claude to Amodei. He specifically cited the Pentagon’s claim that Anthropic raised concerns to partner Palantir over use of Claude during the Maduro raid; Amodei denied Anthropic raised such concerns beyond standard operating conversations. In the room with Hegseth were Deputy Secretary Steve Feinberg, Under Secretary for Research and Engineering Emil Michael, Under Secretary for Acquisition and Sustainment Michael Duffey, chief spokesperson Sean Parnell, and general counsel Earl Matthews. Anthropic responded with conciliatory language: “We continued good-faith conversations about our usage policy to ensure Anthropic can continue to support the government’s national security mission in line with what our models can reliably and responsibly do,” and said Dario thanked the Secretary for his service. The Defense Production Act, rarely used adversarially, was described as giving the president authority to compel private companies to accept and prioritize contracts for national defense and had been used during the COVID-19 pandemic to increase vaccine and ventilator production. The Pentagon was also considering severing its contract and declaring Anthropic a supply chain risk, which would require other companies working with the Pentagon to certify that Claude is not used in their workflows; one defense consultant said Anthropic could argue in court that Claude is custom-built software tailored to sensitive government uses, not a commercially available product for which the DPA can expedite production. Cutting ties would require a replacement for Claude, which was also used for bureaucratic military functions and in the Venezuela operation through Anthropic’s partnership with Palantir. xAI’s Grok had recently signed a contract to enter classified settings, though it was unclear whether it could fully replace Claude; Gemini was seen as a possible replacement if Google agreed to let the Pentagon use it for “all lawful purposes,” the same terms Anthropic rejected, and the Pentagon was speeding up conversations with OpenAI and Google about moving their models from unclassified to classified systems.
