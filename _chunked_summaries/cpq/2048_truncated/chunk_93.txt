### White House says US Navy has not escorted tanker through Strait of Hormuz - AL-Monitor: The Middle Eastʼs leading independent news source since 2012 | 2026-03-10
On 2026-03-10, the White House said the United States has not escorted any oil tanker through the Strait of Hormuz after Energy Secretary Chris Wright’s social media account posted and then deleted a claim that the US Navy had done so; White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt said, “I can confirm that the US Navy has not escorted a tanker or a vessel at this time, though of course that’s an option,” while Iran’s Revolutionary Guards called Wright’s claim a “pure falsehood” and said no US Navy vessel had “dared” approach the strait. An Energy Department spokesperson said “a video clip was deleted from Secretary Wright’s official X account after it was determined to be incorrectly captioned by Department of Energy staff.” The deleted post had said the “US Navy successfully escorted an oil tanker through the Strait of Hormuz to ensure oil remains flowing to global markets,” and the narrative shift moved oil markets sharply, with oil prices dropping after the initial post and then paring back losses after the deletion. The article said at least 10 oil tankers in or near the Strait of Hormuz were struck, targeted or reported attacked between March 1 and 10 according to data compiled by the UK Maritime Trade Organisation (UKMTO), the International Maritime Organization (IMO), and Iranian authorities. No US ships were confirmed to have escorted oil tankers in the waterway since the war was launched on February 28, and Wright’s comments were described as the first time a US official said this had occurred. Nearly a fifth of global oil production passes through the strait, Tehran vowed on Tuesday that none would be exported from the Gulf while the war is ongoing, and since March 2 more than 20 commercial vessels were detected crossing the strait by AFP analysis of Marine Traffic data, some with transponders switched off to conceal their position and only reappearing once safely out of the area. Before the war, a daily average of 138 ships transited the Strait of Hormuz. President Donald Trump’s administration had offered reinsurance to shipping companies and the US Navy’s services to escort tankers to reassure markets, but crude still fluctuated sharply, jumping 30 percent on Monday to nearly $120 per barrel before retreating after Trump hinted the war may end soon, even as his defense secretary vowed a day later to carry out the “most intense day of strikes inside Iran.” The war had already seen strikes on oil depots in Iran and attacks on energy infrastructure in wealthy Gulf countries. ###

### Exclusive / Trump’s China trip planning still scattershot, sources say | 2026-03-10
In Semafor’s “Exclusive / Trump’s China trip planning still scattershot, sources say,” by Andy Browne and Liz Hoffman and dated Mar 9, 2026, 9:33pm EDT, planning for US President Donald Trump’s state visit to China remained “scattershot” with barely three weeks to go before the March 31-April 2 visit to Beijing, and there was no final list of officials and executives accompanying him. The trip was expected to build on a trade truce agreed to in October, when Trump last met Chinese leader Xi Jinping, and could be the first of four potential meetings between the pair this year. The White House and Trump were focused on the US-Israel war in Iran and, before that, the operation to oust Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro, and the article said the lack of particulars reflected the gulf between the countries and the difference between the Trump administration’s last-minute style and the careful planning of Chinese officials. The US Commerce Department and Treasury Department are jointly handling arrangements, but officials had not decided whether to include a business delegation, which is often a centerpiece of state visits; the article contrasted this with German Chancellor Friedrich Merz bringing 30 executives and British Prime Minister Keir Starmer bringing an entourage of almost 60 spanning business, sports, and culture. At least one Wall Street firm had been in talks with the Trump administration about joining a private-sector delegation or convening on the sidelines, and there had been discussion of a CEO dinner in Beijing, but outreach was spotty and the guest list not finalized. A White House official said a list of invited companies had not been compiled but that “no disagreement internally” existed over inviting CEOs and that considerations were underway on what companies and sectors to prioritize. The two US sources with knowledge of the trip’s planning said officials were instead focused on “managed trade,” a concept touted by US Trade Representative Jamieson Greer in which US and Chinese politicians negotiate purchases in a barter-like arrangement, deciding on products, prices, tariffs, delivery schedules, and other details through bureaucratic channels. China had offered proposals for a large fund, possibly involving sovereign wealth, to invest in American companies in batteries and EVs, but US officials had not engaged; one source said Chinese operational control over any such fund would be a non-starter. Scott Kennedy, trustee chair in Chinese business and economics at the Center for Strategic and Economic Studies and a regular at Track II bilateral meetings, said China could aim for “over a trillion dollars” in combined value through the fund and big-ticket Chinese purchases such as Boeing aircraft, soybeans, and LNG, and that one benchmark was Japan’s promised $550 billion of investment in the US, which China would want to top. David Meale, head of Eurasia Group’s China practice and a former deputy chief of mission at the US embassy in Beijing, said the US had been “sluggish in doing its part to nail down deliverables,” and Ryan Hass, a Brookings Institution strategy advisor from the Obama and first Trump administrations, said events in Iran and time pressures “seem to be squeezing the level of ambition for this state visit.” In an “Andy’s view” section, Browne warned the disorganization would create anxiety among working-level officials in Beijing and also among American executives in the auto, agriculture, aviation, and finance industries. In “Room for Disagreement,” Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi said on Sunday in Beijing that he hoped 2026 would be a “landmark year” for US-China relations and urged both sides to “foster a suitable atmosphere, manage existing differences, and eliminate unnecessary distractions”; the story also said China had leverage through rare earths while the US controls much of the technology China needs for high-tech manufacturing, leaving both sides with “a loaded gun at the table,” though neither wants to use it because Trump wants Xi Jinping’s embrace and Xi needs time to deal with serious domestic economic problems. Notable side items said Trump would not leave Beijing during his visit due to security concerns, according to the South China Morning Post, and Politico’s Phelim Kine wrote that Beijing appeared to prioritize summit success over concerns about US military operations targeting Chinese allies Venezuela and Iran. ###
