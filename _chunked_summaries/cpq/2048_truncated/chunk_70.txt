### IRGC says Iran in ‘complete control’ of Strait of Hormuz amid Trump threats | Oil and Gas News | Al Jazeera — 2026-03-04. Iran’s Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) said it maintained “full control” of the Strait of Hormuz, with IRGC Navy official Mohammad Akbarzadeh stating on Wednesday that “Currently, the Strait of Hormuz is under the complete control of the Islamic Republic’s Navy,” via Iran’s Fars news agency. The strait carries about a fifth of the world’s oil, and Al Jazeera’s Tohid Asadi reporting from Tehran said the IRGC claimed it was “impossible for any ships to pass through” and that more than 10 oil tankers were targeted. Reuters reported that in a rare voyage the tanker Pola sailed through the strait en route to a United Arab Emirates port to load crude, switching off its AIS tracker late on Monday as it approached the strait and reappearing Tuesday off Abu Dhabi. Clarksons Research estimated about 3,200 ships, or about 4 percent of global ship tonnage, were idle in the Gulf, including about 1,230 likely operating only within the Gulf, while about 500 ships, or 1 percent of global tonnage, were “waiting” outside the Gulf in ports off the UAE and Oman, according to The Associated Press. Amid the disruption, Brent crude rose to more than $82 a barrel, up more than 13 percent since the start of the conflict and the highest since July 2024. Donald Trump posted on Truth Social on Tuesday that the US Navy would begin escorting tankers through the Strait of Hormuz “as soon as possible,” adding “No matter what, the United States will ensure the FREE FLOW of ENERGY to the WORLD,” and that “The United States’ ECONOMIC and MILITARY MIGHT is the GREATEST ON EARTH — More actions to come.” Pakistan’s Ministry of Energy said on Wednesday it asked Saudi Arabia to route oil supplies through the Red Sea port of Yanbu after Hormuz shipping was disrupted; it said Saudi Arabian sources had assured security of supplies through Yanbu and that one vessel had been arranged to sail there to lift crude for Pakistan, with Riyadh reaffirming support for Pakistan’s emergency energy needs and Petroleum Minister Ali Pervaiz Malik saying most Pakistani energy imports transit Hormuz. ### The Iran war has effectively closed the Strait of Hormuz : NPR — 2026-03-04. NPR reported that the Iran war has effectively closed the Strait of Hormuz, a vital waterway through which about 20% of the world’s crude oil and natural gas typically passes and about 20 million barrels of oil per day move. Kevin Book, co-founder of Clearview Energy Partners, said the situation was “about as wrong as things could go” for global oil markets at any single point of failure. Iran achieved the closure not with a naval blockade but with cheap drones, as traffic through the normally busy strait faded in the first few days of the conflict after Iran declared the strait closed and attacked some ships that tried to use the route. Global crude prices rose more than 10% since the U.S. and Israel attacked Iran, while natural gas prices in Europe and Asia also rose sharply. Recent attacks hit oil and gas infrastructure in nearby Saudi Arabia, Qatar and the UAE, raising concerns that damage to infrastructure could outlast the closure; Iraq, lacking export space for its oil, shut down production in some of its largest oil fields. Helima Croft of RBC Capital Markets called it “the biggest energy crisis since the oil embargo in the 1970s,” and said it was an “insurance-driven shutdown” because insurers would not underwrite ships and companies would not risk passage through the “very narrow S-curve” without coverage. She said Iran had not used mines or anti-ship missiles; “All [Iran] had to do was several drone strikes in the vicinity of the Strait of Hormuz,” which caught the White House off guard. Trump announced on Tuesday that the US government would provide naval escorts, as in the 1980s “tanker war” of the Iran-Iraq conflict, and that the U.S. Development Finance Corporation (DFC), established during the first Trump administration, would provide reasonably priced “political risk insurance” to all shipping lines operating in the Gulf; the agency said it was “ready to mobilize” its products in the Middle East.
