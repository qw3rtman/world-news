It says some files have not been made public despite a law mandating release, including what appear to be "more than 50 pages" of FBI interviews and notes from conversations with a woman who accused Trump of sexual abuse decades ago when she was a minor. NPR reviewed serial numbers across the Epstein database, FBI case records, emails, and discovery logs from the latest tranche of documents published at the end of January, and concluded dozens of pages appear to have been catalogued by DOJ but not shared publicly. DOJ spokeswoman Natalie Baldassarre said unpublished documents are "privileged," "duplicates" or relate to "an ongoing federal investigation." After NPR’s report, House Oversight Committee ranking member Rep. Robert Garcia, D-Calif., said he reviewed unredacted evidence logs and that DOJ appeared to have illegally withheld FBI interviews with "this survivor who accused President Trump of heinous crimes," and Democrats on the House Oversight Committee said they would open a parallel investigation. The article identifies Ghislaine Maxwell, who is "serving a 20-year prison sentence for sex trafficking" and is seeking clemency from Trump, as a related figure; it also says some scrubbed files concern a separate woman who was a key witness in Maxwell’s trial. Some documents were briefly taken down and restored, while others remain hidden, compared against the initial dataset from Jan. 30. White House spokeswoman Abigail Jackson said Trump "has done more for Epstein's victims than anyone before him" and had been "totally exonerated on anything relating to Epstein," citing his release of thousands of pages, cooperation with the House Oversight Committee subpoena request, and signing the "Epstein Files Transparency Act." She also said Democrats Hakeem Jeffries and Stacey Plaskett had yet to explain why they solicited money and meetings from Epstein after he was a convicted sex offender. In the first section, NPR says the FBI internally circulated Epstein-related allegations mentioning Trump in late July and early August 2025, collected from the FBI’s National Threat Operations Center, and agents marked most accusations unverifiable or not credible. One lead was sent to the FBI’s Washington office to set up an interview with the accuser. The first woman said that around 1983, when she was around 13, Epstein introduced her to Trump, who “subsequently forced her head down to his exposed penis which she subsequently bit”; in response, Trump “punched her in the head and kicked her out.” NPR says this allegation appears in more than 3 million pages released in recent months only in copies of the FBI list of claims and a DOJ slideshow. The FBI interviewed this accuser four times, according to a “Serial Report” and Maxwell discovery material; only the first interview, conducted July 24, 2019, is public and it does not mention Trump. Of 15 documents listed for her in Maxwell discovery material, only seven are in the Epstein database, leaving 53 pages of interview documents and notes apparently missing. In that first interview, she discussed Epstein abuse and showed a cropped photo of Epstein; her attorney said it was cropped because she was “concerned about implicating additional individuals, and specifically any that were well known, due to fear of retaliation,” and FBI agents noted it was a “widely distributed photograph” of Epstein with Trump. NPR notes a December 2019 lawsuit filing identified the woman as “Jane Doe 4,” though she did not mention Trump there, and she voluntarily dismissed claims against Epstein’s estate in December 2021. The article also says an FBI note from July 22, 2025 stated Trump’s name was in the larger case files and that “one identified victim claimed abuse by Trump but ultimately refused to cooperate.” The second woman, appearing in Maxwell discovery files as Testifying Witness 3500, said in her first of six FBI interviews between September 2019 and September 2021 that Epstein took her to Trump’s Mar-a-Lago Club to meet him when she was around 13 and attending the Interlochen Center for the Arts; the report quotes Epstein as saying to Trump, “This is a good one, huh,” and she later said both men chuckled and she “felt uncomfortable, but, at the time, was too young to understand why.” That interview was removed after initial publication on Jan. 30 and republished Feb. 19; another interview mentioning Trump was removed and restored, while the mother’s interview remains offline pending additional redactions. In the mother’s account, she recalled hearing that “a prince and DONALD TRUMP visited EPSTEIN’s house,” which made her think that if they were there then how could Epstein be a criminal. DOJ said temporary removals occurred when a victim or counsel flagged files for review, and a spokesperson said the department was working “around the clock” to address victim concerns and redact personally identifiable information, though the website may still inadvertently include sensitive content. Robert Glassman, attorney for the woman who testified against Maxwell, criticized DOJ, saying the department had one job and failed to keep victims anonymous. NPR’s reporting credits Stephen Fowler, Saige Miller and Ryan Lucas.
