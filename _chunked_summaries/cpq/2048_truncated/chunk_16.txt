Thanks to Trump, Xi Has Time on His Side With Taiwan, February 25, 2026, argues that the short-term likelihood of a Chinese attack on Taiwan has diminished not because the danger is gone, but because Chinese leader Xi Jinping has reason to believe President Donald Trump will help facilitate pressure on Taipei without forcing Beijing into an invasion. The piece opens with Marco Rubio’s January 2025 confirmation hearing to become U.S. secretary of state, when he said that “unless something dramatic changes” in Asia’s military balance, China would try to invade Taiwan before the end of the decade; it also cites a May 2025 warning from the House Select Committee on the Chinese Communist Party that “the clock is ticking to stop a war in the Indo-Pacific—and this Congress may be America’s last full chance to do it.” It says China still faces major invasion risks because the PLA is in turmoil, the United States and Taiwan are improving capabilities and security cooperation, and Washington is moving to deploy missile and unmanned systems to the Philippines and nuclear-powered submarines to Australia, making any war potentially protracted, Pyrrhic, and possibly escalatory up to nuclear risk. But the article says political trends in Washington and Taipei favor Beijing: Trump authorized several high-profile arms sales in his first term and first year of his second, yet has shown little concern for Taiwan’s security, saying in a January interview that Taiwan is “a source of pride for him [Xi]. He considers it to be a part of China, and that’s up to him, what he’s going to be doing,” and in July 2024 declaring that Taiwan had stolen the U.S. semiconductor industry and “doesn’t give us anything,” a charge he repeated after the Supreme Court’s tariff ruling on Friday. It notes that in March 2025 Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company announced an additional $100 billion investment in the United States, after which Trump said a Chinese invasion would be “a catastrophic event” but that the company’s U.S. investment would leave “a very big part” of the company’s business in the United States, where it would theoretically be safe. Xi, the article says, has both a stick and a carrot with Trump: China’s rare-earths leverage can inflict pain on the U.S. economy, as shown in last year’s trade standoff, while Xi flatters Trump as a peacemaker, quoting their South Korea meeting in October when Xi said, “Mr. President, you care a lot about world peace,” and that Trump is “very enthusiastic about settling various regional hot spot issues.” The article says Trump might even make security concessions in exchange for economic pledges, and that the two reportedly discussed future U.S. arms sales to Taiwan, signaling a willingness to negotiate a topic U.S. officials had effectively taken off the table with the second of the Reagan administration’s “six assurances” in 1982. It also emphasizes Beijing’s favorable view of Taipei politics: President Lai Ching-te is regarded by Chinese officials as more separatist-minded than Tsai Ing-wen, but Lai leads a divided legislature where the Kuomintang (KMT) and Taiwan People’s Party coalition controls 60 of 113 seats and the Democratic Progressive Party holds 51.
