Security fears rise as Kenya plans to reopen Somalia border, 2026-02-24: Somalia's efforts to open the country took another step forward when President William Ruto of neighboring Kenya announced that two border crossings would be reopened in April. Officially, Kenya's land borders to Somalia have been closed since 2011, after attacks blamed on Al-Shabab, the al-Qaeda-affiliated insurgency group based in Somalia, led Kenya to shut the frontier; Kenya briefly agreed in May 2023 to a phased reopening of the common border, then reversed course two months later after the killing of five civilians and eight police officers near the frontier. The frontier itself is 680 kilometers long (422 miles) and has also been disputed over a potentially oil- and gas-rich chunk of the Indian Ocean. Border-town voices welcomed the plan: Mohamed Aden Ali, a trader in Beled Hawo opposite Kenya's Mandera, said, "It is a big move. Traders on both sides of the border depend on each other for their businesses," and urged reopening "as soon as possible because we cannot wait any longer"; community leader Ahmed Barre Diriye said Kenya and Somalia are linked by "trade, education, security, and social ties" and that reopening comes at a crucial time because both sides face severe drought and people need to move to access support; businesswoman Fahmo said she was happy that "after 15 years of closure, the border will finally reopen," and President Ruto posted on X that "It is unacceptable that fellow Kenyans in Mandera remain cut off from their kin and neighbors in Somalia due to the prolonged closure of the Mandera border post." Somali economic analyst Ahmed Khadar said the move made economic sense because Somalia joined the East African Community in 2024, Kenya is the only EAC member bordering Somalia, and Kenya "can be the gateway to connecting Somalia to the other members of the East African Community." The article also noted Somalia's broader push to boost connectivity, trade and tourism through online visas and visas on arrival, plus its joining this month of the East African Community (EAC) passport system after becoming a member of the bloc in 2024; in Mogadishu, western tourists including Sherry Roberts and Australian Nick Benady toured the ruins of formerly grand buildings, beaches and markets, while tour boat operator Aweis Ali at Liido Beach said more tourists were visiting and sharing activities on TikTok. Roberts said, "The people are lovely, getting on with their daily life. There's beauty. There's destruction as well. Obviously, there are security issues. But it's been really interesting and I'm really enjoying it so far. I'd say come and have a look," and Benady said Somalia was "immensely different" with different "language," "culture," "food," and "people," adding they were "some of the biggest hearts I've met in Africa." Years of conflict beginning in the 1990s devastated tourism and left Mogadishu, once known as the "Pearl of the Indian Ocean," labeled one of the world's most dangerous cities; the city’s Mogadishu Cathedral was badly damaged by Al-Shabab in 2008. The reopening nonetheless raised security concerns: poor roads, banditry risks and immigration logistics remain challenges, and Ruto said a multi-agency security team would patrol the area and police deployment would be doubled. Khadar said security cooperation had once been "very low, maybe because of weak government in Somalia," though he argued the threat was less acute than 10 or 15 years ago. The article recalled that African Union forces, with a large Kenyan contingent, largely restored Somali cities to government control, but Al-Shabab still poses a serious threat and has mounted major attacks in Kenya, including the Westgate shopping mall attack in Nairobi in 2013 and the Garissa University attack in 2015, while in Somalia it has repeatedly targeted Mogadishu and Kismayo with deadly truck and car bombs.
