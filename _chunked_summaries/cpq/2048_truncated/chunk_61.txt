### After the strike: The danger of war in Iran | Brookings | 2026-03-03: Brookings experts weighed in after “On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched a joint military operation against Iran, resulting in the killing of Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei.” Suzanne Maloney’s “‘Khamenei raft’” compared Khamenei’s death in an Israeli airstrike on his compound to the iconic February 1979 “ Shah raft” (“the Shah is gone”) headline announcing Mohammad Reza Pahlavi’s departure; she said Khamenei had ruled for nearly 37 years, centralized the supreme leader’s dominance, and empowered Iran’s regional influence, but warned that the U.S.-Israeli operation may not produce a real transition because “deeply embedded networks and institutions” still favor the Islamic Republic, while Tehran seeks leverage by escalating strikes on neighbors’ energy and economic infrastructure. Itamar Rabinovich’s “Tehran’s radical turn” said the initial air raid killed Khamenei, several family members, and other officials, yet did not topple the regime or trigger immediate popular opposition; he argued the radical core is retaliating against Arab neighbors, that it would likely take “five days to several weeks” to bring regime change, and that tensions could split U.S.-Israeli goals if Trump wants a quick ending and Netanyahu a broader war. Mara Karlin’s “High-end war, low-cost drones” tied the war to the post–October 7, 2023 regional conflict chain, saying U.S. and Israeli militaries had apparently hit “more than a thousand Iranian targets in two days,” while Iranian missiles and drones had attacked “at least nine different countries” by Monday morning, including Gulf bases hosting U.S. forces, with low-cost drones proving more effective than many missiles. Vanda Felbab-Brown’s “The limits of leadership targeting” noted that the Trump administration had announced as a key goal the end of the theocratic regime; she said Khamenei and other IRGC and military leaders were killed in the first days and that more leadership killings would not make the “vast” regime melt away, because the IRGC, militias, and economic assets remain entrenched; she warned that even a transition may not bring democracy and could empower oppressed Kurdish and Baluch groups or Islamist militancy, and criticized the administration’s lack of “clarity, planning, readiness, and accountability,” contrasting Iran with Venezuela and warning that Trump may see only “99%” cosmetic change as enough. Stephanie T. Williams’s “Flying blind” said the U.S. had launched an “entirely volitional and illegal war” against a country with no U.S. diplomatic presence for nearly 50 years, a “country of 92 million people” ruled by a corrupt cabal; she warned of “full implosion, fragmentation” resembling worse than Iraq and Libya, asked whether the U.S. would send ground troops or keep an armada in the Persian Gulf, and argued the biggest beneficiaries could be Moscow and Beijing. Sharan Grewal’s “The most likely scenario is still a deal, not regime change” called a “Venezuela scenario” the best outcome for Trump—“a modified nuclear deal plus some oil concessions”—noting “three U.S. troops already killed over the weekend, followed by a friendly fire downing of three jets Monday morning,” and said the regime’s incentives had not changed even as protesters celebrated Khamenei’s death and nationalist counterprotests continued. Steven Heydemann’s “The regime change gamble” said Trump and Netanyahu were asking Iranians to rise up, but regime change is the war’s “central vulnerability,” with scenarios including regime survival and harsher repression, replacement by even more repressive leaders, or sustained conflict beyond Iran’s borders. Dafna H. Rand’s “Civilian tools, not bombs” argued civilian influence should be expanded if the objective is regime transformation: the Near East Regional Democracy Program, established in 2009, has invested “nearly $600 million” in civil society support; during the Women, Life, Freedom protests, “roughly one in four Iranians used a U.S.-funded virtual private network (VPN),” and the U.S. should restore democracy, human rights, and internet freedom programs and rebuild sanctions teams. In the Regional section, Aslı Aydıntaşbaş’s “After Iran, Turkey and Israel face a reckoning” said the war is sharpening Turkey-Israel enmity and noted Erdoğan blamed Netanyahu for “provocations”; Turkey fears refugee flows, trade and energy disruption, and Kurdish autonomy in Iran, and sees an Israel-led order as a geopolitical threat. Kemal Kirişci’s “Turkey is holding its breath” recalled that after 1979 an estimated “1.5 million Iranians” transited Turkey in the 1980s and early 1990s, and that Turkey’s Syrian refugee experience left it hosting “more than 2.3 million” Syrians, with the current anti-inflationary economy vulnerable to energy shocks. Hady Amr’s “After the attack on Iran, questions at home” asked what comes next for Iran—Hezbollah-style underground leadership, a North Korea-like hardline turn, Iraq/Syria-style internal conflict, or reconstituted status quo—and raised domestic U.S. questions about constitutionality, crude prices doubling, Republican support for a war Trump pledged to end, Democratic inaction on war powers, and whether the war serves U.S. interests or Netanyahu’s; he also asked whether Trump would press Netanyahu to let Palestinians live freely in a safe homeland. Constanze Stelzenmüller’s “Will Europe enter the fray?” said Europe sees Tehran as a long-time security spoiler supporting terror, refugee flows, and Russia’s Shahed drones, but after Iranian drone hits on British and French bases and German troops’ sites in Jordan and Iraq, the “E-3” — France, Germany, and the United Kingdom — said on Sunday night, “We will take steps to defend our interests,” and the U.K. agreed to let the U.S. use British bases while France said it would boost presence; she noted German Chancellor Friedrich Merz was arriving in Washington and that Ukraine has four years of experience downing Iranian drones. Pavel K. Baev’s “For Russia, a major geopolitical setback with a sharp personal twist” said Moscow had tried to deter escalation with naval exercises and was shocked by the massive strike and Khamenei’s “assassination”; Putin held an emergency Security Council meeting and condemned the killing as a “cynical violation of all norms,” while privately fearing personal vulnerability after Gadhafi’s death and the pandemic. Steven Pifer’s “A limited response from Moscow” said Russia condemned the strikes as an “unprovoked act of armed aggression” and invoked the October 2025 Treaty on Comprehensive Strategic Partnership, but will mostly respond with words, as it did after Cuba pressure in February, Maduro’s capture in January, the June 2025 strike on Iran’s nuclear facilities, and Assad’s fall in December 2024; Russia is bogged down in Ukraine and wants to keep Trump from fully backing Kyiv. Ryan Hass’s “The view from China and Taiwan” recalled Trump telling Xi Jinping over dessert in 2017 that U.S. forces had struck Syria; he said China’s interests are harmed by Iran instability but Beijing has prepared with petroleum reserves, and Taiwan will want the U.S. to finish quickly so it can refocus on deterring Chinese aggression. Tanvi Madan’s “A consequential crisis for India” said West Asia is India’s extended neighborhood, with “almost 9 million Indian citizens” there, nearly half of oil imports, over two-thirds of LNG imports, more than one-third of “the $135 billion remittances,” and 15% of merchandise exports; New Delhi is prioritizing diaspora safety and has not condemned the U.S.-Israeli killing of Khamenei, while Modi condemned strikes on Bahrain, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE and wants de-escalation. Lynn Kuok’s “Normalizing the use of force: Legal precedents and fallout for Southeast Asia” said the strikes risk normalizing unilateral force under the U.N. Charter and that ASEAN had not issued a statement; Malaysia condemned sovereignty violations, Indonesia urged dialogue, the Philippines focused on Filipinos in Iran, and Singapore avoided direct criticism while regretting failed negotiations. The article warned that Strait of Hormuz disruptions are spiking energy prices and insurance premiums, that the IRGC has claimed to target a U.S. fuel support vessel in the Indian Ocean, that the South China Sea and Malacca Strait could be targeted, and that “around 60 dark fleet tankers” near Malaysia could be used to disrupt shipping; it also recalled Iranian nationals’ 2012 attacks on Israeli diplomatic targets in Thailand, India, and Georgia. In “Domestic and economic implications,” Michael E. O’Hanlon and Caitlin Talmadge used airpower cases—Kosovo in 1999, Afghanistan in 2001, Iraq in 2003, Libya in 2011, ISIS from 2014 through 2019, and Israel’s bombing of Hamas for “more than two years”—to warn that overthrowing Iran’s regime is a high bar and peace remains uncertain.
