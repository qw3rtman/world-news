### ARTICLE 8e114d791301 | 2026-03-01 | Maps and charts of the Iran War ===
The escalating U.S.–Israeli war with Iran and Tehran’s retaliation against Gulf neighbours have severely disrupted Middle Eastern energy infrastructure and global oil and gas flows. Israeli strikes on Iran’s South Pars gas field and the Asaluyeh processing hub on March 18 triggered retaliatory attacks across the Gulf that hit refineries, gas plants and export terminals in Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Qatar, the United Arab Emirates and Bahrain. Multiple key facilities were damaged or shut: drone and missile attacks struck refineries and LNG plants in Saudi Arabia, Kuwait and Qatar, missile debris forced the shutdown of the UAE’s massive Habshan gas complex, repeated attacks targeted its Fujairah export terminal, Bahrain declared force majeure after its Sitra refinery was attacked, and Iraq sharply cut output from its southern oilfields despite avoiding direct strikes. Several export hubs were spared direct damage but saw operations halted because of security threats, intercepted missiles or precautionary shutdowns. Iran’s military said strikes on Iran’s energy infrastructure had led to “a new stage in the war,” in which it had attacked energy facilities linked to the United States, and Iranian military spokesman Ebrahim Zolfaqari warned, “If strikes (on Iran’s energy facilities) happen again, further attacks on your energy infrastructure and that of your allies will not stop until it is completely destroyed.” QatarEnergy’s CEO told Reuters the Iranian attacks had knocked out a sixth of Qatar’s LNG export capacity, worth $20 billion a year, and that repairs would take three to five years; Israeli media reported an Iranian strike hit oil facilities in Israel’s port of Haifa, causing damage but no casualties. The strikes underscored Iran’s ability to exact a heavy price for the U.S.-Israeli campaign and the limits of air defenses, while energy prices surged worldwide: Middle East crude benchmarks hit record highs, U.S. diesel rose above $5 a gallon, gasoline hit its highest level since late 2023, Asian refiners cut runs amid feedstock shortages, governments from China to South Korea imposed export controls or price caps, and the International Energy Agency called for a historic release of 400 million barrels from global reserves. The article also noted that the difficult task of refueling U.S. fighter jets rested on an aging fleet of Boeing KC-135 tankers from the 1950s and 1960s; these tankers were spotlighted after two collided mid-air over Iraq on Thursday, March 5, killing all six crew, with the U.S. saying one crashed in Western Iraq and the other landed safely, and the Islamic Resistance in Iraq claiming responsibility. The newer Boeing KC-46 replacement fleet had delivery problems, including issues with the refueling boom and visual monitoring system, and deliveries were paused last year after cracks were found in new tankers. A separate update said Iran’s security chief Ali Larijani was killed by Israel, the government confirmed on Tuesday, and a senior Iranian official said Iran’s new supreme leader rejected de-escalation offers; Larijani was portrayed as a pragmatic confidant of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei and his son and successor Mojtaba, who was chosen by Iran’s Assembly of Experts to succeed his late father after he was killed in an air strike. The Strait of Hormuz section said Iran continued to halt almost all shipping through the chokepoint carrying about 20% of global oil and LNG, forcing Gulf exporters onto bypass pipelines: Saudi Arabia rapidly increased flows via its East-West pipeline to Yanbu, with the IEA’s March Oil Market Report saying flows surged from an average of 1.7 million barrels per day in 2025 to a record daily export of 5.9 mb/d from Yanbu on March 9 and were expected to hit 7 mb/d within days, while the UAE’s Habshan-Fujairah pipeline averaged 1.8 mb/d between March 1-10, at reported maximum capacity, up from around 1 mb/d before the crisis. Iran continued shipping 1.1 million to 1.5 million barrels per day, and on Friday U.S. forces struck Kharg Island, the hub for about 90% of Iran’s oil exports; President Donald Trump said U.S. forces “totally obliterated every military target” there and warned Iran’s oil infrastructure could also be hit if it kept interfering with shipping. The shipping section said at least 22 civilian ships—tankers, container ships and bulk carriers—had been attacked since the campaign began, and included a dated list of vessels hit from Feb. 28 through March 11, including the Stena Imperative, MKD Vyom, Hercules Star, Zefyros and Safesea Vishnu. A separate block on arms trade said the Middle East accounted for around 21% of global arms imports in 2025, and Europe 42%; the U.S. supplied 54% of Middle Eastern imports between 2021 and 2025, followed by Italy at 12% and France at 11%, with Saudi Arabia, Israel and Qatar among key U.S. customers. The UAE bought over 13,000 missiles from the U.S. in the last ten years, Saudi Arabia got 89 aircraft, over 150 armoured vehicles and around 1,800 missiles, all of Iran’s imported arms came from Russia, and nearly 73% of Iran’s arms exports went to Russia. Another section said ACLED recorded more than 990 worldwide demonstrations between February 28 and March 6, including 325 in the Middle East with 35 violent; at least 23 protesters were killed in Pakistan on March 1, including 10 in Karachi, 11 in Skardu and two in Islamabad, while Iraq saw tear gas and stun grenades used against pro-Iran protesters near the Green Zone in Baghdad. In Europe, thousands celebrated in Paris waving pre-revolutionary monarchy flags, in Lisbon exiles gathered outside the Iranian embassy, and Britain banned a pro-Iran march in London, invoking the power for the first time in 14 years because the risk of disorder was “so severe”; in the U.S. there were 145 demonstrations in cities including Los Angeles, New York, Washington D.C., Dallas, Philadelphia and Las Vegas, mostly against the Trump administration’s campaign. Another section described Israeli strikes on fuel depots and refineries around Tehran on March 7, with Iran’s foreign ministry spokesperson Esmaeil Baghaei calling it a “dangerous new phase” and a war crime, while Israeli spokesman Lt. Col. Nadav Shoshani said the depots were a legal military target because they fueled the war effort and produced or stored propellant for ballistic missiles; thick smoke containing hydrocarbons and sulfur and nitrogen dioxides spread over Tehran, a city of more than 9 million, prompting warnings to stay indoors and a Pakistan advisory about cross-border pollution. A market section said the conflict had effectively closed the Strait of Hormuz, pushing crude prices sharply higher and hitting Asian equities; the Korean KOSPI fell nearly 20% from before the conflict started until Wednesday, March 4, with a 12% single-day drop on March 4, while Japan’s Nikkei 225 fell 6% from last week and India’s market declined nearly 3%. A Lebanon section said Israeli airstrikes pounded Beirut’s southern suburbs overnight and on Friday, after evacuation orders issued by Israeli military spokespersons covering about 8% of Lebanese territory on Wednesday and the southern Beirut suburbs on Thursday; about 115 targets were struck in residential buildings that Israel said Hezbollah used as headquarters, with strikes also on Tripoli, Tyre, Sidon, Nabatieh and Baalbek, and evacuees telling Reuters they were sleeping in cars, on the street or on the beach. Volker Turk said on Friday Israel’s blanket evacuation orders raised serious concerns under international law, including possible forced transfer. Another section summarized the spreading conflict, saying U.S. and Israeli attacks in Iran prompted retaliatory strikes around the Gulf and rattled markets; Trump said the U.S. had struck numerous Iranian naval and air targets and “just about everything has been knocked out,” while a source familiar with Israel’s war plan said the campaign had been planned to last two weeks but was moving faster than expected after early success in killing Iran’s leaders, including Khamenei. It also described Iranian Kurdish militias talking with the U.S. about attacks on Iran’s security forces, said at least 200 ships remained at anchor off Iraq, Saudi Arabia and Qatar after the February 28 strikes, and noted commercial satellite imagery from Vantor showing two strikes on access points to Natanz’s Fuel Enrichment Plant, an underground facility designed to house 50,000 centrifuges. Global aviation was also heavily disrupted: major airports including Dubai, Abu Dhabi and Doha were affected, airspace over Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, Israel, Bahrain, the UAE and Qatar was virtually empty, and over 6,000 flights were cancelled in seven Middle East countries with Dubai International accounting for over 3,000 cancellations. A final poll section said only 27% of respondents approved of the strikes, 43% disapproved and 29% were not sure, with 55% of Republicans approving versus 7% of Democrats and 19% of independents; 56% of Americans thought Trump was too willing to use military force.
