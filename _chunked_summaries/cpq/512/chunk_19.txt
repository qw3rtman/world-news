Victory Capital goes public with $8.6 billion rival bid for Janus Henderson | Reuters, 2026-02-26: Victory Capital (VCTR.O), based in San Antonio, went public on Thursday with an $8.6 billion cash-and-stock offer for Janus Henderson (JHG.N), after multiple unsuccessful bids in November and December and after Janus had already agreed in December to a $7.4 billion/$49-per-share takeover by Nelson Peltz’s Trian and General Catalyst. Victory’s bid was $30 in cash plus 0.350 of its shares for each Janus share, valuing Janus at $57.04 per share and implying a combined group with more than $800 billion in assets and an enterprise value of $16 billion. Janus shares rose nearly 6% in afternoon trading while Victory fell more than 7%. Victory said its proposal was “superior,” fully financed through commitments from two investment banks, and should be blessed by Janus’s independent board committee as a “superior proposal” before talks can begin; Trian owns about 20.7% of Janus stock per LSEG. Evercore analysts said Victory’s bid valued Janus at 12.5 times 2026 earnings versus Trian’s 11 times. (NASDAQ: VCTR) sent another letter to Janus Henderson Group plc’s Special Committee and Board, calling its “best-of-both-worlds” offer a fully financed, actionable, “Company Superior Proposal” over Trian Fund Management, L.P. and its affiliated funds. The terms were $57.04 per share, consisting of $30.00 in cash and a fixed exchange ratio of 0.350 Victory shares based on Victory’s Feb. 25, 2026 closing price, a 37% premium to Janus’s unaffected share price as of Oct. 24, 2025 and about 16% above the Trian transaction. Janus shareholders would own approximately 38% of the combined company, with total enterprise value of approximately $16 billion. Victory said the merger would retain substantially all Janus investment professionals and preserve the Janus Henderson brand, and claimed lower execution risk, no financing outs, full specific performance protection, a reduced 75% client-consent closing condition versus Trian’s 80%, and a lower 3% termination fee versus 4% in the Trian agreement. It also eliminated Janus’s obligation to pay $111.42 million in expense reimbursements if shareholders failed to approve the merger, and said the Trian termination fee upon a switch would be $297.13 million, or 4% of equity value.
