The piece traces the Pahlavi dynasty back to 1925, when Reza Khan overthrew the previous dynasty and adopted “Pahlavi,” later banning the hijab before being forced into exile by Britain after the Anglo-Soviet invasion of Iran in 1941; his son Mohammad Reza Pahlavi ruled as shah until the 1979 Islamic Revolution. His US-based son, exiled crown prince Reza Pahlavi, 65, is presented by diaspora royalists as the most visible opposition figure and as a transitional leader for a secular Iran, not an absolute monarch; in a Washington Post opinion piece on Sunday he thanked Trump for the strikes and wrote, “Many Iranians, often despite facing bullets, have called on me to lead this transition,” promising “a new constitution drafted and ratified by referendum, followed by free elections under international oversight” and that “When Iranians vote, the transitional government dissolves.” He said opposition figures agreed on “separation of religion and state,” territorial integrity, “individual liberties and equality of all citizens,” and noted his 2023 trip to Israel.
