The strikes then hit US-linked military sites: an Iranian missile struck the headquarters of the US Navy in Bahrain, Qatar said it intercepted missiles headed for Al Udeid Air Base, the largest American base in the region, and Kuwait said Ali Al-Salem air base, which hosts members of the US Air Force, was hit. Hasan Alhasan of the International Institute for Strategic Studies said Iran had crossed "a new threshold" by targeting Gulf cities, airports and energy infrastructure because the Gulf states are closer to Iran and less heavily defended than Israel; Anwar Gargash, former UAE foreign minister and now a presidential adviser, posted on X, "Your war is not with your neighbors," and told The National that the UAE "won't just sit by" while attacked. The article says Saudi Arabia, the UAE and Jordan had earlier said they would not allow the Americans to use their territory to launch airstrikes against Iran, but those assurances proved ineffective; Gulf states were said to have hosted US bases or bought US weapons as deterrence, while also seeking better ties with Iran and a diplomatic solution to Iran’s nuclear issue. On March 1, Israel said it was targeting sites "in the heart of Tehran." Analysts Hasan Alhasan and Andreas Krieg said direct Gulf retaliation is unlikely except perhaps in narrow self-defense, with Krieg saying Gulf states will try to contain spillover, maximizing air and missile defense, protecting bases and critical infrastructure, tightening internal security, and activating continuity plans; Oman and Qatar were expected to keep lines open for US-Iran negotiations.
