### Brookings experts weigh in, After the strike: The danger of war in Iran, 2026-03-03. The piece says that on February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched a joint military operation against Iran that killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei in an Israeli airstrike on his compound. Suzanne Maloney invokes the iconic 1979 “Shah raft” and argues “Khamenei raft” may be equally momentous but not necessarily regime-changing; Itamar Rabinovich says the initial air raid killed Khamenei, several family members, and other officials but did not topple the regime, with radicalization and attacks on Arab neighbors likely, and notes war-phase timing of “five days to several weeks” and possible friction between Trump’s desire for a swift end and Netanyahu’s broader aims. Mara Karlin says the U.S. and Israeli militaries have hit “more than a thousand Iranian targets in two days,” while Iranian missiles and drones had attacked at least nine countries by Monday morning, including Gulf bases where U.S. forces operate. Vanda Felbab-Brown says leadership-targeting will not make the IRGC, with sprawling religious authority and economic assets, “melt away,” and warns even elections may not yield stable democracy. Stephanie T. Williams says Trump has given little clarity, compares a possible Iranian implosion to Iraq and Libya, and notes “92 million” people in a state ruled by armed thugs; Sharan Grewal says the most likely Trump move is still a “Venezuela scenario,” with “three U.S. troops already killed over the weekend” and a friendly-fire downing of “three jets Monday morning,” while the regime’s networks remain resilient and no major defections have occurred. Steven Heydemann argues regime change is the war’s central vulnerability and sketches three grim outcomes; Dafna H. Rand calls for “civilian tools, not bombs,” citing the Near East Regional Democracy Program, established in 2009 and funded at “nearly $600 million,” plus “roughly one in four Iranians” using a U.S.-funded VPN during the Women, Life, Freedom protests. Regional analysts say Turkey and Israel face a reckoning: Aslı Aydıntaşbaş says Ankara sees war as worsening a Turkey-Israel security dilemma, while Kemal Kirişci notes Turkey remembers hosting “more than 2.3 million” Syrian refugees and fears new inflows and economic shocks. Hady Amr asks domestic U.S. questions about constitutionality, oil prices doubling, and whether Congress will object; Constanze Stelzenmüller says Americans and Israelis will decide the outcome with their “voices and with their votes.” Europe, Russia, China, India, Southeast Asia, oil markets, and war powers are all affected: the E-3 (France, Germany, the United Kingdom) moved to defend interests; Pavel K. Baev says Putin’s “beautiful armada” deterrence failed and fears assassination norms; China is focused on oil flows and Taiwan, India worries about “almost 9 million” citizens and “$135 billion” in remittances, Southeast Asian states stress sovereignty, Brent rose only “4.5%” on March 2 despite Strait of Hormuz risk, and Congress is bypassed. ###
