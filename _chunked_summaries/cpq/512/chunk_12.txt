Exclusive: Hegseth gives Anthropic until Friday to back down on AI safeguards, dated 2026-02-24, said Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth gave Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei until Friday evening to give the military unfettered access to Anthropic’s Claude model or face “harsh penalties,” after a tense Tuesday meeting at the Pentagon; officials said the Pentagon would either cut ties and declare Anthropic a “supply chain risk” or invoke the Defense Production Act to force the company to tailor its model to the military’s needs, with Claude described as the only AI model currently used for the military’s most sensitive work and also for a wide variety of bureaucratic functions. Hegseth told Amodei he would not let any company dictate the terms under which the Pentagon makes operational decisions or object to individual use cases, and specifically cited the Pentagon’s claim that Anthropic raised concerns to partner Palantir over Claude’s use during the Maduro raid; Amodei denied Anthropic raised such concerns beyond standard operating conversations. Present in the room were Deputy Secretary Steve Feinberg, Under Secretary for Research and Engineering Emil Michael, Under Secretary for Acquisition and Sustainment Michael Duffey, Hegseth’s chief spokesperson Sean Parnell, and general counsel Earl Matthews. Anthropic said it was continuing “good-faith conversations” so it could support the government’s national security mission “in line with what our models can reliably and responsibly do,” while maintaining red lines against mass surveillance of Americans and weapons that fire without human involvement. The Defense Production Act was described as a rarely used tool that can compel private companies to accept and prioritize contracts for national defense, though Anthropic could argue it is custom-built software for sensitive government uses rather than a commercially available product; cutting ties would require a Claude replacement, with xAI’s Grok already under contract for classified settings and Gemini seen as a possible replacement if Google agreed to “all lawful purposes,” while the Pentagon was speeding up talks with OpenAI and Google.

Pentagon gives AI firm ultimatum: lift military limits by Friday or lose $200M deal, published February 24, 2026, reported the same standoff: the Pentagon gave Anthropic until Friday to lift restrictions on Claude or risk canceling a $200 million contract, declaring Anthropic a supply chain risk, or invoking the Defense Production Act. The dispute was triggered after the Pentagon said Anthropic questioned whether Claude was used in a January military operation to capture Venezuelan leader Nicolés Maduro; Anthropic denied discussing specific operations and said its red lines barred fully autonomous weapons and mass surveillance of Americans. War Secretary Pete Hegseth delivered the ultimatum in a Tuesday Pentagon meeting with Amodei, praised Anthropic’s technology, and said the department wants to keep working with the firm, but insisted the military must be able to use AI for all lawful purposes without company oversight or approval. Claude was described as the only advanced commercial AI model of its kind operating inside the Pentagon’s classified networks under a $200 million contract awarded in summer 2025. Hegseth compared the issue to being told the military could not use a specific aircraft for a mission, and Pentagon officials said the issue “has nothing to do with mass surveillance or autonomous targeting” because “there’s always a human involved and the department always follows the law.” The article noted Grok had agreed to allow use for all lawful purposes including classified integration, and Anthropic’s spokesperson repeated the “good-faith conversations” line.
