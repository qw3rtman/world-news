Now, many aren’t eager to join the war | Reuters, 2026-03-06: in Baghdad, Reuters said Iran’s decades-long, billions-of-dollars effort to build Iraqi Shi’ite proxy militias through the IRGC and Quds Force has not produced a mass mobilization after the U.S. and Israel went to war on the Islamic Republic a week ago. A.J., an Iranian-backed commander from Shi’ite-majority southern Iraq, said he had been waiting for orders from Tehran that never came, and believed only “two or three” of the “dozens” of Iraqi paramilitaries would likely be tasked to act; he said most were “just looking out for their own interests,” that “some will act” through front groups, and that Tehran had sent “no new weapons” since the fight with Israel last year. Some groups claimed drone attacks on “enemy bases in Iraq and the region,” explosions hit Erbil, and more than two-dozen online claims in the name of the Islamic Resistance of Iraq caused no significant damage and sometimes had no evidence. The article says direct missile and drone attacks mostly came from Iran, while Iraqi proxies were weakened by assassinations, loss of Syria as a supply line, and commanders’ drift into politics and business; Gareth Stansfield said Iraqi militia leaders now want to avoid sanctions and keep access to Western healthcare and foreign education, saying the idea they are under Tehran’s thumb is “not the case anymore.” On March 1, thousands of supporters, including off-duty fighters, tried to storm the U.S. embassy through Baghdad’s fortified Green Zone but were beaten back and tear-gassed; Qais al-Khazali, whose banners were visible, only posted a mild X statement urging people to “wear black,” unlike in 2019 when he had joined embassy attackers. The report traces key turning points: the 2019 embassy assault, Trump’s 2020 killing of Qassem Soleimani, Hezbollah’s temporary coordination role, Hassan Nasrallah’s September 2024 assassination, and Bashar al-Assad’s December 2024 ouster, which cut the land corridor through Syria. A former Iraqi intelligence chief said the leaders worship “weapons and money”; Abu Turab al-Tamimi said only “Kataib Hezbollah, Nujaba, and a couple of others perhaps” remained fully loyal, while Khazali and Shibl al-Zaidi privately aligned with the U.S. on Iraq’s new prime minister and reached out to European officials.
