Last year, only three cluster missiles hit Israel. In the current round, Iran has launched more than 100, carrying far more submunitions ■ Two Israelis were killed

Bar Peleg and Oded Yaron

March 12, 2026

UPDATE: 22 cluster missiles have already hit Israel as of 18/03/2026

*

At 10:50 P.M. last Thursday, the cellphones of millions of Israelis in central Israel once again warned of missile fire from Iran. It was the second launch within half an hour. Seven minutes later, sirens sounded. Four minutes after that, videos began circulating online showing something never before seen in Israel: dozens of bright points of light diving toward the greater Tel Aviv area.

The fireballs that have appeared in Israel's skies over the past 12 days have become the most striking visual symbol of the current war with Iran. Sometimes they are single fragments from an intercepted missile – an engine, a fuel tank, or burning pieces of metal falling to the ground. But when dozens of burning objects fly in an organized, steep trajectory, security officials immediately go on alert: this is a cluster missile carrying dozens of submunitions, designed to cause panic and spread destruction across a wide area.

The missile launched last Thursday night was likely a Khorramshahr model– an Iranian ballistic missile fitted with a warhead that disperses up to 80 bomblets, each containing only a few kilograms of explosives. The interception attempt failed, and the bomblets fell across the greater Tel Aviv area. Seven struck populated locations along a 27-kilometer stretch, from Peduel in the West Bank to Holon on the Mediterranean coast. The rest fell in open areas.

In addition to three conventional ballistic missiles that struck populated areas, 11 cluster missiles have penetrated Israel's air defense system since the war began, according to a Haaretz analysis. By comparison, only three cluster missiles struck Israel in last year's war with Iran. Security officials estimate that Iran has launched about 250 at Israel in the current war – roughly half of them were cluster missiles.

On February 28, a Saturday afternoon, a missile barrage was launched toward central Israel. It was the first day of the war, and the rate of launches toward Israel was especially high. The barrage included the war's first cluster missile.

The bomblets struck at least five sites. One landed in Ramat Gan, gouging a crater in a yard and penetrating a basement ceiling to a depth of 15 centimeters. Although it contained only 2-3 kilograms of explosives, the damage was significant. A 90-year-old building made of concrete blocks sustained considerable damage. The strike marked the beginning of a trend: cluster missiles fired into densely populated civilian areas.

The bomblets are less lethal than a standard missile – as long as the public follows instructions and enters shelters.

Conventional ballistic missiles carry warheads containing hundreds of kilograms of explosives. A direct hit can collapse a building and cause heavy destruction to many structures nearby. Two missiles that struck Tel Aviv and Beit Shemesh killed 10 people.

By contrast, a cluster missile warhead opens at an altitude of about seven kilometers above the ground and disperses much smaller munitions, creating damage similar to the short-range rockets used by Hamas and Hezbollah.

Across dozens of sites in the greater Tel Aviv area, the bomblets left numerous craters. They produce large amounts of shrapnel that travels at high speed for dozens of meters, and their blast waves are powerful. This week in Tel Aviv, a car was hurled several meters after it was hit.

However, the bomblets are less lethal than a standard missile – as long as the public follows instructions and enters shelters. This week in Yehud provided a painful reminder: despite several precious minutes of early warning, two construction workers who remained in an open area during the siren were struck by cluster munitions and killed.

Two other Israelis who were outside shelters, in Tel Aviv and Or Yehuda, were seriously injured. Footage from the scene showed that one of the wounded did not lie down on the ground – as recommended if a siren catches someone in an open area. Lying flat on the ground would very likely have prevented serious injury.

Although this is the fourth round of conflict with Iran, Israel encountered these cluster missiles only in the later stages of the third round, in June of last year. At that time, according to a Haaretz analysis, three cluster missiles struck Israel, each carrying only about ten bomblets.

In the current round, far more cluster missiles have been fired, each carrying many more bomblets. The Khorramshahr missile documented Thursday night was so bright and clear that it was captured by a Jordanian photographer's camera from Amman. According to the footage, the missile contained at least 70 bomblets. They caused damage, but no one was injured.

Security officials told Haaretz that they have encountered the full range of Iranian missile types known to Israel's defense establishment.

"Iran's use of warheads containing bomblets has been known for many years," says Tal Inbar, a researcher at the Missile Defense Advocacy Alliance. "Originally, such a warhead was intended for strikes against area targets such as airfields, soft vehicles, radar stations and the like."

In its intended battlefield use, however, a heavy barrage of missiles – not a single missile – would be fired, creating a "rain of bomblets" covering a wide area.

According to Inbar, Iran has a wide variety of cluster missiles, including an entire family of short-range missiles that cannot reach Israel. However, a cluster warhead can be fitted to any long-range missile capable of reaching Israel – including the Ghadr missile, the more advanced Emad, and the Khorramshahr, which can carry up to 80 bomblets.

At the same time the cluster missile was launched toward Israel, the official Twitter account of Iran's late supreme leader, Ali Khamenei, posted an image of a Khorramshahr missile. The missiles differ not only in the number of submunitions they carry but also in their size.

"The heavier ones are covered with a thick thermal coating and fins and weigh about 20 kilograms, four kilograms of which are explosive material," Inbar notes.

The use of cluster munitions creates major challenges for rescue forces because large numbers of unexploded bomblets can remain on the ground. These duds can remain in place for months or even years and might explode if they are touched. The high dud rate is one of the main reasons for the international convention banning the use of cluster munitions. Iran and Israel are not signatories to that convention.

When there is suspicion that cluster munitions were used, a special joint operations center for the police and the Home Front Command is opened, and their personnel scan large areas together. Police bomb disposal units then neutralize the bomblets.

"This is a troubling type of munition," says Chief Superintendent Doron Lavi of the National Police Bomb Disposal Division. "When a single warhead weighing hundreds of kilograms explodes, it creates severe damage – it can bring down buildings and strike an entire neighborhood – but the damage is localized. Cluster munitions, on the other hand, disperse unevenly and cover an enormous area. There are many small explosions, many scenes that need to be reached, many calls to emergency centers, greater panic, and the danger of unexploded ordnance."

"These bomblets can kill and cause damage," Lavi notes, but compared with a half-ton warhead the damage is limited. I'm not belittling it, but it mainly creates enormous disruption because entire areas have to be searched and cleared, and that takes time."

Yoav Saban, the security coordinator of the Drom Hasharon Regional Council, has been especially busy over the past week.

"We've had dozens of incidents involving falling bomblets. One exploded on a house in Sirkin [a moshav southeast of Petah Tikva], we had one in a yard and others in fields. Some also exploded in open areas – for example in the kibbutzim Horshim, Einat and Givat Hashlosha. You see the explosion and understand it's not a fragment of a missile. We oversee 94,000 dunams [94 square kilometers]. Immediately after the sirens, if we hear impacts, we conduct searches throughout the communities with the local emergency squads, and that's how we find the unexploded ordnance. We also scan schools with drones."

### A violation of the laws of war

Military use of cluster munitions is usually directed at focused targets within a radius of up to 100 meters. If used carefully against legitimate military targets, it is in principle permitted under the laws of war. But in the Iranian case, the cluster munitions were fired from the outset toward civilian populations, which experts say constitutes a particularly serious violation.

According to Haaretz's tracking, at least 14 missiles launched by Iran penetrated Israel's air defense system and were not intercepted. Three carried conventional warheads – the deadly strikes in Tel Aviv and Beit Shemesh, and another that caused injuries on a main road in Jerusalem – and 11 were cluster missiles.

It remains unclear why such a high proportion of cluster missiles managed to penetrate the defenses. Had these been conventional warheads striking population centers, the damage would likely have been far greater.

The IDF says its interception systems are capable of dealing with the challenge. But according to Inbar, even a direct hit on a ballistic missile before its warhead opens and disperses the bomblets does not always guarantee complete neutralization of the submunitions. Once the bomblets have been released, intercepting them becomes extremely difficult.

"All dispersal warheads that reach the ground are ones that were not intercepted at high altitude," explained Brig. Gen. (res.) Ran Kochav, former commander of the IDF's air defense forces. "Once they open at an altitude of seven kilometers, the Arrow-3 – which intercepts outside the atmosphere – is no longer relevant, nor is Arrow-2, which intercepts within the atmosphere, or the American THAAD system, which intercepts at even higher altitudes."

Still, given IDF assessments that more than 100 cluster missiles have been launched at Israel, the interception rate remains high – similar to the overall interception rate of Israel's air defense system in the current war.
