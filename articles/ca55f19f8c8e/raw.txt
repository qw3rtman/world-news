## Foreign Affairs says Iran’s regime is weakened but not yet close to collapse

A recent Foreign Affairs analysis argues that Iran’s ruling system is facing one of its most vulnerable moments since 1979 but remains far from toppling. The piece notes that Israeli and U.S. strikes last June severely damaged Iran’s uranium‑enrichment capacity and air defenses, while nationwide protests in December and January exposed deep public anger. Combined with economic and environmental crises the government cannot solve, these pressures have left the Islamic Republic “at its weakest point since its founding.”

Despite these setbacks, the article stresses that none of these blows has dismantled the regime. Iran’s security apparatus—especially the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps—remains intact, and the leadership has shown resilience in the face of external attacks and internal unrest. The author argues that meaningful political change would require sustained external pressure paired with internal mobilization, and that even significant military action would not guarantee a rapid transition.

The analysis concludes that while the regime is “down,” it is not out, and any U.S. strategy aimed at accelerating change would need to be far more comprehensive than previous efforts. It also warns that misjudging the regime’s durability could lead to costly miscalculations for Washington.

## IAEA warns it can’t assure Iran’s nuclear program is peaceful without restored access

The U.N. nuclear watchdog says it can no longer guarantee that Iran’s nuclear activities are exclusively peaceful after losing access to key facilities struck during U.S. and Israeli attacks in June. According to confidential reports seen by Bloomberg and the Associated Press, International Atomic Energy Agency Director General Rafael Grossi told member states that inspectors have been unable to verify the status or location of Iran’s highly enriched uranium since the strikes.

The IAEA said Iran has denied access to all four of its declared enrichment sites, causing a complete “loss of continuity of knowledge” over nuclear material previously monitored at those facilities. Without on‑site inspections, the agency cannot confirm whether enrichment has stopped or determine the current size, composition, or whereabouts of Iran’s stockpile. Satellite imagery has shown regular vehicle movement at damaged sites—including the underground complex at Isfahan and the enrichment halls at Natanz and Fordow—but Grossi emphasized that remote observation cannot reveal what activities are taking place.

Iran is currently estimated to hold more than 400 kilograms of uranium enriched up to 60 percent purity, a short technical step from weapons‑grade levels of 90 percent. Grossi has previously warned that while such material does not constitute a nuclear weapon, it could be sufficient for several bombs if further refined.

## Iran’s enrichment rights emerge as central obstacle in nuclear talks

A core dispute in the U.S.–Iran negotiations is whether Tehran will be allowed to keep any part of its uranium‑enrichment program, which Iranian officials describe as a hard‑won national right. Uranium enrichment is a technically demanding process mastered by fewer than 15 countries worldwide, involving the use of high‑speed centrifuges to isolate and concentrate the fissile isotope U‑235. At low levels, enriched uranium fuels civilian power reactors; at roughly 90 percent purity, it can be used for nuclear weapons.

For Iran, maintaining some level of enrichment is tied to sovereignty, scientific achievement, and domestic political legitimacy. For Washington, any continued enrichment raises concerns about how quickly Iran could move toward weapons‑grade material if talks collapse. The gap between these positions has become one of the most difficult issues to bridge, with negotiators warning that without a compromise on enrichment, the broader diplomatic effort could stall.

## What is Iran’s nuclear program?

Concerns about Iran’s nuclear weapons capability prompted President Donald Trump to order strikes that targeted the Fordow and Natanz enrichment facilities and a research site near Isfahan in June 2025.

Trump boasted the strikes "obliterated" Iran's nuclear program and while the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) said they had dealt a serious blow to the program, the watchdog added Iran's nuclear material remained "in large quantities."

Before the U.S. strikes, Iran had enriched 972 pounds of uranium up to 60 percent purity, according to the IAEA, which is a short step away from weapons-grade 90 percent-enriched uranium. This was up from 605.8 pounds of 60-percent-enriched uranium in February 2025, and from 267.9 pounds the previous year.

The U.S. Defense Intelligence Agency (DIA) estimated in May 2025 it would probably take Iran less than a week to produce enough weapons-grade uranium to make its first bomb, although building a bomb would likely take longer.

While Iran has long reiterated it has no intention of developing a nuclear weapon and insisted its program is peaceful, satellite images from late January show roofs built over damaged buildings at the Natanz and Isfahan sites, which may show attempts to salvage remaining materials.
