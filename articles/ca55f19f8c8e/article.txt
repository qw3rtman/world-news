He added that he is unhappy with the pace and substance of negotiations, saying he does not believe Iran is prepared to offer enough concessions and that discussions will continue through the day.

Analysts say Iran could consider:

- Diluting or partially exporting enriched uranium, potentially through partners like Oman or Russia
- Expanding IAEA monitoring, including adopting the Additional Protocol
- Suspending enrichment for several years

But Iran is unlikely to accept shipping out its entire stockpile or permanently ending enrichment. Maintaining at least a symbolic program is seen as essential for domestic legitimacy and regional posture.

The U.N. nuclear watchdog says it can no longer guarantee that Iran’s nuclear activities are exclusively peaceful after losing access to key facilities struck during U.S. and Israeli attacks in June. According to confidential reports seen by Bloomberg and the Associated Press, International Atomic Energy Agency Director General Rafael Grossi told member states that inspectors have been unable to verify the status or location of Iran’s highly enriched uranium since the strikes.

The IAEA said Iran has denied access to all four of its declared enrichment sites, causing a complete “loss of continuity of knowledge” over nuclear material previously monitored at those facilities. Without on‑site inspections, the agency cannot confirm whether enrichment has stopped or determine the current size, composition, or whereabouts of Iran’s stockpile. Satellite imagery has shown regular vehicle movement at damaged sites—including the underground complex at Isfahan and the enrichment halls at Natanz and Fordow—but Grossi emphasized that remote observation cannot reveal what activities are taking place.

Iran is currently estimated to hold more than 400 kilograms of uranium enriched up to 60 percent purity, a short technical step from weapons‑grade levels of 90 percent. Grossi has previously warned that while such material does not constitute a nuclear weapon, it could be sufficient for several bombs if further refined.

A core dispute in the U.S.–Iran negotiations is whether Tehran will be allowed to keep any part of its uranium‑enrichment program, which Iranian officials describe as a hard‑won national right. Uranium enrichment is a technically demanding process mastered by fewer than 15 countries worldwide, involving the use of high‑speed centrifuges to isolate and concentrate the fissile isotope U‑235. At low levels, enriched uranium fuels civilian power reactors; at roughly 90 percent purity, it can be used for nuclear weapons.

For Iran, maintaining some level of enrichment is tied to sovereignty, scientific achievement, and domestic political legitimacy. For Washington, any continued enrichment raises concerns about how quickly Iran could move toward weapons‑grade material if talks collapse. The gap between these positions has become one of the most difficult issues to bridge, with negotiators warning that without a compromise on enrichment, the broader diplomatic effort could stall.

Omani Foreign Minister Badr Al Busaidi is set to meet Vice President JD Vance and senior U.S. officials in Washington on Friday for previously undisclosed talks aimed at preventing a military confrontation with Iran. The meeting follows a session in Geneva one day earlier, where Al Busaidi met special envoy Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner as part of Oman’s ongoing mediation in indirect U.S.–Iran negotiations.

The diplomatic push comes as Secretary of State Marco Rubio prepares to travel to Israel on March 2–3 for discussions on Iran, Lebanon, and implementation of President Donald Trump’s 20‑Point Peace Plan for Gaza. The State Department announced the trip shortly after authorizing the departure of non‑emergency U.S. personnel from the embassy in Jerusalem due to rising security risks.

The Washington meeting lands at a moment of heightened tension, with Trump warning he may use military force if diplomacy stalls. Vance, however, insisted in an interview aboard Air Force Two that there is “no chance” the U.S. would enter a years‑long war with Iran, despite concerns from Pentagon officials and lawmakers that even limited strikes could escalate.

Iran’s foreign minister, Abbas Araghchi, said the Geneva talks made “further progress” and were the “most intense so far,” adding that both sides will continue working through sanctions relief and nuclear‑related steps. Al Busaidi echoed that “significant progress” had been made, even as the U.S. continues a major military buildup across the Middle East.

A confidential report from the U.N. nuclear watchdog says Iran has refused to grant inspectors access to facilities hit during the 12‑day war in June, leaving the agency unable to determine the status of key parts of Tehran’s nuclear program.

According to the document, seen by the Associated Press, the International Atomic Energy Agency warned it “cannot verify whether Iran has suspended all enrichment‑related activities” or assess “the size of Iran’s uranium stockpile at the affected nuclear facilities.”

Senate Democratic Leader Chuck Schumer is pressing the Trump administration to publicly explain its approach to Iran, warning that Congress and the American public have been left in the dark as tensions rise. Speaking on the Senate floor, Schumer said that after a classified briefing with Secretary of State Marco Rubio earlier in the week, he remained concerned that the administration had not articulated a clear plan.

“Everyone is asking what the plan is with respect to Iran, and we’re all looking for answers that the administration has refused to give,” he said. Schumer argued that the seriousness of the issues discussed behind closed doors requires direct explanation from the president and senior officials.

He added that the White House must “come clean” about its objectives, saying Americans deserve transparency on decisions that could lead to military action. His remarks reflect growing unease among Democrats about the administration’s strategy and the limited information shared with Congress.

China has urged its citizens to avoid traveling to Iran and advised those already in the country to leave “as soon as possible,” citing what it called a sharp rise in external security risks amid fears the United States may be preparing military action. In a notice issued by Beijing’s embassy in Tehran, officials said Chinese nationals should heighten precautions and be ready to depart by commercial flight or overland routes, with consulates in Iran and neighboring states offering assistance.

Beijing’s warning comes despite its close strategic ties with Tehran. China is Iran’s biggest buyer of sanctioned oil and has repeatedly opposed the use of force, calling instead for diplomacy to resolve the standoff over Iran’s nuclear program. Foreign Ministry spokesperson Mao Ning reaffirmed that position, saying China supports Iran’s “stability and legitimate rights” and intends to play a constructive role.

China has supplied Iran with military equipment and surveillance technology, but analysts note that Beijing—much like during last year’s U.S. strikes on Iranian nuclear sites and the 12‑day war with Israel—is unlikely to intervene directly if Washington launches new attacks. The advisory underscores growing international concern as negotiations falter and the risk of escalation rises.

A sharp surge in U.S. military deployments across the Middle East is deepening anxiety in Tehran, where analysts and former officials are split over whether Washington is preparing for an imminent strike or simply trying to strengthen its hand in nuclear negotiations. Multiple American outlets reported Thursday that national security officials have told President Donald Trump the Pentagon now has the air and naval assets in place to launch an attack “within days,” potentially before the end of the week.

Inside Iran, some warn the buildup may reflect more than routine pressure. Political analyst Mohammad Soltaninejad told Entekhab that a breakdown in talks—or a sudden shift in Washington’s posture—could trigger open conflict. “If the negotiations fail or the U.S. position changes… it is possible that war could break out,” he said.

Others argue that despite the heightened rhetoric, regional governments remain wary of a conflict that could expose U.S. bases to retaliation. Former ambassador Jalal Sadatian told ILNA that the balance still leans toward diplomacy, saying Trump appears focused on “threats and exercising pressure” rather than committing to war.

Financial markets are reflecting the uncertainty. Iran’s currency slid nearly one percent in a single day, with the dollar climbing toward 1,630,000 rials. Economic outlet Eco Iran warned that the U.S. deployments represent “operational readiness” rather than mere signaling, while international relations professor Gholamreza Haddad said the scale of the buildup suggests “real preparedness for military conflict.”

Some voices close to Iran’s security establishment have floated the possibility of a limited U.S. strike designed to test Tehran’s deterrence without triggering a full-scale war. Nour News, which is linked to senior security official Ali Shamkhani, argued that such a move could allow Washington to demonstrate strength while avoiding a broader confrontation.

Iran has responded with visible military activity of its own, conducting drills in the Persian Gulf and the Sea of Oman and issuing a NOTAM for a missile test in southern Iran. Yet even if diplomacy survives the current tensions, some analysts warn that any agreement would be fragile. U.S. affairs expert Amir Abolfath told Khabar Online that reaching a deal would be “a miracle,” adding that even war might not resolve the underlying disputes.

The failure of indirect nuclear talks in Geneva has intensified concerns that the U.S. and Iran could be edging toward a broader conflict. With American aircraft carriers and warships deployed across the Middle East, Iranian officials warned that any U.S. strike would trigger retaliation across the region, including against Israel and U.S. bases.

Iran’s foreign minister described the moment as one of the most dangerous in years, saying a military confrontation would engulf multiple countries. Analysts note that both sides appear wary of war but remain locked in a stalemate over enrichment, sanctions, and the scope of future negotiations.

Oil prices have already risen amid fears of escalation, and diplomats say the next round of talks may be the last chance to avoid a wider crisis.

Iran and the U.S. ended another tense round of indirect nuclear negotiations in Geneva without a breakthrough, deepening fears that the standoff could slide toward open conflict. Mediators from Oman described “significant progress,” but Tehran reiterated that it will continue enriching uranium and will not ship material abroad—two core U.S. demands.

Iranian officials said their position had been clearly communicated and that sanctions relief remains essential to any agreement. Washington, meanwhile, is pressing for strict limits on enrichment and wants Iran to address its missile program and support for regional armed groups, issues Tehran refuses to discuss.

Lower‑level technical talks are expected to resume next week in Vienna, where the International Atomic Energy Agency would play a central role in verifying any deal.
