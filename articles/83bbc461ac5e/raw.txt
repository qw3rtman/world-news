# Elite US troops may launch ‘snatch raid’ on Iran’s nuclear stockpile

## The Delta Force could attempt a covert mission to seize enriched uranium, providing Tehran has not already moved it to a secret location

Satellite images show the craters from America’s bombs that targeted Iran’s underground Fordow uranium enrichment facility last yearMAXAR TECHNOLOGIES/AFP/GETTY IMAGES

Larisa Brown, Defence Editor

Wednesday March 11 2026, 10.35pm GMT, The Times

Movements in and out of the Isfahan nuclear facility, where most of Iran’s enriched uranium is believed to be stored, are being constantly monitored by American spy agencies.

There may be a narrow window of opportunity in which it could be retrieved, it is believed, if it has not been moved already.

To do so, US special forces would probably have to mount a so-called “snatch operation”. If President Trump is serious about a ground operation to seize what is left of Iran’s nuclear programme, a rapid, high-stakes covert mission will have been long in the planning.

President Trump, Steve Witkoff and Pete Hegseth on board Air Force OneSAUL LOEB/AFP/Getty Images

Chris Steele, a former intelligence officer for MI6, said: “It will not be easy. [The uranium] will be dispersed and if the Iranians had any sense they would have prepared contingencies.”

Intelligence would be key to the success of any mission, said a source with knowledge and experience of such operations. The source told The Times: “[The Iranians] could be moving it every 24 hours or it might be sat in an underground bunker.” Securing the nuclear stockpile is thought to have already been war-gamed by the Pentagon.

The operation would be likely to involve elite troops, possibly from Delta Force, the US military’s special mission unit, who may have created a replica of the storage facility to practise entry.

Once the mission has been approved, an advance party could parachute in under cover of darkness and secure a path to the entrance points. Then, helicopters would arrive, bringing with them the rest of the troops. They may need to be accompanied by specialist scientists who could explain how to handle the material. If anything goes wrong, the consequences could be deadly. The helicopters would have to fly either at a very high or very low altitude to avoid enemy radar.

“A massive air armada would sit above to protect the troops by killing radar systems and air defence. Everything will get zapped so it is a clean run for the aircraft that drops the troops,” the source said, adding the mission would, in its initial stage, be similar to the raid on the Venezuelan president Nicolás Maduro’s safe house in January.

But entering Iran’s underground sites would be far more complex, especially if the entrance tunnels have been blocked. Special forces would require excavating equipment and may have to operate in protective gear if any leak of the gaseous uranium is detected.

MAXAR TECHNOLOGIES/AFP/GETTY IMAGES

It would be a perilous mission, even if there were a joint US-Israeli force with large numbers of troops to defend the perimeter.

The Iranian regime has not allowed any inspection at Isfahan, where most of the uranium is believed to be stored, nor at Fordow and Natanz, the main sites for enrichment, which may also hold small amounts of uranium.

All three were seriously damaged last June and satellite images show some work compatible with securing them but no sign of any serious attempt to reconstitute the programme. Most of the enrichment centrifuges were believed to be destroyed, while the uranium, stored in gaseous form in canisters, was thought to be buried deep under rubble.

Missile attacks caused “severe damage” to an irradiation sterilisation facility in Isfahan on Saturday, Iran’s ISNA news agency reported.

Iran is believed to have 440kg of uranium enriched to 60 per cent, enough if increased to 90 per cent to build 11 nuclear warheads.

Not even the International Atomic Energy Agency knows the exact location of the entire stockpile, which it had previously verified, or whether it is retrievable.

Other sites may also have uranium. The Iranians were working on a back-up facility near Natanz, 80m to 100m underneath Pickaxe Mountain, which was not targeted last year.

Trump said on Monday that Iran had been “trying to reconstitute its weapons programme at a different site” before the war, “protected by granite” — a possible reference to the mountain.

Israel claims to have evidence of secret sites it is now targeting. Taleghan 2, a weaponisation test site, was hit by Israel in 2024 but the Iranians are reported to have been trying to rebuild it.

“It was a total obliteration, they haven’t been able to get to it,” Trump said on Air Force One when asked about sending in US troops. “At some point maybe we will. It would be a great thing. But right now we’re just decimating them. But we haven’t gone in. It’s something we can do later on.”

Trump has made ending Iran’s nuclear weapon ambitions one of the US aims but there are no easy options.
