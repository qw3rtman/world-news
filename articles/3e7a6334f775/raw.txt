# Could Ayatollah Khamenei’s death mark the end of the Islamic Revolution?

## Killing the supreme leader has left the Islamic Republic in its death throes, sparking Shia protests and re-introducing a religious element to geopolitics

Ayatollah Ali Khamenei took over as supreme leader in 1989

MOHSEN SHANDIZ/GETTY IMAGES

Roger Boyes

Sunday March 01 2026, 4.45pm, The Times

Killing the supreme leader of Iran is not comparable to the removal of the head of any other hostile state. It could be the Islamic Republic itself that is in its death throes, and it won’t be a pretty sight. Expect a decade of upheaval across the Middle East as radical Shia, denied their chance of regional leadership out of Tehran, disrupt other fragile states.

Ayatollah Ali Khamenei had been the supreme leader since 1989, taking over from the more charismatic and spiritually authoritative Ruhollah Khomeini. The country was wounded after losing its long, bloody war against Iraq and as the Assembly of Experts was summoned to decide on Khomeini’s successor with a sense of urgency, it looked as if Iraq was getting ready to exploit the paralysing public grief and attack Iran once again.

Khamenei was to be given extended powers over the country’s military and security forces, gaining control over a new national security council. The point was for Khamenei to make the Iranian people feel safe again. In fact, the confusing, complex overlap between being the spiritual guardian of the Islamic revolution and the builder of a fortress state has proved to be Iran’s undoing.

Ruhollah Khomeini, Khamenei’s predecessor, waves to a crowd of supporters from the window of his Tehran home

The intimate relationship between the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC), with its layers of corruption, and the supreme leader made for a toxic theocracy. It killed Khamenei’s Islamic revolution as surely as the bombs that blew up his command bunker at the weekend.

The problem is that Khamenei, as the senior Shia religious authority, had influence well beyond Iran. For millions of Shia across the world — in Iraq, Lebanon, the Gulf and South Asia — he embodied their religious legitimacy and ideological certainty.

Hence the violence against the US consulate in Karachi on Sunday which led to nine pro-Iranian protesters being killed. Protesters tried to storm the US consulate in Baghdad; thousands took to the streets in Indian-administered Kashmir.

A police officer fires tear gas to disperse a protest condemning the killing of Khamenei near the US consulate in Karachi

ALI RAZA/AP

Crowds also celebrated Khamenei’s death in southern Iran

It seems that rather than clinically removing an obstacle to the quashing of Iran’s nuclear programme, as the US and Israel had intended, the killing of Khamenei has re-introduced a complicating religious element to geopolitics. The conflict has become less about deterrence and more about identity, faith and retribution.

That is what President Pezeshkian of Iran meant when he called for “revenge” — not the piecemeal and inexpertly-executed attacks on luxury hotels in the Gulf states but rather something that will set the wider region on fire. The supreme leader, who died with some of his family members and army commanders, has already been declared a martyr and therefore an inspiration for terrorist assaults, either freelance or orchestrated.

Protesters in front of Israel’s consulate in Istanbul

OZAN KOSE/GETTY IMAGES

President Pezeshkian of Iran has called for “revenge”

SHUTTERSTOCK

Changing a regime from the air was always going to be a messy gamble. Sharp US intelligence co-operation with Israel ensured that the supreme leader was taken out in the first wave of attacks but it will not be clear for a few days, when the damage assessments are in, whether Iran’s missile sites have been badly damaged.

Advertisement

Norman Roule, a veteran Iran-watcher for the US government, argues that “Iran now has the political space to bring about the change that its people, the region and the world have sought for so many years”. But there’s some way to go yet.

First, the opposition has to agree on a common platform. There are some interesting overlaps between the manifesto presented by Reza Pahlavi, the exiled son of the shah, and that of Maryam Rajavi, the exiled president-elect of the left-leaning National Council of Resistance.

Although Rajavi is as critical as ever of what she says are “the remnants of monarchical fascism that seek to steal the democratic revolution”, both seem to agree on keeping religion out of politics, on engaging the youth, giving women greater rights and, as a first step, the IRGC and its associated paramilitaries laying down their arms. The still rather utopian aim of a political process leading to free and monitored elections for a constitutional assembly, leading to the foundation of a democratic state has come a step closer.

But for it to become a reality, the IRGC has to understand that it is no longer the legitimate armed instrument of an all-powerful clerical leadership. That assumption which allowed many commanders to grow rich, profiting even from the western sanctions that were supposed to restrict its activities, fell apart the moment that Khamenei perished in the rubble of his bunker.

The regime has not collapsed yet but the chains of command have. Now is the time for the IRGC officers to make their choice: to abandon the dysfunctional establishment that gave them a comfortable life in the profession of violence, or to pretend that nothing important has happened and wait for a new democratic broom to put them in the dock. If nothing else, President Trump’s bombs have crystallised that choice.
