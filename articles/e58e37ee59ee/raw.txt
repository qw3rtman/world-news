# Kingmakers: What the Greens or Lib Dems would demand of the SNP

## As polls predict a parliament of minorities after May’s elections, the two parties most likely to do a deal have set out their priorities for sharing power

Gillian Mackay and Ross Greer, co-leaders of the Scottish Greens, and Alex Cole-Hamilton, the Scottish Lib Dem leader

Daniel Sanderson

, Scottish Political Editor |

John Boothman

, Political Correspondent

Saturday February 21 2026, 7.00pm, The Sunday Times

The leader of the Scottish Liberal Democrats, at least according to Andrew Bowie, the shadow Scottish secretary, was “already measuring the curtains in the deputy first minister’s office”.

Alex Cole-Hamilton had made clear that his party was willing to back the SNP’s budget for a second year in a row, in what some saw as a prelude to an unprecedented cross-constitutional coalition at Holyrood following May’s elections.

However, the suggestion of a formal SNP/Lib Dem deal has just been emphatically shut down.

“I would resign as leader of my party before that happened,” Cole-Hamilton told The Sunday Times, as the Scottish Lib Dems held their spring conference this weekend. “And I wouldn’t vote them [the SNP] back into government.”

It would, then, take a U-turn of Nick Clegg proportions to revive the prospect. It leaves the Scottish Greens, once again, as the SNP’s only willing political bedfellow.

However, in interviews with the politicians whom polls consistently suggest will be Holyrood’s two most likely kingmakers after May 7 in another parliament of minorities, Ross Greer, the Green co-leader, made clear that he would demand a high price for a rekindled partnership.

Cole-Hamilton and Sir Ed Davey, leader of the Lib Dems, on stage at the party’s spring conference on Saturday

JEFF J MITCHELL/GETTY IMAGES

These would include radical tax hikes and new wealth taxes of the type John Swinney would likely find unconscionable. He pledged to govern from the “moderate centre left” when he took over in the wake of the acrimonious collapse of their last coalition, in what was widely seen at the time as an attempt to end the SNP’s reliance on Greens.

If the first minister fails to secure an outright Holyrood majority, then it appears most likely he would head a minority government for another five years.

And at least for some party figures, an informal alliance with the Lib Dems, rather than leaving himself at the mercy of what is likely to be a more sizable bloc of Greens, is by far the preferable option.

“Alex Cole-Hamilton has to say he’s totally ruling it out this side of the election,” one SNP source said. “But what if the alternative is Anas [Sarwar] relying on Lib Dems and 20-something Reform MSPs to back him up? I would suggest that is very problematic for a liberal party.

“Swinney’s strategy of suggesting a majority is needed for an independence referendum could actually be key here. Fail to get that majority and it’s off the agenda for the foreseeable. Would this allow the Lib Dems more latitude to consider either an informal working arrangement on budgets every year, or something more solid? I’d hope so.

“There should be no reason for two moderate to centre-left parties not to work together. In fact we should actively lock the extremes — Reform and the Greens — out of power”.

## ‘We’re prepared to be grown-ups’

Cole-Hamilton, while shutting down the prospect of a formal coalition or voting for Swinney to be retained as first minister, did not rule out backing the SNP in future budgets.

Under Alex Salmond, after all, an informal alliance with the Tories kept the SNP’s first minority administration from collapse.

Advertisement

“In a parliament of minorities, we’re prepared to be grown-ups and reach for the better nature of our opponents,” Cole-Hamilton said. “In the final analysis, when the numbers are clear, we will make an assessment of what’s in the best interests of this country. But I’ve made it clear that is not a third decade of the SNP.”

The Edinburgh MSP, however, who this weekend revealed ten target constituencies, is also pursuing a second-vote strategy that would address the party’s underperformance on regional lists, and has not given up hope entirely of entering government.

He insists there is still a “line of sight” to the Lib Dems forming an administration with Scottish Labour, which would require, at the very least, a unionist majority at Holyrood.

‘Sarwar could theoretically become first minister even if Labour had far fewer seats than the SNP, should pro-UK MSPs outnumber the SNP and Greens and all of them, including Reform, rallied behind the Scottish Labour leader.

At present, polls suggest it is unlikely. But with an unpredictable election campaign yet to officially begin, it is far from impossible.

“I don’t leap out of bed every morning thinking ‘how do I become deputy first minister?’” Cole-Hamilton claimed.

“But I don’t fear it either. With the levers of power, you get to do big and liberal things. I didn’t get into politics just to send angry press releases from the opposition benches forever.”

He cites education and a mobile phone ban in schools, reforms to social care to unblock the NHS and improved access to GPs and dentists, as well as faster progress on dualling the A9, as “priorities” for the Lib Dems in the next parliament.

While they are areas in which the record of the SNP is, at best, patchy, they are also ones in which Swinney’s administration has at least expressed a willingness to take action. The centrist, relatively uncontroversial wishlist, is one the first minister could easily sign up to.

## ‘The Lib Dems are the cheaper date’

For Greer, however, he cites radical goals such as universal free bus travel, a huge overhaul to council tax that politicians have repeatedly shied away from, and punishing new wealth taxes on the “super-rich” and powerful corporations.

“The Lib Dems are the cheaper date,” Greer said. “They’ll be asking for a lot less full stop. They’re not radical, they’re not a challenging party. They pride themselves on being a party of the centre ground. Scotland doesn’t need that kind of bland status quo politics.”

He said the Scottish Greens have already “written” Scotland’s income tax system, thanks to the previous coalition and budget deals, and now want to focus on reforms to other taxes.

Mackay and Greer

JEFF J MITCHELL/GETTY IMAGES

These would include land and buildings transactions tax, the Scottish equivalent to stamp duty, and business rates, as well as scrapping and replacing the council tax.

“It’s absolutely right to say there would be winners and losers from any changes to the tax system, but there are winners and losers right now,” Greer said.

“The winners of the current system are the wealthier and the losers are poorer people. I don’t think you can justify continuing that.

“We’re not afraid to say we’ll tax the super-rich to provide the public services that everybody else needs to get by. That to us is the only honest position to take. John Swinney understands our position. We don’t arse the government about.

“Clearly, you can get more done when you’re in government than in opposition. The caveat to that is we would have no interest in going back into government unless we were confident we would be able to actually deliver on the policies Greens were elected on.”

A Swinney deal with the Lib Dems, he claims, would make the internal SNP backlash seen to Sturgeon’s green deal look like small beer, given what it would mean for the independence cause.

“As someone who passionately believes in independence, I would be really worried about that sort of coalition,” Greer said. “I’ve spent my entire adult life campaigning for Scotland to be an independent country, just like John Swinney has.

“The Lib Dems, quite legitimately, are vehemently anti-independence. They have every right to make that one of their red lines. But if you had a hypothetical government that crosses the constitutional boundary, I think that can only set the cause of independence back.

“For the SNP to form a government with a party who will clearly demand that preparations for independence stop, I’ve got to presume is an intolerable position for most SNP members.

“But that’s a choice for John Swinney to make.”
