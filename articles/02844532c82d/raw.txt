Meeting this week for the first time in 2026, the Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) declined to lower the federal funds rate.

In a post-meeting statement, the FOMC described economic activity expanding “at a solid pace,” as a factor in its decision to maintain the target rate at a range of 3.50% to 3.75%. “Job gains have remained low, and the unemployment rate has shown some signs of stabilization,” the committee continued. Inflation, though, “remains somewhat elevated.”

Adjusting the fed funds rate determines how much interest banks charge each other for maintaining reserves. That, in turn, impacts the cost of consumer borrowing, including credit cards, personal loans and auto financing.

With continued economic growth and a stable job market, plus the threat of a recession declining, experts have predicted two quarter-point cuts later in the year, according to the latest CNBC Fed survey. That would bring the benchmark rate to a range of 3.0% to 3.25% by 2027.

## What happens when the Fed cuts its rate?

The federal funds rate is the Fed’s primary tool for influencing the economy. It determines how much banks can charge each other when borrowing or lending excess reserves. That, in turn, influences the interest rates they charge consumers.

When the Federal Reserve lowers the fed funds rate, borrowing generally becomes cheaper. After several cuts, interest rates on credit cards, auto loans and personal loans can decline noticeably. That often spurs consumers to apply for new loans or refinancing.

Mortgage rates are not immune to rate cuts or increases, but they are more closely linked to 10-year Treasury bond yields.

Rate cuts also influence what savers earn: When banks adjust returns to reflect a lower rate environment, yields on CDs, high-yield savings accounts and money market accounts often fall. Conversely, if the Fed raises rates, CDs and HYSAs become more attractive to savers.

## Interest rates FAQ

### What is the federal funds rate?

The federal funds rate is the target interest rate set by the Federal Reserve. It dictates the interest that commercial banks charge each other to lend extra reserves overnight. That, in turn, impacts the rates these institutions charge for credit cards, loans and other financial products.

### When will interest rates go down again?

The next Federal Open Market Committee meeting is Mar 17 and 18, 2026. On Jan. 28, the CME Group’s FedWatch Tool gave just 11.4% odds of a quarter-point cut then. Many experts forecast the next rate cut for the second half of 2026, potentially in June.

### How does the federal funds rate affect inflation?

The Federal Reserve attempts to curb inflation by raising the benchmark interest rate, thereby raising borrowing costs and discouraging consumer spending. That, in turn, prompts retailers to lower prices to retain customers. As a result, overall inflation subsides and the economy slows down.

Slower economic activity can lead to rising unemployment, however, or even a recession. When that appears to be a concern, the Federal Reserve may lower its target rate to boost the economy.
