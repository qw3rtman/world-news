# How will the war in Iran end? The scenarios explained

## As the US and Israel escalate strikes, four paths emerge for the Middle East: regime survival, regime adjustment, regime change — or chaos

President Trump has threatened to send troops into Iran, raising the prospect of a full-blown regime change operation such as that which the US carried out in Iraq, and which he had previously promised to avoid.

Trump has sent conflicting messages since the start of the joint US-Israeli operation against Iran on Saturday morning, at times suggesting it could last just a few days and at others saying he is prepared to continue bombing for four or five weeks or even longer. His interview with the New York Post on Monday was the first time he had raised the possibility of “boots on the ground”.

“I don’t have the yips with respect to boots on the ground,” he said. “Like every president says, ‘There will be no boots on the ground’ — I don’t say it. I say, ‘Probably don’t need them,’ [or] ‘if they were necessary’.”

He also hinted that it was the claimed success of the mission that had emboldened him to push further. He said that the original intention had been to spend four weeks or so killing the regime’s leadership. “We’re right on schedule, way ahead of schedule in terms of leadership — 49 killed — and that was, you know, going to take, we figured, at least four weeks — and we did it in one day,” he said.

In a separate interview with CNN he said he intended to escalate the attacks rather than wind them down. “We haven’t even started hitting them hard,” he said. “The big wave hasn’t even happened. The big one is coming soon.”

What he has yet to explain clearly is what “a big one” might be and what form he sees Iran’s governance taking after the war is over.

A missile launch from a US ship

At times he has indicated that his only concern is to stop Iran developing a nuclear weapon. In that case, a new leadership from within the regime that was prepared to do a nuclear deal with Washington in return for being allowed to retain the basic shape of the Islamic Republic might be acceptable to him.

It would also, presumably, have to guarantee that it would end its support for anti-Israeli militias across the Middle East, which would be a minimum requirement for the acquiescence of Binyamin Netanyahu, the Israeli prime minister. Trump now says these are his goals, along with ending Iran’s ballistic missile capability and “annihilating the navy”.

That would, however, disappoint the thousands of Iranian activists in exile who have given support to the operation in the hope of seeing one of their favoured candidates — particularly Reza Pahlavi, the son of the late shah — oversee a new democracy in Tehran.

It would also risk being seen as a betrayal of the thousands — possibly tens of thousands — of people killed after taking to the streets of Iran earlier this year to call for the regime’s fall, in part encouraged by bellicose rhetoric from Washington.

Pete Hegseth, the US secretary of war, also hinted that Trump’s goals were not so ambitious, contrasting the Iran operation with the war on Iraq in 2003, an explicit attempt by President Bush to introduce democracy to the country.

He also said that ground troops were not being ruled out. “We’re not going to go into the exercise of what we will or will not do,” he told reporters. He said the war could last “four weeks, two weeks, six weeks”.

But he added: “No stupid rules of engagement, no nation-building quagmire, no democracy-building exercise. No politically correct wars. We fight to win and we don’t waste time or lives. This is not Iraq.”

That leaves several different possibilities open for the future of Iran.

## Regime survival

The precedents for a regime being brought down even by the might of the United States using an air campaign alone are few. It remains clearly possible instead that Trump and Netanyahu could eventually stop the bombing, declaring that the destruction of the nuclear programme, missile bases, much of the military hierarchy and the top clerical leadership represented victory.

In the meantime other regime leaders could be selected by the surviving apparatus to take over. A three-man leadership committee has already been named, including President Pezeshkian; the hardline cleric Ayatollah Alireza Arafi; and the chief justice, Gholam-Hossein Mohseni-Eje’i. Arafi said on Monday that he hoped a new supreme leader would be chosen quickly.

He is himself a possible nominee. Others include Mojtaba Khamenei, the son of the killed supreme leader, Ayatollah Ali Khamenei; Hassan Khomeini, the grandson of the Islamic Republic’s founding father, Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini; and Ayatollah Mohammad Mehdi Mirbagheri, another hardline cleric.

The name of Hassan Rouhani has been frequently mentioned. The former president — who agreed the 2015 nuclear deal with President Obama — is widely disliked by both hardliners, who regard him as too soft, and the general public, who remember his failed economic policies.

That would appear to make him an unlikely choice, unless the regime was trying to signal that it was open to a better relationship with the West.

## Regime adjustment

When American troops seized the Venezuelan leader Nicolás Maduro, it ended the opposition’s hopes of regime change. Instead, his vice-president, Delcy Rodríguez, apparently in co-ordination with the US, took over, keeping hold of the same organs of power but agreeing to certain demands of Trump’s on political posture and oil supplies. It is still unclear whether she will remain president for the longer term, or what a Rodríguez presidency would look like.

There is no sign yet of a similar figure emerging in Iran. Ali Larijani, the head of the national security council, a veteran former nuclear negotiator, and all-round “fixer” for the regime, has notably not been killed by the bombing.

His is a name often cited as one “we can do business with”, as Margaret Thatcher famously described Mikhail Gorbachev in the 1980s. But on the surface he remains a hardline critic of western and Israeli power, railing against Trump’s “delusional fantasies”.

“We will fiercely defend ourselves and our 6,000-year-old civilisation regardless of the costs and make the enemies sorry for their miscalculation,” he said on Tuesday afternoon.

## Regime change

When Iranians rose up in protest across the country in January there was no doubt in the mind of their supporters, including much of the US political establishment, that they were seeking to topple the regime and replace it with a pro-western democracy.

That, the supporters went on, was an endeavour behind which President Trump should throw the weight of American power.

It is still their preferred outcome. But Trump has sought to play down expectations of such a positive result, while Netanyahu has been noticeably quiet about the shape of future governance.

What he has done is show moral support for Pahlavi, who has appeared publicly in Washington and Israel offering himself as at least an interim leader. He says he would shepherd Iran towards a constitutional monarchy.

However, his father’s regime remains deeply unpopular with many Iranians, not just the remaining supporters of the Islamic regime, though they are probably no more than a significant minority.

The Mojahedin-e-Khalq (MEK), also known in English as the People’s Mojahedin Organisation of Iran, is another group that has won widespread support from western politicians, particularly on the right. Its public face, Maryam Rajavi, with her Islamic headscarf, sometimes appears on the surface more representative of “ordinary Iranians” — as opposed to the liberal, westernised urban women who flock to Pahlavi rallies.

However, that hints at the MEK’s unorthodox political history as a revolutionary adherent of Islamic Marxism. It says it has forsworn its terrorist origins and now supports democracy, and undoubtedly has some support in Iran. Whether Rajavi or Pahlavi or any other figure could rally Iranians into a unitary, pro-western democracy remains plausible but open to doubt.

## Collapse, break-up, chaos

Iran is not like Iraq, which proved after the 2003 invasion to be a sectarian tinderbox. It has an overwhelming Shia majority. While Sunni militant groups have staged attacks there, they do not have enough support to tear the country apart in a similar way to Iran’s Arab neighbour.

However, that does not mean that opposition to the Islamic Republic’s rule in Tehran could not, in the right circumstances, turn violent.

In the east, an ethnic Baloch insurgency with some Sunni jihadist characteristics has been rumbling for decades. It has staged large bombings, including of Shia mosques. In the north, there is a large Kurdish population, with radical dissident movements, some armed, some affiliated with the smorgasbord of groups that make up the wider Kurdish political scene, including Turkey’s proscribed guerrilla group the PKK. Kurdish towns have been among the leaders of recent rounds of anti-regime protest.

There are also ethnic Arab areas, largely Shia but some Sunni, as well as an Azeri Shia population, largely but not entirely integrated into the broader population. If order broke down with continued bombing of police stations, revolutionary guard headquarters, and other security forces, separatist, opposition or other radical factions could emerge from the chaos.
