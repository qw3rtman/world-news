# The Russia-Ukraine war in maps and charts — four years on

See how since 2022 the front lines have changed, the numbers of civilian and military deaths, how Ukrainians have been displaced and more

Marieta Marinova

|

George Willoughby

Tuesday February 24 2026, 5.50pm, The Times

Marieta Marinova

|

George Willoughby

Tuesday February 24 2026, 5.50pm, The Times

Four years after Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine, hundreds of thousands of lives have been lost, billions of dollars of aid has been sent, and — for most of that time — the front line has largely been frozen.

Donald Trump pledged to end the conflict within a day of becoming president, yet a year later, despite the latest round of peace talks, attacks on Ukraine’s energy grid persist. These maps and charts tell the story of the four-year war.

## How the invasion unfolded

In 2014, Russia seized 8 per cent of Ukraine’s territory including Crimea and the Donbas. This proportion increased after the invasion on February 24, 2022, and reached 27 per cent by March of that year, according to the Institute for the Study of War (ISW). But a Ukrainian counter-offensive drove Russian tanks away from Kyiv and other territories, and by the autumn, the share of territory held by Russia fell to 18 per cent.

In the following three years Russian advances ground to a halt. In its most prominent offensives since 2024, Russia has been advancing between 15 and 70 metres per day, according to the Center for Strategic and International Studies (CSIS) — slower than most major offensives in any war over the last century.

## How many people have been killed?

According to the UN Human Rights Office, more than 15,000 Ukrainian civilians have been killed by Russian activity since February 2022, and more than 41,000 have been injured.

Although the first year of the war was the most deadly — more than 4,300 civilians were killed in March 2022 alone — for the whole of last year there were 2,500 deaths. This includes 312 in July 2025, when Trump set a deadline of “ten or 12 days” for Russia to reach a peace deal or face tough new sanctions.

The number of military deaths is more contested. In a rare public disclosure this month, President Zelensky said that “officially on the battlefield, the number of military personnel killed, whether career soldiers or people who were mobilised, is 55,000”.

Other estimates are far higher: The CSIS puts Ukrainian military deaths as of December at between 100,000 and 140,000, with total casualties (including killed, wounded and missing) at between 500,000 and 600,000.

By all measures, however, Russian losses have been greater. The CSIS suggests Russian casualties are about 1.2 million, including 325,000 deaths. Recent estimates by BBC News Russian and Mediazona, an independent Russian current affairs orgnaisation, suggest 177,000 have been killed.

Separately, Russia has been struggling to offset its losses with recruitment. Last year casualties exceeded 415,000 and 406,000 people were recruited, according to the Kremlin.

At this rate, combined casualties from both sides could reach two million by the end of spring 2026, according to the CSIS.

## How has the war changed?

Following prolonged periods of minimal territorial gain, both countries have shifted their strategies. Drones are now responsible for up to 80 per cent of injuries or deaths, according to Latvia’s Constitution Protection Bureau intelligence service.

Kherson, on the banks of the Dnipro River, has become a hotspot for drone warfare. According to Tochnyi, a collective project reporting on the invasion, some 5,300 civilians have been hit by drone strikes in the Kherson region since January 2024.

Ukraine’s power grid has increasingly become a target. Figures from the non-profit organisation Armed Conflict Location and Event Data show there were 978 Russian attacks on energy infrastructure in 2025, almost triple the 337 in 2022.

Ukraine has been enduring repeated power outages as temperatures drop below zero. The country has been forced to increase electricity imports to keep the supply stable. Following strikes in January, imports surged to nearly 900 GWh, the highest level since the launch of the new electricity market in July 2019. Exports have been halted since November last year.

## Where have Ukrainians gone?

Millions of Ukrainians fled abroad in the first months of the war. Four years later, 5.9 million people remain outside the country, according to the UN Refugee Agency, while another 3.7 million are internally displaced.

Most refugees are in Europe (5.3 million), with a further 549,090 recorded elsewhere. Germany had taken in more than 1.3 million as of December, and Poland 980,000. There are about 264,000 refugees in the UK, according to the latest figures from June.

The majority (61 per cent) of refugees said they were planning or hoping to return home when last surveyed by the UNHCR between July and August 2024. However, this was a significant decrease from 83 per cent in the early months of the conflict between August and September 2022.

## How has life in Ukraine changed?

Inflation was already high at around 11 per cent in the months leading up to the invasion, but it accelerated sharply afterwards. Inflation peaked with a 26.6 per cent year-on-year increase in October 2022, driven by the destruction of production facilities, disrupted supply chains and higher costs for businesses as a result of the war. Although it eased during the following year, electricity shortages and labour constraints sent prices up again in 2024, before inflation slowed to 7.4 per cent in January this year.

Meanwhile, Russia experienced two major surges in inflation — one immediately after sanctions were imposed in April 2022, when inflation hit 17.8 per cent, and another in March last year, when it reached 10.3 per cent. After the immediate shock of sanctions and the withdrawal of hundreds of western companies, the country’s economy proved more resilient than many expected. Increased military spending helped keep factories running, so output per person rose 20 per cent from $40,900 (£30,370) in 2022 to $49,000 (£36,400) in 2025.

## Who has been supporting Ukraine?

International bilateral aid sent to Ukraine had reached €343bn (£300bn) by December, with at least 41 countries providing support, according to the Kiel Institute for the World Economy. Nearly half of this, €162bn (£141bn), was military aid, including weapons, equipment and training.

The US had been the single largest donor, contributing €115bn (£100bn), but bilateral aid almost entirely stopped last year. Military support coming from Europe rose by 67 per cent in 2025 compared with the 2022-24 average, while financial and humanitarian aid grew by 59 per cent.

## Is Ukraine receiving enough?

According to the Centre for Economic Strategy, the aid disbursed last year covered 56 per cent of Ukraine’s financing needs, compared with 72 per cent and 71 per cent in 2023 and 2022 respectively. One of the main sources of aid this year is expected to be the €90 billion (£78.6bn) support loan package, which the European parliament approved earlier this month. Of this, €60 billion (£52.4bn) will be dedicated to strengthening Ukraine’s defence, while the rest is meant for budget support.
