# Iran crisis: Three ways this could end

Analysis by

Brett H. McGurk

Brett McGurk is a CNN global affairs analyst who served in senior national security positions under Presidents George W. Bush, Barack Obama, Donald Trump and Joe Biden.

From one moment to the next, this Iran crisis appears to be cascading in ways that are increasingly uncertain for governments, investors and ordinary citizens alike. Much now turns on the individual decisions of unpredictable leaders, including President Donald Trump, and now perhaps Iran’s new and untested supreme leader, Mojtaba Khamenei.

On Monday, Trump sent mixed signals, calling the war a “short-term excursion” that could end “soon,” while also saying it should not end before Iran “no longer [has] any capacity whatsoever for a very long period of time of developing weaponry that could be used against the US, Israel or any of our allies.”

So where is this all headed? Inside the White House, during a crisis we would often draw on experts from across the US government to model potential end-state scenarios with a base (most likely) case, best-case and worst-case outcome.

Here is how that analysis might look, based on public information and reporting.

## Military campaign needs time

US Navy aircraft fly over the USS Abraham Lincoln in support of Operation Epic Fury on March 3.

US Navy/Handout/Reuters

In any scenario, the military campaign needs weeks to be completed. The military objective as described by the Pentagon is to degrade Iran’s ability to project power outside its borders. That means striking Iran’s missiles and missile production facilities, drones and drone manufacturing facilities, naval forces, air forces, command and control structures, and what’s left of its nuclear program.

Degrading a country’s military capacity is a viable military mission, and it’s separate from whatever political outcome the United States and Israel might hope to see in Iran. It takes time, however, and military planners are likely telling Trump that while the operation is on track and perhaps ahead of schedule, it requires weeks at least to be carried through.

Notably, officials at a Pentagon briefing on Tuesday refused to discuss timeframes, and the White House confirmed the original timeframe as being four to six weeks. As a planning presumption, we can presume the military operation has a couple of more weeks to run, at least.

## Economic and political variables

Scenario planning is only as good as the facts that are known at the time. In this case, we will presume that Iran’s new supreme leader remains alive, and there are no further political shake-ups in Tehran. We can also presume energy prices remain elevated, even if ships begin to transit the Strait of Hormuz once again.

So long as a conflict is continuing in the heart of a vital transit route, there will be economic aftershocks felt globally. Iran is aiming to increase economic pain to influence Trump to call the war short, something the president has rejected.

Based on these assumptions, here are the three scenarios to consider as a landing spot over the coming period, with my projected percentages for each:

## 1. Base case (60%): Contained Iran — think Iraq in the 1990s

On the base-case scenario, Trump provides the military the time it needs to complete this defined mission of degrading Iran’s power projection. This means the US together with its allies and partners can do enough to contain economic shocks, and that the president remains committed to the mission he ordered.

This scenario presumes by the end of this month that Iran’s power projection capacity and defense industrial base is significantly degraded but that its political structures remain intact. The high-intensity military campaign would cease — having met its defined objective — but without promise of regime change in Tehran.

From there, US and international sanctions would remain firmly in place against Iran, unless and until its new government agreed to stop pursuing a nuclear program and give up its long-range ballistic missile program. Both of those programs remain under UN Security Council sanctions, and that will continue.

Militarily, the US and Israel — and possibly other partners — would continue to patrol Iran’s skies with limited risk given Iran’s lack of air defenses. If Tehran sought to restore its missile, drone or nuclear programs, it could be struck at will, thereby deterring such moves.

This base case over the coming months and even years might resemble Iraq in the 1990s: weakened, contained and with American pilots overhead to deter future threats. That would not promise regime change in Iran, but given the political unrest in the country, we should anticipate future protests and an eroding regime apparatus over time.

## 2. Worst case (30%): Emboldened Iran — worse than where we started

Demonstrators gather with Iranian national flags for a rally in support of the new supreme leader Mojtaba Khamenei, in Tehran on Monday.

Majid Saeedi/Getty Images

The worst case would see economic shocks force Trump’s hand to declare victory prematurely before the military campaign has run its course. This would leave behind an Iran with power structures reconsolidated — embittered and emboldened — and with its military and nuclear capacity intact enough to be reconstituted. The region would be left even less stable, as the Gulf states would remain under constant threat of an Iran with expanding missile and drone capabilities, which Tehran has proved willing to use.

This scenario might draw the United States deeper into the Middle East, required to further support the defenses of Gulf partners after confronting thousands of missiles and drones, and with the US military campaign pulled short before destroying those capabilities. The costs of doing business in the region — from shipping insurance to long-term capital deployment — could rise sharply before a new equilibrium develops. That new equilibrium would also presume a strengthening Iran with empowered hardliners.

President Dwight D. Eisenhower remarked that no commander in chief should consider decisive military action before all the facts are laid out “cold and hard” given that wars, once started, proceed unpredictably and with unintended consequences. The economic swings now unfolding were predicted in any Iran war scenario, across presidential administrations. The Trump administration — and thus my base case — projects determination to carry the campaign to conclusion despite those shocks, but the president also signals (“short-term excursion”) that a reverse gear may come at any moment.

A decision to reverse course before the US military completes the task it was given would risk a more unstable regional balance. Whatever the merit of launching this war, an early halt to the campaign would risk leaving Iran’s regime emboldened and the region and the world in an even more precarious position.

## 3. Best case (10%): New Iran — and a new Middle East

A satellite image shows a closer view of the destroyed tunnel entrances at Esfahan missile complex after reported airstrikes Iran on March 8.

Vantor/Reuters

In a best-case scenario, the military pressure on Iran, including strikes against its repressive security apparatus, weakens the regime and strengthens the confidence of Iranians to retake the streets and demand an overthrow of the Islamic Republic. The case might have had better odds if not for the violent crackdown in January, which reportedly killed thousands. In the near term, Iranians may retake the streets in critical mass only if Tehran’s repressive apparatus — the Basij militias and Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps — are significantly and visibly degraded, something that’s difficult to do through air power alone.

History shows that external military pressure alone rarely produces rapid regime collapse without organized internal and armed opposition.

The low odds for this case are inherent in the military plan as it’s been described from the Pentagon, particularly by the chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, Gen. Dan Caine, and the commander of Central Command, Adm. Brad Cooper. The objective is to degrade Iran’s power projection outside of its own borders, not to degrade its ability to exert power inside Tehran — something that would likely require US ground forces or an organized opposition force. Neither of those are likely available in this military campaign, and without them, the likelihood of a near-term regime change in Tehran remain low at best.

## Not mutually exclusive

Iran’s new supreme leader, Mojtaba Khamenei, attends a meeting in Tehran on July 22, 2018.

Mehdi Ghasemi/Wana News Agency/Reuters/File

These three scenarios are not mutually exclusive. Iran’s new supreme leader, Mojtaba Khamenei, is likely to be a figurehead of the IRGC, and it’s not known whether he’ll effectively consolidate power, or give rise to competitors. The Islamic Republic came to power in 1979 in opposition to a system of hereditary power, and Mojtaba’s primary claim to his new post is inheritance from his deceased father.

So, over the longer term, we might witness the steady erosion of the Islamic Republic in any case, and one that gives rise to the Iranian people directing the course of events, finally overcoming the system’s repression. The weakened Iran in my base case could hasten that outcome, but it’s not one to expect over the coming months.

The new Iranian government will also have a vote in any scenario, and it may seek to continue harassing drone and missile attacks in the region — presuming capacity to do so — even after the United States ends major operations.

In any scenario, US engagement with Iran — including deterrence, containment and the possibility of further military action — is unlikely to end when this crisis does. The most likely outcome is not a clean resolution, but a weaker and more contained Iran, with new regional balances of power, and uncertainty as to where this all leads.
