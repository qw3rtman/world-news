As the Iranian government battles on following the death of Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei amid joint U.S. and Israeli strikes across the Islamic Republic, questions are swirling over the future of a nation now in the throes of its greatest moment of uncertainty since the 1979 Islamic Revolution.

Thousands of miles away, Reza Pahlavi —the U.S.-based son of Iran’s last shah, Mohammad Reza Pahlavi, who was toppled in that uprising 47 years ago—believes he has the answer, positioning himself as the natural leader of what he says would be the establishment of a democratic system rather than a return to monarchy.

“I ask you, for now, to remain in your homes and remain calm and safe,” Pahlavi said in a message issued in the opening hours of the U.S.-Israeli bombardment. “Stay alert and ready to return to the streets for the final action at the appropriate time, which I shall communicate to you.”

“Follow my messages through social media. If disruptions occur in the internet and satellite broadcasts, I will remain in contact with you via radio,” he added. “We are very close to final victory. I hope to be with you as soon as possible so that together we may reclaim Iran and rebuild it.”

In some ways, he hopes to find himself in a similar position to Khamenei’s predecessor, the late Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini, who emerged from exile in France to take the reins of Iran from Pahlavi’s father nearly half a century ago.

But rather than popular protests—the likes of which the Islamic Republic weathered in January—it is foreign military intervention that most readily threatens the survival of Iran’s government today. Pahlavi’s support for such action has garnered him criticism from a number of Iranians, including critics of the Islamic Republic who have hoped to seek change from within the system and opposed direct conflict. The disunity among Iran’s opposition further obscures Pahlavi’s path to power, with several other factions actively seeking to discredit his claims.

“I believe that Reza Pahlavi has become a divisive figure,” analyst Alireza Nader told Newsweek. “His political agenda was completely different when I supported him. I fear that the movement around him is becoming increasingly radicalized and has moved to the far right of the political spectrum, only appealing to a narrow section of Iranian society.”

Newsweek reached out to representatives of Pahlavi for comment.

## Rivals To Power

Among the most active of these rivals is the National Council of Resistance of Iran, led by the Mojahedin-e Khalq dissident group. Headed by Maryam Rajavi since the disappearance of her husband in Iraq in 2003, the MeK has a long history of far-left insurgency against both the Shah’s rule and that of the Islamic Republic, even collaborating with then-Iraqi President Saddam Hussein during the 1980s war between the two nations.

The MeK today has shed its Marxist-Leninist leanings and portrays itself as a deeply entrenched network of operatives capable of leaking state secrets and overseeing a democratic transition upon the fall of the Islamic Republic. Rajavi outlined a 10-point plan to guide such a transition shortly after the U.S.-Israeli strikes commenced early on February 28. The platform calls for dismantling Iran’s system of clerical rule, known as velayat-e faqih, in favor of a secular, democratic republic grounded in universal suffrage, political pluralism and full civil liberties, including freedoms of speech, assembly, press and religion, alongside the dissolution of the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps and other security and censorship bodies. It also pledges gender equality, minority autonomy, an independent judiciary and abolition of the death penalty, while promoting a free-market economy with social protections, environmental restoration and a non-nuclear foreign policy centered on “peace, coexistence and international and regional cooperation.”

Pahlavi previously outlined his own democratic vision for Iran in comments shared with Newsweek in early January, as protests swelled across the Islamic Republic, whose deadly crackdown first spurred President Donald Trump’s military threats and three rounds of attempted nuclear negotiations. Pahlavi further discussed his platform during a March 1 interview with CBS News, during which he stated he was “not running for office,” but rather seeking to become a “transitional leader” for Iran.

“I think what today unites us are four core principles that I think is the subject of how we can work together towards that end,” Pahlavi said. “Number one is Iran’s territorial integrity. Number two is a clear separation of religion from state, which is a prerequisite to democracy. And we paid the price understanding what it means to live under a religious dictatorship. Number three is of course equality of all citizens under the law and individual liberties. And most importantly, the process, or democratic process, to allow the people to elect and decide what the future system of governance should be.”

Yet another contender has emerged amid Iran’s unrest this year in the form of a bloc representing one of the nation’s largest ethnic minorities, known as the Coalition of Political Forces of Iranian Kurdistan. This organization brings together five major Kurdish groups, including the Democratic Party of Iranian Kurdistan, or PDKI, Kurdistan Freedom Party, known as PAK, Kurdistan Free Life Party, or PJAK, Organization of Iranian Kurdistan Struggle and Komala of the Toilers of Kurdistan.

Each group has a history of armed struggle, with the PAK claiming attacks against Iranian security forces in January amid the height of the nationwide demonstrations. They have been accused by the Iranian government of separatist tendencies in their bid to promote Kurdish rights and autonomy. Pahlavi has directed similar criticisms toward the new coalition since its establishment, drawing the ire of Kurdish parties and marking yet another schism in Iran’s already fractured opposition landscape.

“I find Pahlavi’s labeling of a new Iranian-Kurdish coalition as ‘separatists’ to be disappointing,” analyst Nader said. “Why would anyone form a coalition with such a radical movement?”

“Pahlavi promised 50,000-100,000 regime defectors before the January uprising. The defection campaign was broadcast by Iran International,” he continued. “What happened with the campaign and the reported defectors?”

Nader argued that it would ultimately be up to the Iranian people themselves to choose their post-Islamic Republic leader, and should Pahlavi prevail, a return to autocracy may be inevitable.

“I fear that if Pahlavi becomes the ‘transition leader,’ Iran will never have truly free elections,” Nader said. “His ‘transition plan’ is antidemocratic. How can someone who wants to be the shah also serve as a transition leader?”

“It’s a conflict of interest. I believe Pahlavi and his group intend to use his role as transition leader as an attempt to seize power, with Pahlavi playing more of a figurehead role.”

## The Islamic Republic Fights On

The most immediate obstacle to any plots to seize power in Iran remains the Islamic Republic’s persistence, even in the face of its most dire existential threat to date.

With the swift establishment of an interim Leadership Council headed by newly named Ayatollah Alireza Arafi alongside President Masoud Pezeshkian and Chief Justice Gholam Hossein Mohseni Ejehe, Iran is fighting fiercely to preserve command and control, while continuing to unleash missile and drone strikes against Israel and U.S. bases across the region. Trump has alleged that senior officials, including members of the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps, or IRGC, are seeking to strike a deal with Washington, though no top figures have yet publicly defected.

The organized nature of the Iranian reaction to the decapitation of its top leader, along with scores of senior IRGC commanders, has thus far contrasted with the ongoing infighting among Iran’s leading opposition factions.

“The Iranian opposition lacks effective leadership, organizational capacity, financial resources and a unifying vision for a post–Islamic Republic Iran,” Ali Alfoneh, senior fellow at the Arab Gulf States Institute, told Newsweek. “Aspiring leaders often expend more energy fighting one another than confronting the regime.”

“This dynamic is evident in the recent dispute between Mr. Reza Pahlavi, the exiled former crown prince of Iran, and both the Mojahedin-e Khalq Organization and political parties representing Iran’s Kurds,” Alfoneh said. “Kurdish groups, in particular, have been angered by what they perceive as Mr. Pahlavi’s indirect threat to deploy the Iranian army to suppress separatism once the regime is overthrown.”

Meanwhile, Alfoneh argued that Pahlavi “has also failed to establish meaningful rapport with the Islamic Republic’s loyal opposition—the reformists—whom monarchists frequently attack more harshly than they do hardliners.”

The position of Iran’s reformist camp, which includes Pezeshkian, may prove decisive in shaping the short-term future of the nation. Yet little evidence exists of a rapprochement between this group and Pahlavi, who continues to portray himself as the primary vanguard of a “Lion and Sun Revolution” that has yet to manifest on the ground. “While such tensions are not unusual in exile politics,” Alfoneh said, “they weaken the opposition overall and give the regime opportunities to portray it as divided and politically immature.”

And despite Pahlavi’s public image, Arash Azizi, research associate and lecturer at Yale University, argued that he “lacks any serious organization even outside Iran let alone groundwork inside Iran.”

“Politically, he has been very divisive, with his camp focusing much of its energy on attacking others in the Iranian opposition,” Azizi told Newsweek. “It is true that they’ve successfully cultivated much support amongst Iranians, but it’s never been clear how they think they can translate this support to political fruits. His theory of change seemed to rely on [the] U.S. and Israel attacking Iran, but what comes next?”

“He says he will soon call on Iranians to come out and ‘finish the job’ but it’s not clear how he thinks disorganized masses in Iran can overcome the regime,” Azizi said. “Perhaps he hopes for further destruction of the country and its leadership and this creating a vacuum that could be filled by him. But even then, I don’t see it. I think there is very little chance that could find a path to power in Iran.”

Azizi argued only the MeK and some Kurdish factions possess “any degree of organization” across the Iranian opposition landscape, though neither of them “has the kind of seriousness that could make them a credible alternative to the regime.”

And among the remnants of Iran’s government stand several actors with an interest in consolidating control, though Azizi said such moves would likely manifest through political maneuvering within the system rather than through a seizure of power with foreign aid.

“Inside the Iranian regime, there are many willing figures,” Azizi said, “but instead of a coup, they’ll likely find a constitutional or more or less orderly path to power.”

Mostafa Najafi, a Tehran-based security analyst, also cast skepticism on the opposition’s viability to seize the moment.

“Figures such as Reza Pahlavi and Maryam Rajavi have for years sought to present themselves as viable political alternatives,” he told Newsweek. “However, the...reality inside Iran suggests that neither commands an organized or substantial social base.... Their political visibility is amplified largely in external media environments, rather than reflected in domestic mobilization capacity.”

“Similarly, coalitions or factions that attempt to elevate their profile during periods of unrest do not necessarily represent meaningful social leverage inside Iran,” Najafi added. “There exists a broad consensus when it comes to opposition to foreign intervention and the defense of national sovereignty. Political actors perceived as aligned with or dependent upon foreign powers face significant obstacles in establishing domestic legitimacy.”

As for whether insiders may seek to strike a deal with Trump, he argued that “historical experience suggests that on matters of sovereignty and national security, there is a strong institutional consensus...against decision-making under external pressure.”

“Any diplomatic engagement—if and when it occurs—would be shaped by long-term strategic calculations, not by short-term crisis bargaining,” Najafi said.
