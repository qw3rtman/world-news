# Strait of Hormuz crisis sends oil price close to $120

## The Middle East conflict pushed Brent crude to $119.50 a barrel for the first time since 2022 but prices fell back on reports that the G7 will intervene

The widening conflict across the Middle East has sent the price of Brent crude close to $120 a barrel for the first time since 2022.

Brent crude oil rose 29 per cent to $119.50 a barrel in overnight trading before easing back to $107 a barrel on reports that the Group of Seven (G7) finance ministers will on Monday discuss a joint release of oil from emergency reserves.

Sharp falls in Asian stock markets also eased, and the dollar strengthened a basket of currencies as investors expected higher energy prices to stoke inflation.

Analysts at Goldman Sachs, the American investment bank, said the price could exceed the peak of $146 set in 2008 if the flow of tankers through the Strait of Hormuz did not start to recover before the end of the month.

The price of Brent crude, the global oil benchmark, rose 28 per cent last week to break through $90 a barrel after Qatar, the United Arab Emirates, Kuwait and Iraq lowered production and the narrow strait, a waterway that usually carries about a fifth of the world’s oil exports, was effectively closed.

JP Morgan, another US banking company, has calculated a potential hit of up to 1.2 percentage points on global growth if oil and gas prices remain at a much higher level than expected this year.

They added that, in a scenario where oil prices settled at $120 per barrel, global inflation would rise by more than 1 per cent.

Other developments:

- Israel struck an oil refinery in Tehran, as it targeted Iran’s energy infrastructure.
- Shares of Saudi Aramco, the world’s largest oil producer, rose by 4.1 per cent in Riyadh.
- Oil futures contracts traded in China rose to $109 a barrel.
- Kuwait Petroleum declared force majeure after the state-owned QatarEnergy did so to liquefied natural gas buyers last week.
- Kuwait and the United Arab Emirates reduced oil production as land storage ran down.

JP Morgan said: “The near-term scenario is a near-term spike towards $120 per barrel, followed by moderation as the conflict soon subsides. But absent a clear and decisive political resolution, Brent crude oil prices are expected to settle at an elevated $80 per barrel through midyear.”

Hundreds of tankers attempting to traverse the 104-mile-long strait have come to a halt after Iran’s Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps threatened to “set ablaze” any vessel using the route.

Only nine commercial ships passed through the Strait of Hormuz last week, data from the tracking organisation Marine Traffic showed, compared with an average of 50 a day before the US-Israeli strikes on Iran. President Trump had said that the US could provide insurance guarantees and naval escorts to ensure safe passage for shipping, but details of this proposal have yet to emerge.

Iraqi oil production from its main southern oilfields has fallen by 70 per cent to 1.3 million barrels per day, according to industry sources. Daily production from the fields stood at 4.3 million before the war.

“Crude storage has reached maximum capacity,” an official with the state-run Basra Oil Company told Reuters, with no new tankers able to reach Iraq’s southern terminals.

“This is the most serious operational threat Iraq has faced in more than 20 years,” a senior Iraqi oil ministry official said.

Goldman said its analysis of the drivers of the decline of shipping “doesn’t suggest a quick solution is necessarily imminent”, adding: “Our conversations highlight that most shippers are in a wait-and-see mode while physical risks in the strait are high.”

It said its analysis of trade flows through the Persian Gulf last week suggested the impact was 17 times greater than the hit to Russian production after the 2022 full-scale invasion of Ukraine, which lifted the oil price to just shy of $128 a barrel.

“Based on these new data, developments and the size of the shock, we now think that oil prices would likely exceed $100 next week if no signs of solutions emerge by then,” Goldman said in a note published on Friday night.

“We now also think it’s likely that oil prices, especially for refined products, would exceed the 2008 and 2022 peaks if Strait of Hormuz flows were to remain depressed throughout March.”

The price of Brent has risen by more than 50 per cent this year, having begun 2026 at just over $61 a barrel.

Paul Krugman, the economist, wrote: “The disruption of world oil supplies caused by the war in Iran looks extremely serious. Indeed, if the Strait of Hormuz remains closed for an extended period, this will be a worse disruption than either the aftermath of the [1973] Yom Kippur war or the aftermath of the 1979 Iranian revolution.”

Traders have drastically scaled back their bets on central banks lowering interest rates this year due to the looming energy shock. Markets now think it is more likely than not that the European Central Bank will increase borrowing costs this year.

Sanjay Raja, chief UK economist at Deutsche Bank, said that if the energy price surge persisted the Bank of England would cut rates just once this year, in the final quarter, to 3.5 per cent and leave them there for the foreseeable future, adding that inflation could reach 3.8 per cent. Rachel Reeves may be forced into cuts to fuel duty if such a scenario played out, he added.
