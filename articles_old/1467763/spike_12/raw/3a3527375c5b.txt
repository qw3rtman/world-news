Crude oil briefly tops $111 on Middle East risks, highest since July 2022 - The Mainichi

NEW YORK (Kyodo) -- Crude oil futures briefly surpassed $111 per barrel in New York on Sunday, hitting their highest level since July 2022, amid concern that the conflict in the Middle East will disrupt supplies.

The launch of U.S. and Israeli attacks on Iran on Feb. 28 and concern that the conflict will spread across the oil-producing Middle East have put markets on edge.

The benchmark West Texas Intermediate futures contract ended below the $100 mark on Friday.

News that Mojtaba Khamenei had been named the next Iranian supreme leader following the killing of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei also sent crude oil prices higher, amid fears the conflict will be prolonged if he maintains his father's uncompromising stance.

Oil prices also jumped after Bloomberg reported Sunday that U.S. President Donald Trump is weighing the option of deploying special forces on the ground to seize Iran's near-bomb-grade uranium.

Higher oil prices, if they persist, would cast a pall over the global economy, hitting countries that are heavily reliant on energy imports such as Japan, which gets most of its oil from the Middle East.

About 20 percent of global oil passes through the Strait of Hormuz, a narrow channel between the Persian Gulf and the Gulf of Oman that is effectively closed to shipping.
