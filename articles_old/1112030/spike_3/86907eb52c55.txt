# Ayatollah Ali Khamenei's death may not lead to regime change in Iran

- Topic:Unrest, Conflict and War
- By Allyson Horn

Sunday 1 MarchSun 1 Mar 2026 at 4:14am

The architecture of the Islamic regime in Iran was built to outlast any single man. (Reuters: Toby Melville)

Ayatollah Ali Khamenei had long been an assassination target of Israel and the United States.

But his downfall doesn't necessarily mean a new Iran, free of tyrannical rule, is around the corner.

In fact, his death leaves a power vacuum in a perilously unstable country.

And it could end with a military takeover and a new leader with a no-limits approach to war.

## Ayatollah Khamenei's succession plan

In recent months, it was reported the supreme leader had prepared multiple scenarios for a line of succession to ultimately protect the Islamic regime that he'd spent decades safeguarding.

Khamenei had ruled with an iron fist since 1989 and wielded sweeping, oppressive powers, guarding the endurance of the Islamic regime.

Over decades, Khamenei mercilessly resisted attempts for regime change, often crushing dissent and ordering the killing of civilians who tried to challenge his rule.

But as threats to his own life increased, it was reported he had developed a multi-layered succession plan comprising personally appointed military and political figures who would make decisions, and ultimately rule, should he be killed.

It is unknown how many of them are still alive. If US administration reports are correct, the designated replacements are likely dead.

Which leaves one glaring question: Who is next and what does that mean for a future Iran?

## A military leader may be even more dangerous

Middle East analysts have long theorised the downfall of Khamenei could take on three possible directions: regime continuance, regime change, or military takeover. These are not necessarily mutually exclusive.

Under normal circumstances after the death of the leader, Iran's group of religious experts and leaders, called the Assembly of Experts, convenes to select a new leader from the clerical establishment.

But with the country's logistical operations in chaos and its leaders facing a literal fight for survival, convention will likely be disposed of.

At least in the short term, it's more plausible that a military leader could take control to direct Iran's armed resistance to the US and Israel and lead a crackdown on any public dissent that could further threaten the regime.

The world is now facing the likely scenario that no-one is left inside Iran to tell its military to stop — or put limits on what it will do.

America's intelligence sources had concluded before the attack that the top spot would likely be filled by hardline figures from the country's military, the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC).

The elevation of a leader from the IRGC, and a military-like takeover of leadership in Iran, could result in an even more dangerous military battle unlike anything ever seen before in the Middle East.

The IRGC is an armed force specifically charged with defending Iran's regime. It is composed of ground, air and naval forces, internal security militias and other paramilitary groups, with nearly 1 million personnel under its command in some capacity.

These forces are designed to retaliate against the United States and Israel, and they may continue to do just that — even without the ayatollah.

It's likely lower-ranking officials will now be either delegated, or take it upon themselves, to make attack decisions across the region.

## Regime change is not a given

After killing the supreme leader, US President Donald Trump said many of Iran's security forces were ready to lay down their arms.

"We are hearing that many of their IRGC, military, and other security and police forces, no longer want to fight, and are looking for Immunity from us," he said.

The reality of that bold statement is hard to imagine, given the depth and breadth of the security force apparatus in Iran, and a current environment where religious motivations may be compounded by a desire for revenge and a fight for Iran's sovereignty.

Khamenei's death brings about only the second leadership change in Iran since the establishment of the regime nearly 50 years ago.

Trump has used the moment to call on the people of Iran to rise up and affect regime change.

"This is the single greatest chance for the Iranian people to take back their Country," he wrote on social media.

But the very architecture of the Islamic regime in Iran was built to outlast any single man.

And the reality of achieving a political movement, wide enough and strong enough to affect that change, could take significant time.

Any uprising on the streets would likely face the deadly wrath of paramilitary forces, which would take it upon themselves to prevent the regime's potential downfall with lethal force.

And with no opposing military forces inside the country, the political will of anti-regime movements may not be enough to endure against merciless killing.

Iran's future sits on a precipice. What happens next is anyone's guess.

But previous limits that might have applied to battle and war are likely now gone.
