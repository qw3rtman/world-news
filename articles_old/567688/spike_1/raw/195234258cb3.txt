# The Binyamin Netanyahu I knew: ending Iran’s regime is his life’s work

## Never bet against Bibi, says a former British ambassador who saw the Israeli prime minister at his charming best and scheming worst

Binyamin Netanyahu’s politics are shaped by three powerful men in his family, Matthew Gould writes

Matthew Gould

Saturday March 14 2026, 11.00pm, The Sunday Times

It is unlikely the United States would be bombing Iran today were it not for the Israeli prime minister, Binyamin Netanyahu. To understand the war, we need to understand him — how he thinks, how he operates and how he sees the world.

For Netanyahu, this war is the culmination of a lifetime fixation on Iran. It has been at the centre of his worldview for as long as he has been in politics. He was warning about the threat from Iran’s nuclear programme when I was still in school in the 1980s. He believed then that the threat from Iran’s nuclear programme was existential for Israel; for all his other compromises and pivots, he has never wavered on this point.

I was British ambassador to Israel from 2010 to 2015. I spent much of those years trying to work out if Netanyahu was serious about his threats to bomb Iran, where I had been stationed as acting ambassador a few years before. I remember early on in my tenure feeling I had understood the place Iran occupied in Netanyahu’s head when he was advised to meet a particular Iran expert. He tetchily replied: “But I am an Iran expert.”

Netanyahu sees the Iranian regime as Amalek — the Old Testament eternal enemy whose sole purpose was the destruction of the Jewish people. It was not always like this. Under the shah there was a quiet but deep alliance between the two countries, Iran providing most of Israel’s oil and Israel training Savak, the shah’s secret police.

Despite doing damage to Hamas and Hezbollah in recent years, Netanyahu still saw the primary threat remaining: Iran’s nuclear programme. He has never had time for deals that reward Iran for suspending enrichment, nor trusted airy talk about security guarantees. He started the job of dismantling the programme in June last year, when Israel launched airstrikes on facilities in Natanz and Isfahan. In a foretaste of the current conflict, after the first Israeli attacks the US joined in with the massive munitions only its forces could deploy.

For Netanyahu, the job was far from complete. Iran’s nuclear programme was not destroyed, its missile programme was largely untouched and the Israel-hating leadership still in place.

Netanyahu, known since childhood as Bibi, has dominated Israeli politics for three decades. Even for the brief period he spent out of power, the defining poles of Israeli politics were pro-Bibi and anti-Bibi.

The young Bibi, right, with a friend outside his childhood home in Jerusalem

GPO/GETTY IMAGES

In 1996, aged 46, he became Israel’s youngest prime minister and the first born after the creation of the Israeli state. That made him a sabra, the term for those born in the State of Israel. A sabra is a prickly pear — tough on the outside, soft on the inside. In Bibi’s case, only the first part of that is true.

In 2019 he became Israel’s longest-serving prime minister, passing the record of David Ben-Gurion, Israel’s first leader. The same year, he broke a record of a different sort, when he became the first Israeli prime minister to face criminal charges in office — of bribery, fraud and breach of trust, charges he still faces (and denies) today.

His politics have been shaped by his relationships with three powerful men in his family, spanning three generations — his father Benzion, his brother Yoni and his son Yair. Benzion Netanyahu was one of the leading historians of the Spanish Inquisition. When he died aged 102 in 2012, I went to his house to sit shiva with Bibi, the Jewish tradition of open-house mourning being too good a networking opportunity to miss.

## His father’s influence

As we talked about his father, Bibi told me to read the first 50 pages of his father’s book The Origins of the Inquisition in Fifteenth Century Spain. Those 50 pages were a stunning account of the origins of antisemitism, going back to 525BC and the Persian conquest of Egypt. They described how the Jews in Egypt aligned themselves with the Persian conquerors for protection, causing the Egyptians to despise them as treacherous collaborators with the enemy.

Netanyahu with his father, Benzion, weeks before he died in 2012

AVI OHAYON/GPO/GETTY IMAGES

This ancient history had a powerful impact on Bibi’s worldview. He took from it that hatred of Jews was eternal, originally secular and political, an inevitable reaction to being an exiled minority which, he believed, can never be solved through assimilation or conversion. It was this logic that drove his father’s Zionism, his attachment to the lands of “Greater Israel” and his fierce opposition to territorial compromise. Benzion shaped Bibi’s deep respect for America and, in particular, a belief that Congress is the seat of real power. It was this attitude that caused Bibi to go to Congress in 2015 to lobby against the agreement with Iran that President Obama was then negotiating, an extraordinary and highly partisan move that showed remarkable contempt for the incumbent of the White House at the time.

## His brother’s shadow

Bibi’s older brother also casts a long shadow over his political career. The handsome, charismatic Yoni was the commander of the elite Sayeret Matkal unit, famed for operating behind enemy lines. He was the only Israeli soldier killed during the hostage rescue at Entebbe in 1976, and became an instant national hero. The operation remains celebrated in Israel for its heroism, daring, creativity and courage.

Yoni’s death caused Bibi — also a veteran of Sayeret Matkal — to abandon a business career in America with Boston Consulting and return to Israel. It gave Bibi unassailable security credentials but also turned his beloved brother into a ghost whose achievements were impossible to live up to.

A picture of Yoni Netanyahu looms over his brother in 2019

AFP

When David Cameron came to Israel in 2014, I wrestled with what the British prime minister should bring with him as a gift. A colleague suggested we dig up the diplomatic telegram from our high commission in Kampala reporting the Entebbe raid. It was a masterstroke. The telegram described the operation as “an extraordinary feat of arms”. When Cameron handed over a copy of the report in Bibi’s office, I saw for a moment this consummate political actor’s most genuine emotion.

## His son’s extremity

In the next generation, Bibi’s son Yair has become an important adviser for his father. Yair is a controversial figure in Israel. Unlike his father and uncle, he served in the Israel Defence Forces spokesperson’s unit rather than a combat brigade. He lives in Miami and has pushed his father to be even more populist and combative. Yair’s own social media voice is extreme. He has called the state prosecutors who charged his father traitors. He has expressed the hope that all Muslims leave Israel (and got suspended from Facebook in 2018 for doing so).

Yair’s growing influence has coincided with Bibi moving further and further to the right, becoming more explicitly anti-Arab in his politics and bringing into his coalition parties and politicians who would have been unthinkable partners ten years ago, such as Itamar Ben-Gvir, the national security minister, and Bezalel Smotrich, the finance minister. Both are banned from entering the UK for promoting settler violence.

Sara Netanyahu, the prime minister’s wife, with their sons Yair and Avrnir at the swearing-in of the hard-right coalition in 2022

AMIR COHEN

Bibi is like Donald Trump in some respects — a populist making his country more divided with crude fearmongering, including against Israel’s own Arab citizens; a huge character who sucks the oxygen from the entire political scene.

In other respects, he is nothing like Trump. He is erudite, well read, intellectual and eloquent, with his perfect English and low, sonorous voice. When he wants to turn on the charm — I have seen him do it — he can be funny and excellent company.

He is also an outstanding political tactician. My rule No 1 of Israeli politics is this: never bet against Bibi. He has repeatedly come back from apparent political death, always finding a way to keep hold of power. His signature move is to persuade his political opponents to join his coalition through rarely fulfilled promises of power-sharing, only to spit them out later, diminished and bitter.

Bibi is unsentimental to an extraordinary degree, one of the most calculating politicians I have ever met. Soon after I arrived in Israel in 2010, the Israeli newspaper Yedioth Ahronoth published a leaked internal document from Bibi’s 2007 primary campaign. It became known as the “List of Millionaires” and was covered with Bibi’s handwritten notes about potential donors and what he could get out of each. Trump was on the list as a “category four” — a low chance of getting money.

Netanyahu with President Trump in Washington in 2020 after signing the Abraham Accords

TOM BRENNER/REUTERS

One evening, my wife and I took a British pop star to dinner at the prime minister’s residence. We had a wonderful evening with Bibi and Sara, his wife — we were treated to an impromptu concert from the pop star using Yair’s guitar, which only finished when his manager pointed out that it was 1am and he had a concert the next day. I was sure this would have won me access and influence. I got my office to call the following week to ask if Bibi would discuss Iran. Almost immediately the answer came back: no.

## Stained legacy

Bibi will have decided to attack Iran because he believed it necessary, but he will have also had a close eye on the upcoming elections in October. While believing his decisions are for the good of Israel, it is striking how often they coincide with his own political advantage — understandable for a leader who equates his own survival in power with the survival of his people. It is not simply an expression of personal ambition but, for Bibi, a holy mission.

Bibi and Sara feel keenly that they have made enormous, unappreciated sacrifices for the Israeli people. He will feel vindicated by the current war and the fact that he may finally be able to take out the Iranian threat in a definitive way. This will be the legacy he wants.

Unfortunately for Israel, it will not be his only legacy. For many Israelis, the October 7 attacks will remain an indelible, even defining, stain on his record. Bibi will also leave an Israel more divided and less at peace with itself, let alone with its Palestinian neighbours, its democratic institutions weakened and the fabric of its society torn. He will leave the bipartisan consensus on Israel in Congress fractured and Israel less popular with younger generations around the world than ever before.

And, just possibly, he will be remembered as much for becoming the first Israeli prime minister to be convicted in office as for getting rid of the Ayatollah. He might find himself following in the footsteps of his hero, Winston Churchill — saving his country and then being turfed out. Though, knowing Bibi, I wouldn’t bet on it.

Matthew Gould was deputy and acting British ambassador to Iran between 2003 and 2005 then British ambassador to Israel between 2010 and 2015
