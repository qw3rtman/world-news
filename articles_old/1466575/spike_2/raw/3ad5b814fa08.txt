## Asian markets stumble as US, Israel strikes on Iran rattle investors

Stocks across Asia tumbled on Monday after U.S. and Israeli strikes on Iran stoked fears of a wider conflict in the Middle East. Nervous investors rushed to safer bets like the U.S. dollar, gold, and government bonds.

An index tracking emerging Asian markets dropped 1.1%, its worst day in nearly a month. Stocks in Southeast Asia slid even further — a regional gauge fell 1.5%, hitting a two-week low.

Oil prices spiked roughly 7% over the weekend after Tehran retaliated with missile attacks across the region, sending energy markets into a frenzy. Trump added to market jitters, telling reporters the conflict could continue for another four weeks.

Singapore’s stock market sank as much as 2.4%, its sharpest intraday drop since last April. Air carrier Singapore Airlines plunged 7.5%, its worst day since mid-2025, as war-related airspace shutdowns in Dubai, Abu Dhabi, and Doha continued for a third day, snarling global air travel.

Elsewhere, Australian airline Qantas fell more than 10%, and Hong Kong’s Cathay Pacific shed 7%. Malaysia’s benchmark index and Indonesia’s market each lost around 2%, while Taiwan and the Philippines were also hit hard − both down more than 2%.

Indonesia's central bank said it would step in to steady markets if needed as its currency, the rupiah, slipped 0.5% to about 16,825 per U.S. dollar. Other currencies across the region — including those in Singapore, Malaysia, and the Philippines — also weakened as the dollar surged to its strongest level in weeks.

## Oil prices expected to stay high for days

Analysts expect oil prices to remain elevated over the coming days with conflict escalating in the Middle East, as they assess the impact to supplies, especially flows through the Strait of Hormuz, a conduit for more than 20% of global oil.

Crude futures surged more than 8% on Monday to multi-month highs in the first trading after the U.S. and Israel launched attacks on Iran and killed its Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, with Tehran striking back against Israel and at least seven other countries.

Attacks have damaged tankers, and many ship owners, oil majors and trading houses suspended crude oil, fuel and liquefied natural gas shipments via the Strait of Hormuz.

- Wood Mackenzie said oil prices could potentially exceed $100 per barrel if tanker flows through the Strait aren't quickly restored, with analysts adding the disruption creates a "dual supply shock."
- Goldman Sachs estimates an $18 per barrel real-time risk premium in crude prices, the bank said in a note on Sunday. It expects this estimated impact to moderate to a $4 premium if only 50% of flows through the Strait of Hormuz are halted for a month.
- Citi analysts see Brent trading between $80 and $90 per barrel over at least the coming week in their base case, they said in a note. The bank expects prices to pull back to $70 a barrel on de-escalation.

## Strikes in Lebanon

BrieAnna J. Frank

## What is the Strait of Hormuz?

Kathryn Palmer

The Strait of Hormuz is an energy transportation route critical to global trade, accounting for about 25% of seaborne oil shipments and about 20% of liquified natural gas flows.

The narrow channel, whittling down to just 21 miles across at one point, connects the Persian Gulf to the Gulf of Oman and the wider Arabian Sea. The waterway's choke point is sandwiched by the Iranian coastline to one side and a small Omani peninsula on the other. It is a vital route for exporting oil from major producers like Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Iran, Qatar and the UAE.

The U.S. and Israeli strikes on Iran have already led to disruptions. Tehran has warned ships against moving through the waterway.

On Sunday, at least 150 tankers, including oil and natural gas vessels, dropped anchor in open Gulf waters beyond the Strait of Hormuz and dozens more were stationary on the other side, Reuters reported.
