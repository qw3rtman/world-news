# Kharg Island could be Iran's weak spot as Washington reportedly considers ground invasion options

- Topic:War
- By Emily Clark

Wednesday 11 MarchWed 11 Mar 2026 at 6:54pm

At times, 90 per cent of Iran's oil exports move through the terminal at Kharg Island. (Reuters: Planet Labs PBC)

A small island off the coast of Iran's mainland could unlock the war for the regime's adversaries, but analysts say damaging strikes by the US or Israel could put the global oil market on a perilous path.

After Israel bombed Iranian oil and fuel storage facilities over the weekend, Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu promised "many surprises" were in store for the next phase of the war.

Videos of the facilities in the wake of the strikes showed billowing flames and thick smoke blanketing Tehran before reports of oil-laden rain across the city followed.

After more than a week of war focused on the regime's leadership, security services and arsenal, these weekend attacks appeared to be the first strikes targeting Iran's energy infrastructure.

There are plenty of similar targets in mainland Iran, but one small island off the coast makes the regime's oil industry and the cash pipeline it provides particularly vulnerable.

Kharg Island is a coral outcrop that sits about 25 kilometres off the coast of Iran in the Persian Gulf.

It's small, but importantly, its long jetties provide supertankers access to deep water, unlike mainland Iran's sloping shoreline.

Kharg Island has a long history of trade, but became a crude oil terminal in the middle of the 20th century. (ABC News: Kylie Silvester)

The island has a long history of trade. In medieval times, Kharg Island saw pearls and produce pass through its ports, but by the mid-2020s, the terminal could load 10 supertankers at once, sending crude oil, fertilisers, liquid gas and other products across the world.

The island is a vital connection between Iran's most valuable exports and its customers, with up to 90 per cent of the regime's oil trade passing through the Kharg Island terminal.

From Iran's largest producing fields, including Ahvaz, Marun and Gachsaran, the oil travels to Kharg Island via pipeline before being loaded onto tankers that then pass through the Strait of Hormuz and onto the Arabian Sea.

What it brings the regime in return is immense wealth, funding the salaries of its bureaucrats and soldiers.

There is some reporting that Washington and Israel are considering moving on the island — something that could choke the flow of cash to the regime.

It would be an action with a very strong reaction and could have long-term consequences for global resource markets.

"A direct strike would immediately halt the bulk of Iran's crude exports, likely triggering severe retaliation in the Strait of Hormuz or against regional energy infrastructure," ⁠according to analysis from JP Morgan.

Kharg Island is of immense strategic significance, and what happens to it next could change the course of the war.

## Strike, seize or leave the island

Kharg Island is a critical piece of infrastructure for Iran's oil industry. (Getty: Morteza Nikoubazl/NurPhoto)

US president Donald Trump has refused to rule out American boots on the ground in Iran, although he recently said he was a long way from giving such an order.

There are some places in Iran where a limited ground presence might have a significant impact — most notably in a mission to remove uranium stockpiles, and on Kharg Island.

Some reports in US media, including Axios, suggest that Trump administration officials have been discussing the seizure of Kharg Island.

"Trump has not ruled out some sort of limited ground intervention or invasion and if they, for example, seize this particular little island … Iranian exports come to a halt," said associate professor of international relations at the University of Sussex, Kamran Matin.

"There will be basically no income whatsoever for the regime for the foreseeable future.

"But then that would also mean that in order to protect that little island, which is very close to the mainland, they have to also occupy some more land in the mainland, and mission creep will just expand and expand.

"So it's a very momentous decision to make for Trump in terms of ground deployment, but it's not … inconceivable, so that would probably change the course of the war."

Former US special envoy to Ukraine and White House advisor Keith Kellogg has been speaking to the media about a possible American ground operation in Iran.

He has been explaining that the nation is so large that strategic targets like Kharg Island present a real opportunity.

"The country is so darn large that they [troops on the ground] really can't make any impact. They're not going to march to Tehran," he told Fox News.

> "If you take out that island … you basically shut them off economically, they cannot support China, they cannot support Russia as well."

China buys more than 80 per cent of Iran's shipped oil, according to Reuters, and before the US swooped on Nicolás Maduro it was Venezuela's biggest oil customer too.

Since the middle of the last century, oil has been pumped from Iran's oil fields to Kharg Island via pipelines. (AP: Horst Faas)

With Iran under Western sanctions, Beijing-backed firms picked up a good deal on oil shipped out of Kharg Island, so any move on its terminal by the US or Israel would leave China particularly exposed.

Kharg Island did sustain Israeli strikes in the first week of the war, although the damage was not clear, according to Reuters.

Former Israeli prime minister Yair Lapid also backed a move on Kharg Island, saying: "Israel must destroy all of Iran's oil fields and energy industry on Kharg Island; that is what will cripple Iran's economy and topple the regime."

The decision over Kharg Island appears to be strike, seize or leave, with Israel showing much more willingness to hit Iranian oil and fuel facilities than the US.

After the weekend's strikes that sent oil-laden rain over Tehran the White House reportedly asked Israel not to carry out further strikes on Iranian energy infrastructure, in part for fear of retaliation on similar facilities across the Gulf.

Former Pentagon official and senior fellow at the American Enterprise Institute Michael Rubin wrote about the Kharg option in January, saying seizing the island would be "the ultimate checkmate to Iran".

He wrote that Kharg Island as a military target was "tailor-made for Trump."

"After all, Trump always says he will take adversaries' oil to benefit American companies and local allies," Mr Rubin said.

"Should he take Kharg, rather than destroy it, he can not only ensure the regime can never again pay the salaries of its bureaucrats and soldiers, but also that, in the future after regime change, he can ensure that the new Iranian regime can finance its own rebuilding."

Dr Matin said capturing Kharg Island militarily was "perfectly possible".

"It's a very small place, and obviously [the] Iranian Navy has been completely destroyed, which might have been actually done precisely with the intention of [the] subsequent seizure of some of these islands, especially Kharg," he said.

In a recent Truth Social post, Mr Trump warned that the US could pick off what he described as Iran's easy targets.

"If Iran does anything that stops the flow of oil within the Strait of Hormuz, they will be hit by the United States of America TWENTY TIMES HARDER than they have been hit thus far," he said in the post.

"Additionally, we will take out easily destroyable targets that will make it virtually impossible for Iran to ever be built back, as a Nation, again."

## 'Single most important piece of energy infrastructure'

Kharg Island is one of the few islands in the Persian Gulf that have natural freshwater supplies.

It has an ancient history of human activity that dates back hundreds of years BC.

It has been a trading station for the Dutch East India Company. And it was developed into an oil terminal under a joint venture with US company Amoco in the 1960s under the shah.

Kharg Island has an history of human activity that dates back hundreds of years BC. (AP: Horst Faas)

As Iran got richer during the oil boom in the middle of the last century, Kharg Island emerged as a critical piece of real estate.

Now, as a Barron's analyst described it, Kharg Island is "the single most important piece of energy infrastructure Iran possesses".

The US has considered moving on the island in the past.

Declassified US national security records revealed the administration of then-president Jimmy Carter was considering an attack on Kharg Island during the Iran hostage crisis in 1979.

Those records also included the assessment made by a national security advisor at the time that the result of a US move on Kharg Island would punish friends of Washington seeking oil, and that: "Going after Kharg Island would create a worldwide oil crisis."

Kharg Island has been in operation since the 1960s, when it was developed, in part, by US oil company Amoco. (Getty: KOCH/Gamma-Rapho)

Jimmy Carter backed away from moving on Kharg Island.

Ronald Reagan, during the 1980s Iran-Iraq tanker war, prioritised protecting shipping and targeting Iranian vessels and missile batteries, also leaving Kharg Island untouched.

"Although Iraqi forces struck some ‌terminals ⁠and tankers during the eight-year war, Kharg remained largely operational," according to JP Morgan analysis.

"Damage was typically repaired quickly, demonstrating that disabling it would require sustained, large-scale attacks."
